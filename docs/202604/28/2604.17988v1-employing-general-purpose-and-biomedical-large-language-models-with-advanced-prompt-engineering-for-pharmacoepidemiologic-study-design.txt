Abstract 

Background: The potential of large language models (LLMs) to automate and support 

pharmacoepidemiologic study design is an emerging area of interest, yet their reliability remains 

insufficiently characterized . General -purpose LLMs often display inaccuracies, while the 

comparative performance of specialized biomedical LLMs in this domain remains unknown. 

Methods: This study evaluated general -purpose LLMs (GPT -4o and DeepSeek -R1) versus 

biomedically fine -tuned LLMs (QuantFactory/ Bio -Medical -Llama -3-8B -GGUF and 

Irathernotsay/qwen2 -1.5B -medical_qa -Finetune ) using 46 protocols (2018 -2024) from the HMA -

EMA Catalogue and Sentinel System . Performance was assessed across relevance, logic of 

justification, and ontology -code agreement across multiple coding systems using Least -to -Most 

(LTM) and Active Prompting strategies .

Results: GPT -4o and DeepSeek -R1 paired with LTM prompting achieved the highest relevance 

and logic of justification scores, with GPT -4o -LTM reaching a median relevance score of 4 in 8 

of 9 questions for HMA -EMA protocols. Biomedical LLMs showed lower relevance over all and 

frequently generated insufficient justification. All LLMs demonstrated limited proficiency in 

ontology -code mapping, although LTM provided the most consistent improvements in reasoning 

stability. 

Conclusion: Off -the -shelf general -purpose LLMs currently offer superior support for 

pharmacoepidemiologic design compared to biomedical LLMs . Prompt strategy strongly 

influenced LLM performance. 

Keywords: pharmacoepidemiology; artificial intelligence; large language model; real -world 

evidence .3

1. Introduction 

The growing need for rapid evidence generation in pharmacoepidemiology places strong demands 

on study design and data quality. [1] This is particularly important for populations underrepresented 

in clinical trials and comparative effectiveness /safety research in routine care. [2,3] As artificial 

intelligence (AI) becomes increasingly integrated into biomedical research, large language models 

(LLMs) have been proposed as tools to support time - and resource -consuming tasks , including 

those relevant to pharmacoepidemiology. [4 –6] Interest in this area is growing within regulatory 

settings. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has explored the use of LLMs such as 

BERTox and GPT to support document analysis, literature review, and toxicity assessment. [5] 

The European Medicines Agency (EMA) has issued high -level recommendations on the use of 

LLMs in regulatory activities . [6] Despite this momentum, the role of LLMs in time - and 

consuming -tasks in pharmacoepidemiolog y, such as study design , remains unclear. 

Pharmacoepidemiologic study design requires accurate identification and formulation of multiple 

interrelated methodological elements, including temporal assessment windows, inclusion and 

exclusion criteria, exposure definition, and ontology -code mapping. [7 –9] Early attempts to use 

off -the -shelf LLMs for these tasks have highlighted limitations . [10 –12] However , it remains 

uncertain whether such limitations are mainly related to the type of model (i.e., g enera l-purpose) ,

prompt strategy, or both. This study therefore aimed to evaluate the performance of off -the -shelf 

general -purpose LLMs and biomedical LLMs, combined with prompt -engineering strategies, for 

supporting pharmacoepidemiologic study design. 

2. Methods 

2.1 Data Sources 

This study evaluated LLM performance using study protocols from two pharmacoepidemiologic 

sources (i.e., HMA -EMA Catalogue and Sentinel System) . From the HMA -EMA Catalogue of 

Real -World Data (RWD) Studies, all available DARWIN EU ® protocols were included at the time 

of analysis (n=16). In addition, 15 non -DARWIN expert -developed protocols were randomly 

sampled from the HMA -EMA Catalogue and were required to be registered between January 1 st ,4

2023, and February 22 nd , 2024, finalized, written in English, and publicly available. From the 

Sentinel System, 15 protocols were selected registered between January 1 st , 2022, and February 

22 nd , 2024, marked as complete, classified as “drug and outcome analysis” or “drug use,” and 

publicly available. [13,14] 

2.2 Pharmacoepidemiologic Design Concepts 

The evaluation framework was structured around key pharmacoepidemiologic design concepts 

derived from the HARPER checklist. [15] These concepts were operationalized as a sequence of 

concept -specific tasks to reflect the main elements typically required for protocol design in RWD 

studies. The concept -specific tasks were structured as follows: a) Question 1 assessed the correct 

identification of the study design; b) Question 2 assesse d the definition of the index date; c) 

Question 3 asse ssed inclusion and exclusion criteria; d) Question 4 assessed the inclusion and 

exclusion assessment window; e) Question 5 assessed exposure; f) Question 6 assessed the 

outcome; g) Question 7 assessed the follow -up period; h) Question 8 assessed covariates; i) 

Question 9 assessed the covariate assessment window; and then it was separately assessed 

ontology -code mapping. 

2. 3 Human Expert Gold Standard 

From each protocol, 2 human assessors (XZ and MS) extracted pharmacoepidemiologic design 

concept mention ed in section 2.2 , which served as the gold standard against which LLM outputs 

were evaluated. Specifically, t hey included study design, index date, inclusion and exclusion 

criteria, inclusion and exclusion assessment window, exposure, outcome, follow -up period, 

covariates, covariate assessment window, and ontology -code mapping. [15] LLM -generated 

outputs were com pared with protocol -derived expert answers across the 10 questions (Table 1 

provides the specific prompts used for each question ).

2. 4 Prioritization of Large Language Models and Prompt Strategies 5

Two general -purpose LLMs (GPT -4o and DeepSeek -R1) and four biomedical LLMs were 

included in the screening phase: QuantFactory/Bio -Medical -Llama -3-8B -GGUF, 

mradermacher/DeepSeek -r1 -Medical -Mini -GGUF, Plaban81/gemma -medical_qa -Finetune, and 

Ira thernotsay/qwen2 -1.5B -medical_qa -Finetune. [16 –19] Model selection for biomedical LLM 

was based on overall performance as reported by the Open LLM Leaderboard hosted on Hugging 

Face (https://huggingface.co/spaces/open -llm -leaderboard/open_llm_leaderboard#/ ) as of March 

2025 . LLMs were paired with seven prompt engineering strategies identified from the literature: 

1) basic prompt with term definitions, 2) synthetic prompting, 3) active -prompt, 4) plan -and -solve, 

5) least -to -most (LTM), 6) Tree -of -Thought, and 7) decomposition prompting. [20 –22] Together, 

this yielded 42 model -prompt combinations for the initial pre -assessment of t he most performing 

combinations . Prompt construction was guided by the CLEAR framework, which emphasizes 

concise, logical, explicit, adaptive, and reflective prompt design. [23] A pre -assessment was 

conducted on three randomly selected protocols, one from each protocol source . Model -prompt 

combinations were evaluated against the human expert gold standard using a 1 -5 Likert scale for 

relevance and logic of justification , a binary agreement measure for logic of justification, and error 

frequency as an additional criterion. Based on this screening, the five top -performing combinations 

were selected for the main analysis: GPT -4o with LTM, GPT -4o with Active Prompt, DeepSeek -

R1 with LTM, QuantFactory/Bio -Medical -Llama -3-8B -GGUF with LTM, and 

Ira thernotsay/qwen2 -1.5B -medical_qa -Finetune with LTM. Protocols, extracted data, and LLM -

generated output from the pre -assessment are available at:  https://github.com/madelsu/LLM -for -

pharmacoepi -study -design/tree/main/Data/pre_assessment .

2. 5 Study Outcome s

The primary outcome was relevance, defined as agreement between LLM -generated answers and 

protocol -derived expert answers measured with Likert -scale . Agreement was considered 

acceptable if scores were identical or differed by no more than one Likert level. Logic of 

justification was assessed as a key secondary outcome using a 5 -point scale , with 1 indicating 

illogical or inconsistent reasoning and 5 indicating coherent, well -supported, and methodologically 6

rigorous reasoning . Ontology -code agreement was assessed by comparing predicted and reference 

codes (i.e., from the protocols) for exposure and outcome across ontology groups and summarizing 

percentage agreement by model, prompt strategy, and protocol source. Error patterns were 

examined through qualitative review of predefined error types, and frequencies were summarized 

descrip tively. 

2. 6 Data Analysis and Software 

A descriptive analysis of the included protocols was performed by protocol source (DARWIN 

EU®, HMA -EMA, and Sentinel System ). Counts and proportions were calculated for publication 

year, geographical origin of authors, study type, database use, and ontology -code mappings used 

to define exposure and outcome. For relevance and logic of justification, Likert -scale results were 

summarized for each model -prompt combination using medians, interquartile ranges (IQRs), 

means, whisker ranges, and outlier counts. The se distributions were visualized using box plots. 

Comparisons were made across model -prompt combinations, by LLM architecture, by prompt 

strategy, and by protocol source. In the box plots, white dots represented mean values, black dots 

indicated statistica l outliers, and whiskers extended to the range within 1.5×IQR. Ontology -code 

agreement was calculated as percentage agreement between model -predicted and reference codes 

and was summarized by ontology -code group, model, prompt strategy, and protocol source .

Results were visualized using comparative bar plots. Error frequencies were tabulated and 

summarized descriptively. To explore error patterns across questions and biomedical LLMs ,

deviations from the mean occurrence of each error type per question were calculated and visualized 

using a heatmap. All statistical analyses and visualizations were performed in R version 4.5.1. 

Data handling and plotting were conducted using tidyverse version 2.0.0, box plots and bar charts 

were generated using ggplot2 version 3.5.1, and heatmaps were created using the heatmap package 

version 1.0.12. 

2. 7 Reporting Standards 7

This article was prepared in accordance with the TRIPOD -LLM and CHART reporting standards 

(Supplementary Tables 2 and 3). [24,25] . All statistical analysis code, study protocols, LLM 

prompt strategies, relevance and logic of justification assessments are publicly available via a 

GitHub repository (Link:  https://github.com/madelsu/LLM -for -pharmacoepi -study -design.git ). 

3. Results 

3.1 Descriptive Analysis 

A total of 46 pharmacoepidemiological protocols were included: 16 from DARWIN EU®, 15 from 

the HMA -EMA Catalogue, and 15 from the Sentinel System. The 4 6 protocols are available at 

https://github.com/madelsu/LLM -for -pharmacoepi -study -design/tree/main/Data . There was full 

agreement between the human experts when extracting information from the protocols. The 

protocols were published between 2018 and 2024, with annual frequencies of 1 from 2018, 2 from 

2019, 4 from 2020, 3 from 2021, 3 from 2022, 29 from 2023, and 4 from 2024. DARWIN EU® 

and HMA -EMA protocols involved researchers from Belgium, Estonia, Finland, France, Germany, 

Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, Romania, Spain, and the United Kingdom, whereas Sentinel 

protocols originated from the United States. Study topics were primarily drug utilization (n=18) 

and disease epidemiology (n=15), followed by saf ety (n=11) and effectiveness (n=2). Across the 

included protocols, 15 data sources were represented, of which IQVIA Germany (n=23), SIDIAP 

(n=15), and the Sentinel Distributed Database (n=14) were the most frequently used. Fifteen 

ontology -code systems were identified, with ICD, SNOMED, ATC, CPT, and RxNorm among the 

most frequently used for concept definition . Less frequently used systems included MedDRA and 

Read codes. 

3.2 Relevance 

Overall, GPT -4o -LTM and DeepSeek -R1 -LTM achieved the highest relevance scores. GPT -4o -

LTM showed the most stable performance, reaching a median score of 4 with a narrow IQR in 8 

of 9 questions for HMA -EMA protocols. Across evaluated concepts, general -purpo se LLMs 

outperformed the biomedical LLMs, with higher median relevance scores and greater overall 8

stability (Figure 1). LTM prompting also generally performed better than Active Prompting, 

particularly for study design and exposure -related tasks, where it produced higher scores and 

narrower score distributions (Figure 2). Protocol sources influenced model performance. HMA -

EMA protocols yielded the highest and most stable relevance scores, particularly for GPT -4o -

LTM and DeepSeek -R1 -LTM. Sentinel protocols also supported relatively strong performance but 

with greater variabi lity. In contrast, DARWIN EU® protocols were associated with lower and less 

stable relevance, especially for outcome and follow -up definitions. Across all protocol sources, 

biomedical LLMs showed lower relevance overall, with DARWIN EU® protocols yielding 

consistently low -scoring outpu ts (Figure 3). 

3.3 Logic of Justification 

The overall pattern for logic of justification largely mirrored the relevance results. GPT -4o -LTM 

and DeepSeek -R1 -LTM again achieved the strongest performance, with GPT -4o -LTM on HMA -

EMA protocols reaching the highest and most stable scores across question s. Non -biomedical 

LLMs generally produced more coherent and methodologically plausible justifications than 

biomedical LLMs, which were more frequently not assessable because of missing or inadequate 

reasoning (Figure 4). As in the relevance analysis, HMA -EMA protocols yielded the most stable 

performance, whereas Sentinel and DARWIN EU® protocols showed greater variability across 

concepts (Figure 5). LTM prompting generally outperformed Active Prompting, particularly for 

study design, exposure, and outcome definition tasks. However, LTM also produced occasional 

low -end outliers, suggesting that errors introduced in early steps could propagate across later 

dependent resp onses (Figure 6). 

3.4 Ontology -Code Agreement 

Ontology -code agreement was markedly lower than performance on the narrative design tasks. 

Across protocol sources, agreement was observed mainly for ATC and selected procedure -based 

coding systems, whereas performance was limited for ICD -based systems and absent o r negligible 

for RxNorm and SNOMED in several settings. The highest agreement was observed for ATC 9

coding in HMA -EMA protocols with DeepSeek -R1 -LTM (28.6%) and for HCPCS coding in 

Sentinel protocols with GPT -4o -LTM (22.2%). Overall, ontology -code mapping remained a weak 

area across all LLMs. 

3.5 Error Classification 

Error analysis showed that the biomedical LLMs were particularly prone to recurrent structured 

failures. The most frequent error types were irrelevant auto -generated questions (n=189) and lack 

of justification (n=105), followed by long answers (n=36) and n on -adherence to the intended 

algorithm (n=11). Less frequent errors included prompt echoing, model crash, and language 

inconsistency. Overall, QuantFactory/Bio -Medical -Llama -3-8B showed more concentrated error 

clusters across core questions, particularly Q uestions 3 -7, whereas Qwen displayed a flatter but 

still suboptimal error profile ( Supplementary Figure 1). See Supplementary Table 1 for error 

definitions and examples. 

4. Discussion 

The use of LLMs in pharmacoepidemiology has received increasing research attention in recent 

years. [11] Despite this growing interest, important concerns remain regarding the reliability of 

general -purpose LLMs, the suitability of their training data for scientific and regulatory tasks, and 

the performance of newly released models and prompt -engineering stra tegies in supporting 

pharmacoepidemiologic study design. These uncertainties highlight the need for systematic 

evaluation of LLM capabilities in this domain. This study addressed that need by evaluating off -

the -shelf general -purpose LLMs, biomedical LLMs, and advanced prompt -engineering strategies 

for support of pharmacoepidemiologic study design. A total of 46 study protocols from DARWIN 

EU®, the Sentinel System, and HMA -EMA were assessed by both human experts and LLMs. 

Performance was assessed across multiple dimensions, including relevance, logic of justification, 

ontology -code agreement, and error patterns. 

The main finding was that general -purpose LLMs , particularly GPT -4o and DeepSeek -R1 paired 

with LTM prompting, consistently outperformed the biomedical LLMs evaluated in this study. 10 

This pattern was observed not only for the primary outcome of relevance, but also for logic of 

justification, suggesting that the advantage of these LLMs extended beyond answer selection to 

broader methodological reasoning. Although the biomedical LLMs were domain -specific, they 

showed lower relevance, weaker justification quality, and more frequent structured failures, 

particularly irrelevant auto -generated questions and lack of justification. This suggests that domain 

adaptation alone may be insuffici ent for tasks that require not only biomedical knowledge, but also 

structured interpretation of study design elements such as index date, follow -up, covariates, and 

outcome definitions. In other words, the task appears to depend as much on methodological 

reaso ning and contextual interpretation as on domain vocabulary. This is consistent with findings 

reported by Heckmann et al. in pharmacovigilance, where general -purpose LLMs also 

outperformed biomedical LLMs on tasks requiring structured reasoning and explaina ble output .

[26] It is also in line with Dada et al., who showed that increasing biomedical specialization does 

not necessarily translate into better performance on medical tasks and may come at the cost of 

instruction -following or broader task adaptability. [27] Together, these findings suggest that 

pharmacoepidemiologic study design is not simply a knowledge retrieval task, but one that 

requires LLMs to understand instructions and organize interrelated pharmacoepidemiological 

design choices in a coherent way. The stronger performance of general -purpose LLMs therefore 

suggests that, for this type of task, broader semantic understanding may currently be more 

important than narrow biomedical tuning. 

Prompt strategy also influenced LLM performance. LTM prompting generally produced more 

relevant and better -justified outputs than Active Prompting, particularly for study design, exposure, 

and outcome -related tasks. This likely reflects the advantage of decomposing complex 

pharmacoepidemiologic reasoning into sequenti al subtasks, allowing LLMs to build more 

complete and coherent reasoning chains. For tasks that involve multiple dependent design elements, 

such structured prompting may be particularly useful. At the same time, the persistence of low -

end outliers indicate s that this structure may also increase sensitivity to early -step errors, which 

can then propagate into later responses. Prompting therefore improved performance but did not 

remove the need for caution or review. This is especially relevant in workflows wh ere later answers 11 

depend directly on earlier ones, as was the case for several of the pharmacoepidemiologic concepts 

assessed in this study. 

A notable finding was the influence of protocol source and format. HMA -EMA protocols yielded 

the highest and most stable scores, while Sentinel protocols showed broader variability, and 

DARWIN EU® protocols tended to produce lower and less stable performanc e. Because this 

pattern was seen across model types, protocol structure itself appears to shape model performance. 

This suggests that LLM outputs are influenced not only by model architecture and prompting 

strategy, but also by the clarity, consistency, a nd reporting style of the source material. If protocol 

format affects model performance, then standardization is not only a reporting issue but also a 

deployment issue. More consistently structured protocols may reduce ambiguity for both human 

and machine readers and may be especially important in highly regulated settings, including EMA -

related contexts, where documentation quality, traceability, and methodological consistency are 

central requirements. 

Ontology coding remained a clear weakness across all LLMs and protocol sources. Even when the 

LLMs performed well on narrative design tasks, ontology -code agreement was generally limited, 

particularly for more complex coding systems such as ICD, RxNorm, and SNOMED. This 

suggests that coding tasks place different demands on LLMs than narrative interpretation tasks and 

that good performance in one area does no t necessarily translate into reliable performance in the 

other. This distinction is important because ontology coding is not a minor technical detail in 

pharmacoepidemiologic work . It is part of how exposure, outcome, and covariate definitions 

become operationalizable and reproducible. Poor coding performance therefore has direct 

implications for downstream validity. This means that although LLMs may assist with 

interpretation and drafting, ontology coding should not rely on free -text inference alone. 

Deterministic support, such as curated dictionaries, crosswalks, or rule -based validation, remains 

necessary. 

4. 1 Strengths and Limitations 12 

A key strength of this study is its systematic comparison across multiple dimensions, including 

model architecture, prompt strategy, protocol source, and ontology coding . This multidimensional 

framework provides a more comprehensive picture of LLM behavior in pharmacoepidemiologic 

contexts than would be possible from isolated performance metrics alone. Another strength is the 

use of human expert ratings as a benchmark, wh ich grounds the findings in practical relevance and 

helps identify where automation may meaningfully support expert workflows and where risks 

remain. The inclusion of protocols from three major networks, DARWIN EU ®, HMA -EMA, and 

Sentinel, further strengthens the study by allowing performance to be examined across diverse 

reporting standards and coding systems. In addition, the focus on both relevance and logic of 

justification extends the evaluation beyond surface -level accuracy and addresses the 

interpretability and soundness of model reasoning. 

Several limitations should nevertheless be acknowledged. First, the study evaluated off -the -shelf 

LLMs without task -specific fine -tuning. While this reflects real -world use cases, performance may 

differ with domain -adapted training pipelines incorporating structured ontology coding . Second, 

the evaluation of logic of justification showed substantial variability, particularly for complex 

concepts, suggesting that the scoring framework may require further refinement to capture nuance 

reliably. Third, although protocols from three networks were included, they are not fully 

representative of all global pharmacoepidemiologic practices, and results may differ in settings 

with alternative reporting standards or coding systems. Finally, the reliance on Likert -scale ratings 

and agreement thresholds introduces subjectivity into the evaluation, even though multiple raters 

and comparisons were used to mitigate this limitation. 

5. Conclusions 

This study showed that off -the -shelf general -purpose LLMs, particularly GPT -4o and DeepSeek -

R1 combined with LTM prompting, outperformed the biomedical LLMs evaluated in this study 

for support of pharmacoepidemiologic study design. Their advantage was evident not only for the 

primary outcome of relevance, but also for logic of justification, indica ting that stronger 13 

performance was observed both in alignment with protocol -derived expert answers and in the 

ability to provide more coherent methodological reasoning.
