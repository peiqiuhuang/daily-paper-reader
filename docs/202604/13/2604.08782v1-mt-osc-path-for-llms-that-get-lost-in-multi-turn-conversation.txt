## Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) suffer signif-icant performance degradation when user in-structions and context are distributed over mul-tiple conversational turns, yet multi-turn (MT) interactions dominate chat interfaces. The rou-tine approach of appending full chat history to prompts rapidly exhausts context windows, leading to increased latency, higher computa-tional costs, and diminishing returns as conver-sations extend. We introduce MT-OSC, a One-off Sequential Condensation framework that efficiently and automatically condenses chat history in the background without disrupting the user experience. MT-OSC employs a Con-denser Agent that uses a few-shot inference-based Condenser and a lightweight Decider to selectively retain essential information, reduc-ing token counts by up to 72% in 10-turn di-alogues. Evaluated across 13 state-of-the-art LLMs and diverse multi-turn benchmarks, MT-OSC consistently narrows the multi-turn perfor-mance gap—yielding improved or preserved ac-curacy across datasets while remaining robust to distractors and irrelevant turns. Our results establish MT-OSC as a scalable solution for multi-turn chats, enabling richer context within constrained input spaces, reducing latency and operational cost, while balancing performance. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable results on single-turn benchmarks, yet their performance can deteriorate signifi-cantly when task instructions or critical con-text are fragmented across multiple conversa-tional turns—sometimes as early as the second exchange (Laban et al., 2025; Kwan et al., 2024). Despite this, such multi-turn (MT) interactions are characteristic of real-world dialogue, where users naturally break up information, clarifications, and intent across several exchanges. This fragmentation introduces two fundamental challenges: (1) Dispersed information across turns makes it increasingly difficult for LLMs to accu-rately recall and integrate crucial details, often lead-ing to inconsistencies and degraded performance compared to single-message queries (Laban et al., 2025); (2) The prevalent solution of concatenating the entire chat transcript consumes valuable context window and quickly becomes unsustainable, incur-ring excess latency, cost, and potential information loss as conversations grow. Although ad-hoc summarization of prior turns has become common, especially for longer chats, these methods risk omitting essential context and are often insufficient for complex multi-turn reason-ing. Prior work on chat compression(Gliwa et al., 2019; Bae et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2025) and retrieval-augmented dialogues(Xu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023) have explored strate-gies to shrink context size while preserving rele-vance. Nonetheless, such approaches frequently depend on model fine-tuning, explicit context se-lection, or complex hierarchical schemes, limiting their scalability and ease of integration. In contrast, we address a complementary chal-lenge to task decomposition research where prior work improves results by splitting a complex re-quest into multiple focused prompts (Wen et al., 2024; Sarangi et al., 2025; Prasad et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025b). Here, we focus on different, realistic scenario where information is fragmented 

over time and must be robustly integrated to avoid compounding errors. We propose MT-OSC, a One-off Sequential 

Condensation framework that systematically con-denses chat history as conversations evolve. MT-OSC operates entirely in the background, requiring no model modifications or fine-tuning and avoiding user-visible latency. Through a combination of a few-shot Condenser and a lightweight, data-driven Decider, MT-OSC dynamically preserves salient context, sharply reducing token overhead and safe-guarding against information loss. ## 2 Related Work 

A central theme in multi-turn research is com-pressing chat history into compact, reusable state representations instead of naively appending full transcripts (Gliwa et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2025a). While there have been ex-tensive work on model pre-training (Press et al., 2022; Su et al., 2023) and optimization for fine-tuning (Rafailov et al., 2024) to improve long con-text handling, the core challenge remains: preserv-ing task-relevant context as conversations lengthen. Bae et al. (2022) tackles this using finetuned T5 summarization model to retain only essential dia-logue. Zhang et al. (2022) leverage hierarchical encodings and attention for iterative refinement of conversation history. Chen et al. (2025) introduce compressive memory by integrating user informa-tion with conversation summaries; their approach relies on supervised fine-tuning and preference op-timization. Jiang et al. (2024) proposed using a small language model to compute token perplexity and selectively remove low-perplexity tokens, also reordering relevant content to beginning or end to address the lost in the middle (Liu et al., 2024a; Levy et al., 2024) issue for long-context scenarios; however, this method requires re-compression for each query, limiting the ability to cache context. In contrast, our approach is distinguished by its lack of reliance on hierarchical encodings, model fine-tuning, heuristic-based compression, or man-ual context selection. Instead, our method operates unobtrusively in the background, enabling easy in-tegration into any LLM-based chat system without specialized architectures or operational overhead. Long context prompt compression methods (Pan et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024) compress very long inputs via token/segment pruning (often importance/perplexity-based) and filter-ing/reordering. This differs from short-horizon multi-turn settings, where redundancy is limited and correctness can depend on small but decisive cues (e.g., negations, corrections, key entity men-tions); token deletion can therefore alter intent or break coreference, and reordering is less meaning-ful given the causal chronology of dialogue. As a result, these methods are not directly comparable baselines for MT-OSC, but are better viewed as complementary prompt-compression tools for very long histories. Another influential line of research, retrieval-augmented conversation (Xu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Aadhithya et al., 2024; Salama et al., 2025), selectively re-injects relevant dialogue snippets using retrieval-based methods to address context growth and improve grounding. While retrieval helps limit prompt size, it may miss nuances span-ning multiple turns or temporal dependencies, and often increases system complexity or user latency via real-time retrieval (Chen et al., 2025). By con-trast, our strategy offers a complementary alterna-tive, maintaining a continuously updated, compact representation that eliminates the need for instanta-neous look-up or large-scale context storage. Recent work has focused on systematic evalua-tion of multi-turn context handling methods. MT-Eval (Kwan et al., 2024) benchmarks LLM abil-ities on dialogue recollection, expansion, refine-ment, and follow-up. Zhang et al. (2025) stress evaluation criteria such as conversational quality, multi-turn reasoning, and tool usage. (Gorle et al., 2025) presents information loss study as multi-turn conversations progress. Laban et al. (2025) present a sharding-based evaluation protocol where fully specified single-turn data is artificially split into underspecified multistep interactions to simulate realistic chat dynamics. Baseline methods in (La-ban et al., 2025) such as CONCAT and SNOW-2Figure 1: MT-OSC framework with one-off sequential condensation of chat history (w turns/exchanges at a time). Other proposed methods like RECAP intervene only at the final turn (unknown a priori), limiting their applicability for ongoing dialogues. Furthermore, recommendations such as "retry with more time" or "consolidate before retrying" require human intervention and lack sys-tematic, automated mitigation strategies. Overall, while prior work has elucidated the scope and severity of multi-turn performance de-cline, effective and generalizable solutions for au-tomated multi-turn condensation remain relatively underexplored. MT-OSC fills this gap by provid-ing a robust, scalable condensation process that streamlines multi-turn context integration without sacrificing accuracy, latency, or deployability. ## 3 Methods 

MT-OSC Framework (illustrated in Figure 1) com-prises a Condenser agent comprising a Condenser 

that condenses chat history and a Decider which de-termines the selective application of condensation; and a one-off sequential application strategy. 

Condenser: This module leverages an LLM agent prompted in a few-shot style with explicit reasoning, tuned through curated exemplars to ro-bustly condense chat history. We construct a di-verse set of multi-turn chat examples to illustrate a spectrum of condensation policies: cases where key user text should be retained verbatim for fu-ture reference, scenarios requiring comprehensive consolidation, and situations where irrelevant turns may be safely excluded. Each hand-crafted ex-ample is paired with detailed rationales for infor-mation retention. This enables the Condenser to adapt dynamically to distinct dialogue patterns fre-quently observed in public multi-turn datasets, such as LMSYS-Chat1M (Zheng et al., 2024). These ex-emplars are independent of any of the data sources (discussed in Section 4) we use for our experiments in the paper which will further signify their gener-ality in our results (Section 5). The few-shot sample set (Figure 2) is central to maintaining nuanced context, as our ablation studies demonstrate that generic summarization often misses such subtleties (Section 5). Formally:

Decider: The Decider is a lightweight and con-figurable component that adaptively determines whether or not to apply condensation to a given 3Figure 2: Examples of Multi-Turn Chat Condensing with Reasoning.

Although condensation sub-stantially reduces token load in many cases, cer-tain dialogues where essential information is re-peatedly referenced over several turns with dense context could risk information loss if condensed. To safeguard against this, the Decider employs a rule-based function that quantifies redundancy and novelty in assistant responses relative to prior user queries. Each user message Ui and assistant re-sponse Ai is represented as a set of normalized con-tent words, following standard preprocessing (stop word removal, case normalization, and lemmati-zation). For each turn i, we extract unique, rel-evant words from Ai and remove those already mentioned in user messages up to turn i to identify new assistant terms. We further eliminate words that appear in later assistant turns, isolating content unique to each step and tracking overlap. If average overlap between assistant turns exceeds γ% and to-tal user content is over τ tokens across 4 turns, condensation is withheld to avoid potential loss of repeatedly referenced information. This selective approach ensures the condenser maintains fidelity in information-dense dialogues. Mathematically:

This approach provides a lightweight, configurable safeguard for information-rich or repetitive conversations. 

One-off Sequential Condensation: Relevant in-formation is extracted from prior User and Assis-tant turns to generate a condensed chat history, which then replaces the raw history. The process condenses the conversation w turns at a time. At turn turn w + 2 , the condensed history ( C1) is used in place of the original chat history, while the most recent turn ( w + 1 ) remains in its original form. The condensation repeats sequentially whenever another w turns have accumulated. ( C1 (computed from the first w turns) plus the next w − 1 turns after turn w). This process repeats as the dialogue progresses, ensuring that only a compact and con-textually salient form of history is included in sub-sequent model inputs.

We evaluate MT-OSC across 10 datasets, selected to encompass a wide spectrum of multi-turn chat scenarios. These datasets include both sharded static benchmarks and resembling naturally occur-ring dialogues, representing chats of up to 12 user turns—beyond the multi-turn range typically cov-ered in prior work. 

Sharded Datasets. We employ six prominent benchmarks (Laban et al., 2025)—GSM8K, BFCL-V3 Parallel, HumanEval (HEval), Spider, ToTTo, and Summary of Haystack (SoH)—in their sharded form. Each task, originally single-turn, is split into multiple turns to simulate underspecified, real-world multi-turn workflows, where instruction and context are fragmented across user utterances. 

MT-EVAL Datasets. We further adopt four datasets drawn from MT-EVAL (Kwan et al., 2024), each reflecting a different facet of real conver-sational flow: recollection, refinement, expan-sion, and follow-up. Noting data scarcity in the recollection and expansion categories (each with only ten instances), we synthesize additional sam-ples for these groups by generating new instances matched to the original style and distribution (see Appendix C.2). 

Dataset Splits. We partition all data into develop-ment, validation, and test subsets with no instance overlap in downstream evaluation. Seven datasets are held out exclusively for testing; the remainder are split to support model development and hyper-parameter tuning. Our primary results are reported on a test set of 752 multi-turn chat samples with 4–12 user turns. Complete breakdowns of corpus sizes and splits are in Appendix C. 

Robustness Evaluation Data. The datasets are relatively clean without noise, distractions, or un-necessary context. To probe robustness, we in-ject simulated noise and redundancy between turns to mirror more realistic conversational messiness. These perturbations include: (i) Repetition In-fusion —randomly duplicating N turns in each chat; ( ii ) Filler Injection —randomly inserting vac-uous filler turns (e.g., “Um,” “Hmm”); ( iii ) Con-textual Diversion —inserting topically related but task-irrelevant utterances. These methods are in-spired by prior attempts to stress-test context ro-bustness (Levy et al., 2024; Mirzadeh et al., 2025), but are newly extended here to multi-turn settings

Evaluation Metrics. We use a suite of estab-lished metrics tailored to each dataset: accuracy for code and math (HumanEval, BFCL, GSM, Spi-der, Recollection+); LLM-based quality ratings (10-point scale) for open-ended dialogic tasks (Re-finement, Follow-up, Expansion+); BLEU score for ToTTo; and a composite joint-score F1 for SoH. We evaluated the robustness of our approach using three simulated variants of the original datasets (as described in Section 4), applying both full multi-turn chat execution and MT-OSC. Our results show that the inclusion of extra turns degrades multi-turn performance relative to the clean version of the data , with a performance drop of about 7.88% for the contextual diversion 8variant and 2.5-3% for the other two variants (see Figure 7). This drop aligns with prior work, which reports that increased context length can impair task performance (Levy et al., 2024). Our results extend this observation to multi-turn scenarios. Importantly, MT-OSC effectively mitigates the impact of unnecessary information by condens-ing the chat history to retain only the most relevant content, maintaining stable performance benefits even on noisier data. 

## 6 Conclusion 

Multi-turn conversations present persistent chal-lenges for LLMs, resulting in both degraded task performance and unsustainable increases in prompt length as dialogues scale. To address these limi-tations, we introduce MT-OSC, background con-densation framework that sequentially condenses chat history as conversations grow. MT-OSC achieves significant reductions in chat history to-ken counts (up to 72% fewer tokens for 10-turn dialogues) while either preserving or enhancing accuracy across a diverse set of benchmarks and state-of-the-art LLM architectures. Our evaluation demonstrates that MT-OSC not only narrows the multi-turn performance gap but also provides robust gains in settings with conversa-tional noise or distractors, enabling more efficient and reliable interactions. Importantly, MT-OSC operates entirely in the background: it does not require model modification, retraining, or user in-tervention, and it delivers tangible improvements in latency and efficiency for real-world deployments. Overall, MT-OSC provides a practical, scalable solution to the multi-turn context bottleneck, en-abling broader applications of conversational AI in chat systems. 

## Limitations 

This study utilizes publicly available datasets that are designed for comparing single-turn and multi-turn conversational interactions. These datasets typ-ically contain fewer than 10–12 user turns per con-versation and primarily feature clean, single-topic dialogues with minimal agentic behavior—that is, they involve straightforward LLM calls without exploring the tool-use capabilities that confer agen-tic properties to LLMs. While we applied data augmentation to assess robustness, the underlying data represents limitation in linguistic complexity and topic diversity that characterize more diverse real-world conversational settings. For future work , model training can explicitly incorporate more complex multi-turn interactions, enabling forthcoming models to better handle the challenges presented by extended conversations. Developing or incorporating datasets with longer, more diverse, and multi-topic conversations would provide a richer foundation for analysis and mod-eling. Further, leveraging data that reflects greater conversational complexity, including open-domain and agent-driven exchanges, could enhance the gen-eralizability and applicability of future findings. Exploring methods such as topical decomposition may also provide valuable support for tackling the challenges posed by complex, multi-faceted dia-logue interactions.

12 A Extended related work 

Chat systems in Natural Language Processing (NLP) (field comprising text and audio process-ing) have existed well before large language mod-els (LLMs) became state of the art (Vaswani et al., 2017; Singh, 2022). NLP applications are widely used across industry verticals (Singh, 2023) and diverse data sources, including business and finance, social media, and even unstructured data (Yang et al., 2025; Singh et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024b; Singh, 2021), spanning task-oriented, data-analysis-focused (Singh et al., 2021), and free-form settings, and now LLMs are heavily used across these data types and applications. While LLMs of-fer substantial advantages along with some limita-tions (Yuan et al., 2026), they have made real-world chat applications far more prevalent by reducing reliance on separate intent/entity detection models and hand-crafted rules to control behavior based on conversational signals. Still, reliance on single-turn benchmarks prevail and multi-turn chats underper-form single-turn specified prompts. 

Memory compression Chen et al. (2025) intro-duce the idea of compressive memory where they integrate conversation summaries with information about the user. They use Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to train a Llama-2 model to summarize and com-press memories without dependence on external modules. Jiang et al. (2024) uses a small language model to calculate the perplexity of each token in the original prompt and then remove tokens with lower perplexities, and reorders relevant parts to the beginning and end to combat the "lost in the middle" problem for long-context scenarios. It re-quires re-compression for different questions, even with the same context, preventing caching of the context. In addition, aggressive compression can inadvertently remove key details, impacting accu-racy for tasks requiring nuance. 

Why long-context work is not competitive but complimentary Long-context compression methods such as LLMLingua-2 (Pan et al., 2024) and LongLLMLingua (Jiang et al., 2024) target a different regime of compressing very long inputs via token/segment pruning (often importance/perplexity-based) and, for LongLLM-Lingua, reordering/filtering. In short-horizon multi-turn setting, redundancy is limited and correctness often hinges on small but decisive state (e.g., nega-tions, corrections like ’instead,’ key entity men-tions), so token-level deletion can disproportion-ately change intent or break coreference; reorder-ing is also less meaningful when history is short and chronologically causal. Therefore, they are not an apples-to-apples comparison and not a suitable baseline for MT-OSC; rather, they are best viewed as prompt-compression tools that may be used in addition to MT-OSC for very long histories. 

Multi-turn sharded datasets and hinted solu-tion limitation Laban et al. (2025) formulates a unique evaluation method for underspecification in multi-turn conversations. They introduce a shard-ing process where they transform datasets with fully-specified instructions into sharded instruc-tions that are revealed on subsequent turns. The CONCAT method proposed in the paper could per-form better in underspecified settings but is imprac-tical for real-world scenarios, as users are unlikely to resend all prior context with each message. If automated, it is also ineffective for episodic multi-turn conversations, where the model may strug-gle to distinguish between the current and previ-ous tasks. Additionally, the approach significantly increases the number of tokens in the conversa-tion, reducing overall efficiency. There are two other methods mentioned in the paper, RECAP and SNOWBALL. Although RECAP performs better than SNOWBALL, its intervention is conducted on the last turn of the conversation, which is not known a priori, and both have been shown to have worse performance than CONCAT. The two rec-ommendations in the paper to mitigate information loss, "If time allows, try again." and "Consolidate before retrying.", are not principled approaches as they both require human evaluation and interven-tion.

## C Dataset details 

In general, there has been work done to study conversations across LLMs and applications (Pat-tnayak et al., 2025; Meghwani et al., 2025; Agarwal et al., 2025), but not specific to multi-turn short-term memory. Below, we dig into this problem and closely related datasets specific to our paper’s multi-turn conversation scope in single chat ses-sions. 

Datasets choice and details: While other multi-turn datasets exist in the literature, many contain only short conversations—for example, ToolAce (Liu et al., 2025) is limited to a maximum of four turns, and the more recent BFCL multi-turn and multi-step function calling dataset (Patil et al., 2025a) has very few examples exceeding five turns. The datasets we selected for our experiments con-tained up to 12 turns. Figure 8 shows the number of samples by number of turns for the datasets used for our experiments (sharded and MTEval datasets combined). Table 3 shows a breakdown of datasets used, their source, sizes, splits between development, val-idation, and test sets. All samples in the dataset are less than or equal to 12 turns, with a vast majority of them being <=10 turns. The total size of our development set is 50 samples, validation set is 72 samples, and test set (on which we report all the results in the paper) is 752 samples. The development set was primarily used for man-ual inspection and for analyzing the impact of dif-ferent experimental techniques and prompt varia-tions. As a result, the development subset is smaller in size relative to the validation and test subsets. To ensure unbiased evaluation, we strictly excluded any overlap between the datasets used in the de-velopment phase and those used in validation and testing. In particular, all four MT-EVAL datasets were reserved exclusively for the test set. Among the six sharded datasets, some were allocated en-tirely to the test and/or validation sets, while others were divided across the development, validation, and test sets. 

C.1 Evaluation details 

Evaluation metrics per dataset are shared in Table 3. For all datasets, we adhere as closely as possible to the original evaluation methods, making only minimal necessary assumptions. There has been more work on evaluation of multi-turn conversa-tions using LLM-as-a-Judge (Duan et al., 2024), however those are free-form chats rather as task-oriented and hence not applicable to our work. Sharded versions (Laban et al., 2025) of Hu-manEval (Chen et al., 2021), Spider (Yu et al., 2018), BFCL-V3 Parallel (Patil et al., 2025b), and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) are assessed based on binary correctness—either through execution (code in HumanEval and SQL queries in Spider) or by checking semantic equivalence to a reference an-swer (API calls in BFCL and numerical responses in GSM). Each sample consists of multiple con-versational turns, with each final answer attempt evaluated for correctness. If a correct answer is produced at any point during the conversation, the sample is considered a success. Sharded versions of ToTTo (Parikh et al., 2020) and Summary of Haystack (Laban et al., 2024) datasets resemble refinement tasks evaluated on a continuous scale from 0 to 1. ToTTo uses the BLEU metric (Papineni et al., 2002), while Sum-mary of Haystack is scored using a custom LLM-as-a-judge metric called Joint Score, designed to assess the summary’s information coverage and attribution accuracy. For both tasks, we evaluate the model’s response at the final turn of the con-versation, as it is expected that earlier turns will progressively refine the answer leading up to this point for these datasets. MT-Eval datasets (inspired by LMSYS-Chat-1M (Zheng et al., 2024) and MT-Bench (Bai et al., 2024)) for Refinement, Expansion, and Follow-up are passed through an LLM judge to rate answer for each turn in a sample, which is then averaged to get an average score per sample in the dataset. C.2 MT-EVAL Synthesis 

Two out of the four MT-EVAL datasets, namely 

Recollection and Expansion , originally contained only 10 samples each. To increase this number to 100 samples per dataset, we used the following methods: 

MT-EVAL Recollection Dataset Each sample consists of a user instruction followed by document text in all subsequent turns. The dataset contains 100 unique documents in total, with 10 documents (one per turn) used in each sample. To generate 16 additional samples, we randomly selected 10 differ-ent documents from the pool of 100 for each new sample, while keeping the top-level user instruction and task unchanged. 

MT-EVAL Expansion Dataset Each sample be-gins with a document in the first turn, followed by 10 different questions related to that document in subsequent turns. These questions depend only on the initial document and are not order-sensitive. To create variants, we shuffled the order of the 10 questions for each sample, producing 9 new vari-ants per original sample. This process, combined with the 10 original samples, resulted in a total of 100 samples for this dataset. 

C.3 Generating distracting turns 

We observe that most publicly available multi-turn datasets are relatively clean with no distractions or noise. To further assess the robustness of our exper-iments, we introduced simulated redundancy and task-irrelevant information between turns within the above datasets. This modification allowed us to evaluate performance in less curated, more real-istic conversational settings, thereby ensuring our results generalize beyond cleaner, idealized data. Specifically, we introduced the following types of redundancy and irrelevant information into the multi-turn conversations: 1. Repetition Infusion : Repeated random turns within the multi-turn dialogues - for each multi-turn conversation, we randomly select N turns in the middle (not the first turn or the last turn) and repeat them once in the same position. N is set to be a portion of the total number of turns in the conversation. 2. Filler Injection : Inserted random turns con-taining only meaningless filler words, such as ’Um.’, ’Uh.’, ’Well.’, ’Anyway.’, ’Ok.’, ’Hmm.’, etc. - for each multi-turn conversa-tion, we randomly select N turns in the middle (exclude the first turn) and add a randomly se-lected filler word as extra turn right before them. 3. Contextual Diversion : For each multi-turn chat, we randomly select N turns from the middle (excluding the first and last) and add new, contextually relevant turns that aren’t essential for task completion. If a selected turn isn’t blank, we generate an extra turn based on its content and place it immediately after. Appendix C.3 shows the prompt we use to generate the extra distracting turns.
