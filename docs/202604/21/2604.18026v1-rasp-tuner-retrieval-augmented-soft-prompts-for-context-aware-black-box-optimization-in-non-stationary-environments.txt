## Abstract 

Many deployed systems expose black-box ob-jectives whose minimizing configuration shifts with an externally observed context. When con-texts revisit a small set of latent regimes, an op-timizer that discards history pays repeated adap-tation cost; when each step must remain inex-pensive, full Gaussian-process (GP) refits at high observation counts are difficult to sustain. We cast online tuning as context-conditioned regret minimization and present RASP-Tuner , which instantiates a decomposition motivated by first principles: (i) identify a regime proxy by re-trieving similar past contexts; (ii) predict short-horizon loss with a mixture-of-experts surrogate whose input concatenates parameters, context, and a retrieved soft prompt; (iii) adapt chiefly in a low-dimensional prompt subspace, invoking full surrogate updates only when scalarized error or disagreement spikes. A RealErrorComposer maps heterogeneous streaming metrics to [0 , 1] 

via EMA-stabilized logistic scores, supplying a single differentiable training target. On nine syn-thetic non-stationary benchmarks, an adversarial-context sanity check, and three tabular real-world streams (Section 6.4), RASP-Tuner improves or matches cumulative regret relative to our GP-UCB and CMA-ES implementations on seven of nine synthetic tasks under paired tests at hori-zon T =100 , while recording 8–12 × lower wall-clock per step than sliding-window GP-UCB on identical hardware. Idealized analysis in a cluster-separated, strongly convex regime model (RA-GD) supplies sufficient conditions for bounded dynamic regret; the deployed pipeline violates several of these premises, and we articulate which gaps remain open.

## 1. Introduction 

Black-box optimization (BBO) underpins tuning of con-trollers, hyperparameters, database knobs, and resource allocations when gradients are unavailable or unreliable. Stationary textbook models rarely match deployment: light-ing, workload, and wear induce shifts in the mapping from decision variables to performance. A recurring empirical structure—also emphasized in non-stationary bandit litera-ture (Wei & Luo, 2021; Hamadanian et al., 2023)—is that 

context vectors (sensors, embeddings, logs) trace trajecto-ries that intermittently revisit a small number of operational modes. The prevailing algorithmic split poses a tension. Covariance-matrix adaptation strategies such as CMA-ES (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) maintain low per-step cost and strong local search, yet their state is tied to the evolutionary path rather than to explicit context–solution associations; when a mode returns, the optimizer may expend fresh samples to relocate an optimum already visited (Hamadanian et al., 2023). Contextual GP-UCB-style methods (Krause & Ong, 2011) specify a surrogate over (θ, c) and inherit GP regret machinery in stylized regimes, but exact GP inference scales cubically in the observation count and becomes costly when evaluations arrive at high cadence unless additional structure or approximations are introduced (Snelson & Ghahramani, 2005; Wilson et al., 2016; Shahriari et al., 2015). Retrieval-conditioned adaptation from NLP (Lewis et al., 2020; Lester et al., 2021; Khandelwal et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2023; Anantha et al., 2023) offers a different decomposition: retrieve a compact conditioning signal, update only a thin adapter, leave bulk parameters stable (Tang et al., 2022; Cao & Wang, 2022; Anupam et al., 2025). The analogy to BBO is not identity—prediction errors and exploration bonuses differ—but the information flow parallels our setting: reuse past successful evaluations indexed by context rather than re-fit a monolithic global model each step.

## 2. Related Work 

Bayesian optimization and contextual variants. 

Gaussian-process BO remains the reference class for low-dimensional, moderate-budget stationary objec-tives (Shahriari et al., 2015; Krause & Ong, 2011). Finite-time analyses of GP-UCB and variants prescribe exploration bonuses tied to posterior variance; contextual kernels over (θ, c) extend the hypothesis space but inherit 

O(N 3) inference unless sparsity, inducing points, or deep kernels intervene (Snelson & Ghahramani, 2005; Wilson et al., 2016; Shahriari et al., 2015). Non-stationary formula-tions inject time or drift processes into the prior (Bogunovic et al., 2016); dynamic regret in online convex optimization scales with variation budgets when the comparator path may shift (Besbes et al., 2015; Hall & Willett, 2015; Wei & Luo, 2021). Those frameworks seldom assume finitely recurring context clusters—the structural pattern our benchmarks emphasize. 

Evolutionary and population-based search. CMA-ES (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) tracks local geometry via covariance adaptation with low overhead per generation. Hyperparameter and architecture search pipelines (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012; Snoek et al., 2012) build on similar search primitives. Without an explicit context channel, the learner’s state summarizes recent samples rather than an indexed set of past modes, which informs our choice of memory-augmented comparisons. 

Meta-learning and learned optimizers. Offline meta-learning fits initializations or recurrent update rules on task families (Finn et al., 2017; Andrychowicz et al., 2016). RASP-Tuner instead constructs its memory online and does not assume a pretraining corpus of tasks (Liu et al., 2025); the distinction matters for credit assignment when evalua-tion budgets are thin. 

Retrieval and soft prompts. Prefix and soft-prompt meth-ods reshape transformer activations with a small trainable tensor (Lester et al., 2021); retrieval augments paramet-ric prediction with nearest-neighbor evidence (Lewis et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2023; Khandelwal et al., 2020). We trans-plant the control flow —retrieve, condition, adapt narrowly— to regret minimization: prompts gate the surrogate forward map, while retrieval selects which past evaluations anchor the prompt (Tang et al., 2022; Cao & Wang, 2022; Anantha et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2025; Hou et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2025; Anupam et al., 2025).

## 3. Method: RASP-Tuner 

First-principles decomposition. Regret accumu-lates whenever the optimizer deploys θt far from 

arg min θ f (θ, ω t). Under piecewise stationary ωt, three bottlenecks recur: (i) inferring which latent mode is active from ct; (ii) approximating the conditional loss f (·, ω t)

with limited capacity; (iii) allocating compute between revising the global surrogate and adapting a lightweight correction. RASP-Tuner maps (i) to k-NN retrieval over stored contexts, (ii) to a gated MoE regressor on 

( ˜θ, c, p), and (iii) to prompt-only stochastic updates with occasional full-network steps triggered by scalarized error or inter-expert disagreement. The design trades certifiable global optimality of the surrogate for O(1) forward depth at inference and bounded-dimensional prompt updates—an explicit inductive bias aligned with recurring contexts but misspecified when ct carries no information about ωt

4.3. Discussion 

Theorem 4.2 assumes exact gradients, convex regimes, and perfect retrieval—hypotheses violated by the Prompt-MoE pipeline. Appendix D adds Theorem D.4 (misaligned mem-ory indices inflate regret by an additive term linear in mis-take count) and Theorem D.5 (gradient perturbations ac-cumulate under bounded error). These pieces still omit acquisition-side bias from LCB with heuristic ˆvt, non-stationary replay mixing, and EMA nonlinearity. What remains is a conditional reading: • Lemma D.1 shows that if contexts live in sepa-rated balls and memory covers each regime, nearest-neighbor retrieval recovers the discrete mode—the dis-crete analogue of a Voronoi partition in context space. • Under that identification plus strong convexity, per-regime projected gradient descent attains finite dy-namic regret in the RA-GD abstraction; a single evolu-tionary search path without context indexing lacks an analogous partition and may pay extra samples when-ever the environment revisits a mode. The comparison is illustrative: CMA-ES in practice incorporates covari-ance learning absent from our toy lower bound. Closing the gap to the deployed system requires bound-ing surrogate bias, MoE calibration error, and prompt-only updates—a research program orthogonal to the empirical section below (Besbes et al., 2015; Hall & Willett, 2015). 

## 5. Benchmarks and Experimental Protocol 

We evaluate RASP-Tuner on nine non-stationary, context-rich environments plus a failure case, all imple-mented in a unified ExperimentDomain interface ex-posing context fn , metric fn , true loss , and 

build sequence . Full mathematical definitions are de-ferred to Appendix A; here we briefly summarize the sce-narios.

6.2. Ablations and Efficiency 

Figure 3 juxtaposes three diagnostics: memory ablation, wall-clock per step, and threshold-crossing adaptation steps. 

Memory ablation. NoMemory-RASP masks ct before retrieval, severing the link between context keys and stored prompts. Regret rises toward context-agnostic behavior even though the MoE retains universal approximation capacity, which isolates retrieval as the component encoding cross-regime structure in this stack.

## 7. Discussion and Limitations 

Augmenting time versus indexing regimes. Stationariz-ing (θ, c) with explicit time coordinates or expanding slid-ing windows lets a single surrogate track slow drift when capacity permits. RASP-Tuner instead bets on finite recur-rence : contexts that approximately repeat admit a discrete memory index. The bet pays when Voronoi-like separation holds (Lemma D.1); it fails when ct is orthogonal to ωt, as in Section 6.3. 

Empirical regimes of advantage. Across the synthetic suite, the largest margins appear where modes repeat and 

ct encodes mode identity (LLM Inference, Switching LQR, Server Flash Crowd). High-dimensional contexts inflate GP covariance cost, reinforcing the latency gap recorded in Figure 3. Claims about “thousands of steps” remain speculative: our reported runs stop at T =100 ; extrapolation demands experiments at longer horizons. 

When retrieval hurts. Any method that writes memory from uninformative ct risks coupling noise to parameters. Practitioners can screen dependence by measuring predic-tive utility of ct against offline logs before enabling contex-tual retrieval. 

Evaluation scope. The T =100 protocol controls vari-ance across nine generators; it undersamples the large-N

regime where sparse GPs, TPE, or TuRBO challenge sliding-window GP-UCB. Latency numbers compare two concrete implementations on one machine class; they bound nei-ther algorithmic asymptotics nor GPU-accelerated GP code paths. 

Memory and theory. FIFO-capped memory omits cover-age guarantees when the context stream covers an expanding support set. Closing the theory gap requires simultaneous bounds on MoE empirical risk, prompt-only dynamics, and retrieval FNR—each introduces distinct technical obstacles beyond RA-GD. 

## 8. Impact Statement 

PromptMemory retains context vectors and parameter traces that may identify users or infrastructure in deployed services. Mitigations include TTL eviction, differential privacy noise on ki, or hashed keys at the expense of retrieval fidelity. The adversarial-context benchmark is not decorative: it demonstrates harmful variance when features are injected without predictive value. ## 9. Conclusion 

RASP-Tuner operationalizes a decomposition—retrieve a regime proxy, predict with a gated surrogate, adapt prompts before touching shared weights—tailored to non-stationary BBO when contexts recur. Under fixed hyperparameters and T =100 , the agent lowers terminal regret relative to our GP-UCB and CMA-ES baselines on seven of nine synthetic tasks while cutting measured step latency by an order of magnitude against that GP implementation. The ablation and adversarial experiments delineate when memory car-ries signal versus noise. Theoretically, Proposition 4.1 and Theorems 4.2, D.4, and D.5 chart sufficient regimes for sim-plified surrogates; the MoE pipeline only partially overlaps those assumptions. 

Next steps. (i) Benchmark against TPE, trust-region BO, and warm-started CMA-ES under matched budgets; (ii) calibrate ˆvt via probabilistic scores before interpreting LCB as exploration guidance; (iii) extend horizons to stress-test memory management and GP approximations.
