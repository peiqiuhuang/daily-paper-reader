## Abstract 

Adapting large language models (LLMs) to a targeted task efficiently and effectively remains a fundamental challenge. Such adaptation often requires iteratively improving the model toward a targeted task, yet collecting high-quality human-labeled data to support this process is costly and difficult to scale. As a result, synthetic data generation has emerged as a flexible and scalable alternative. One straightforward approach is through an iterative generation–training loop , where candidate data are synthesized through an external generator, the model is updated using these data and the process is repeated over iterations. However, generated samples can be noisy, highly redundant, or even misaligned with the targeted task distribution. Training indiscriminately on such data can dilute useful learning signals and even degrade model performance. To address this, we introduce a refined paradigm, namely an iterative generation–selection–training loop ,which incorporates a selection step prior to model updates. Building on this paradigm, we propose E VO SELECT , a data-efficient framework to evolve LLM effectively. Given candidate samples produced by the data generator, E VO SELECT selects training data by jointly modeling targeted task alignment and diversity. We estimate task relevance through optimal transport with proxy gradient representations, which quantifies how well candidate samples align with the targeted task distribution. To mitigate redundancy, we incorporate a diversification mechanism that promotes coverage of complementary training samples. By interleaving alignment and diversification, E VO SELECT enables progressive LLM evolution toward targeted tasks. Extensive experiments on various benchmarks demonstrate that with either weak or strong data generators, E VO SELECT consistently improves adaptation efficacy over existing data selection methods. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong general-purpose capabilities and are also widely deployed as powerful autonomous agents (Minaee et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2026). However, many real-world applications require adapting LLMs to specific task distributions under limited supervision and constrained post-training resources (Zhang et al., 2026; Han et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025a; Albalak et al., 2024). To overcome the limitation, one prevalent solution is via a practical setting: iterative generation–training loop (Zelikman et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2025), where candidate data is synthesized by an external data generator, used to update the base model through fine-tuning, and the process is repeated over multiple iterations. However, even with abundant synthetic data, new challenges emerge: data efficacy and data efficiency . Firstly, not all generated examples are equally useful. Under the iterative setting, these generated samples can progressively drift away from the targeted task, leading to reduced effectiveness for adaptation. Secondly, even well-aligned samples may become redundant over successive iterations, adding little new information while increasing unnecessary computational cost. To solve this puzzle, we argue that effective data selection in an it-erative setting must jointly con-sider two objectives across itera-tions. On one hand, selected exam-ples should be target-aligned , mean-ing they need to stay functionally relevant to the targeted tasks. On the other hand, selected examples must maintain diversity , covering complementary regions of the data space to avoid accumulating re-dundancy over iterations. How-ever, existing approaches typically address these objectives in isola-tion. Some methods leverage train-ing data attribution (Deng et al., 2025) to prioritize target-aligned examples (Xia et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2023), while others emphasize diversity through naive sampling or clus-tering strategies (Ding et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024; Ling et al., 2025). Even when both aspects are considered, they are often combined in a sequential/heuristic manner, such as through weighted sums, multiplication or local diversity adjustment (Liu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025b). In response, we propose E VO SELECT in this paper, which synergizes data attribution and diversity into a unified, principled framework for effective data selection in targeted task adaptation. Given a pool of generated candidate samples in each iteration, E VO SELECT as-sesses task relevance through an optimal-transport-based alignment and promotes diversity by favoring samples that provide complementary training signals. A key component of EVO SELECT is the use of optimal transport (OT) (Peyr ´e & Cuturi, 2019; Flamary et al., 2021) 1

for target distribution alignment. Instead of matching a single centroid as in attribution-only methods (Xia et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2023; San Joaquin et al., 2024), OT aligns the training and full target distribution. In Figure 1, we demonstrate that due to the naive selection procedure, attribution-only methods may only select data according to the largest cluster (i.e. the red cluster) and fail to cover the entire target distribution. E VO SELECT employs an interleaved optimization procedure that iteratively refines sample importance by jointly optimizing target alignment and diversity, synergizing two signals in the iterative process. To demonstrate the superiority of E VO SELECT , we conduct evaluation across 10 knowledge-intensive benchmarks with both weak/strong data generators. Experimental results show that E VO SELECT outperforms strong data selection baselines and even training on all generated data across different selection ratios and iterations. ## 3 Methodology 

In this section, we illustrate our method, E VO SELECT , which selects data to evolve LLM for targeted task adaptation. In Section 3.1, we first discuss the limitations of existing methods. Motivated by these limitations, we then introduce E VO SELECT in Section 3.2. Specifically, EVO SELECT inherently considers task alignment and diversity simultaneously and synergies both signals into a unified, principled selection framework for LLM adaptation. 

3.1 Limitations of Existing Methods Attribution-Only Selection. A popular approach to materialize data selection for targeted task adaptation is through data attribution methods (Deng et al., 2025; Xia et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025a), especially gradient-based ones 2. These methods assign attribution score to each training sample typically via gradient similarity (Pruthi et al., 2020) or variants of influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2017; Kwon et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). Each training sample is scored by its similarity to validation samples, and the overall score is obtained by averaging over the validation set.

However, this naive averaging implicitly reduces the validation distribution to a single centroid representation. As a result, samples are ranked based on their proximity to this average signal, without accounting for the geometric structure of the data. When selecting the top-k samples as selected data, this leads to a subset concentrated in a narrow region of the training space. What’s worse, this concentration manifests as redundancy: many selected samples exhibit highly similar gradient directions. Consequently, the selected subset provides limited additional coverage of the target distribution, leading to inefficient use of the selection budget and even diluting the effectiveness of training . We compute the Vendi Score (Friedman & Dieng, 2022), a widely-used diversity metric, to showcase this redundancy issue (the higher, the less redundant). As shown in Figure 2, while attribution-only selection can successfully identify task-aligned samples (Fig. 2a), the selected samples exhibit the lowest Vendi Score (i.e. the least diverse) among baselines (Fig. 2b). 

Diversity-Only Selection. On the other hand, diversity-based selection methods (Wu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2025) attempt to address redundancy by encouraging coverage of the data space. These approaches typically select samples that maximizes the pairwise embedding distance (Wu et al., 2023) or kernel similarity measure (Wang et al., 2024). While such methods promote coverage of the training distribution, they do not explicitly account for the target task represented by the validation set. Consequently, the selected subset may be diverse but poorly aligned with the target objective, which may potentially degrade performance. In Figure 2, we can observe that the diversity-only method selects training samples that are the least aligned with the task (Fig. 2a), even though it achieves the highest Vendi Score (i.e. the most diverse) among all the baselines (Fig. 2b). 

3.2 EVO SELECT : Synergizing Data Attribution and Diversification 

In this section, we detail our design of E VO SELECT . To efficiently represent each data sample, we first utilize a proxy model to collect gradient features for both training and validation samples. Then, we apply an OT-based iterative algorithm to simultaneously optimize the targeted task alignment and coverage over the data representation space. 

Proxy Model and Data Representations. Recent gradient-based attribution meth-ods (Charpiat et al., 2019; Pruthi et al., 2020) approximate sample influence via the dot product of gradient features. Formally, the similarity between training sample x and valida-tion sample x′ is given by ∇Θℓ(xi; Θ)⊤∇Θℓ(x′; Θ), where ℓ(x; Θ) denotes the loss function. Motivated by this, we represent each data by its gradient feature: ∇Θℓ(x; Θ). However, collecting per-sample gradients requires a full backward pass per example, leading to a prohibitive computational cost. To overcome this limitation, we utilize a proxy model (Engstrom et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2025; Diao et al., 2025) to collect approximate gradient representations, where the proxy model is much smaller than the base LLM. In addition, the representations are compressed through projection techniques 3 that efficiently preserve pair-wise distances. We also normalize these compressed gradients. ## 4 Experiment 

4.1 Experimental Setup General setting. We consider the generation–selection–training setting. At each iteration, an external data generator produces candidate examples based on the current seed set and a fixed validation set. A selection method is then applied to construct a training subset under a specified selection ratio, after which the base LLM is fine-tuned on the selected data. In this paper, we run this process for two iterations. The construction for seed/validation sets and partition for each dataset is detailed in Appendix B. The problem/answer generation configurations and prompts are included in Appendix C. The detailed training configuration is in Appendix D. 

Datasets. We consider a diverse set of benchmarks spanning science, commonsense, logical and biomedical question answering datasets. These datasets include (i) scientific reasoning: 

ARC-Challenge (Clark et al., 2018), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018) and ClimaQA (Manivannan et al., 2024); (ii) commonsense/logical reasoning: 

CommonsenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019), LogiQA (Liu et al., 2020) and LogiQA2 (Liu et al., 2023); (iii) biomedical/healthcare reasoning: Med-MCQA (Pal et al., 2022), MedQA (Jin et al., 2021) and 

HeadQA (Vilares & G ´omez-Rodr ´ıguez, 2019). The prompts and detailed configuration for evaluation is in Appendix E. 

Models. We primarily conduct experiments with the Qwen2.5 family (Team, 2024b) due to its various model sizes and wild adoption. The data generator is fixed to 

Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct , which is used to generate questions and solutions. The LLM to be trained (i.e. the base model) is either Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct (i.e. strong generator setting) or Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct (i.e. weak generator setting), and we use Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct 

as the proxy model to collect gradient features. 

EVO SELECT and baseline selection methods. For E VO SELECT , we fix learning rate η = 0.1, consider update steps T ∈ { 10, 20 } and regularization coefficient ϵ ∈ { 0.5, 1.0 }. The selection ratios are 0.2 and 0.5. We also evaluate several baseline methods. First, we include simple methods: All and Random , which train the base model on full and random subsets. Second, we consider Diversity , which selects examples by prioritizing smaller clusters (Jung et al., 2025), and Attribution (Xia et al., 2024), which favors samples with high gradient similarity scores against validation data. For methods that incorporate diversity and task relevance, we consider TSDS (Liu et al., 2024) as one representative method. ## 5 Related Work 

Data Selection for LLM Adaptation. Existing work can be grouped into attribution, diversity and hybrid approaches. Attribution-only methods (Xia et al., 2024; San Joaquin et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023) mostly rely on variants of influence-function (Koh & Liang, 2017) to estimate the contribution of each sample, but they may under-cover the target distribution. Diversity-only methods (Wu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2025) instead improve coverage via sampling/clustering, but they do not model task alignment. Some works attempt to consider both aspects: in pretraining, these methods (Liu et al., 2025; Zhuang et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025) combine quality and diversity through mixture weighting. However, these methods cannot be directly applied to target task adaption since (1) they operate without target-conditioned signals and (2) they learn mixture weights at the corpus or domain level instead of sample level. For targeted fine-tuning, some methods (Zhang et al., 2025b; Wu et al., 2024) typically combine multiple criteria through heuristic scoring functions and TSDS (Liu et al., 2024) solves a distribution alignment problem with an additional diversity regularizer and performs sampling to select data. In summary, these methods either rely solely on attribution/diversification or combine the two in a heuristic manner. 

Optimal Transport for Data Attribution. We note that a line of work leverages Optimal Transport (OT) (Peyr ´e & Cuturi, 2019) to compute data attribution scores. LAVA (Just et al., 2023) and SAVA (Kessler et al., 2024) directly use the magnitude of the calibrated gradient of the OT objective as attribution scores. In contrast, TSDS (Liu et al., 2024) solves for a probability distribution over candidate data via an OT-based objective with local diversity augmentation, and samples training instances from this distribution. Similarly, TAROT (Feng et al., 2024) leverages greedy algorithm to select data that minimizes OT distance between candidate training and validation samples. We note that to the best of our knowledge, E VO SELECT is the first OT-based method that directly synergizes attribution and diversity objective for targeted data selection. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We study data-efficient LLM adaptation under an iterative generation-selection-training loop ,where generated samples can be redundant or misaligned with the target task and thus need to be selected before training. We propose E VO SELECT , an OT-based selection framework that jointly models task alignment and diversity to identify high-utility training examples for each iteration. Experiments across various benchmarks show that E VO SELECT consistently outperforms existing baselines under both weak and strong generators, highlighting the importance of a principled selection framework to achieve effective LLM adaptation. 9Preprint. ## D Training Configurations 

The base model is post-trained using SFT with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) adapters through TRL (von Werra et al., 2020). For LoRA, we set r = 64, α = 128 and dropout rate 0.05. ## F Baselines 

We elaborate the detailed setup for each baseline we compare in Section 4. 1. Attribution (Xia et al., 2024) selects top-k samples with highest scores, which are computed by the inner product between training and the average validation representations. Gradients are obtained from proxy model and no warm-up training is involved. 2. Diversity (Jung et al., 2025) first runs K-means clustering over the training repre-sentations, then keeps filling the candidate pool from the smallest clusters until the budget is exhausted. We follow the default configuration: cluster ratio = 0.1 (i.e. #clusters = #samples * cluster ratio). 3. Attr-Div first filters out all the bottom-25% data by attribution scores, then apply the same selection strategy as Diversity to select data from the pruned candidates. 4. TSDS (Liu et al., 2024) finds candidate samples that are closest to the validation examples via OT and down-weights redundant ones using a kernel density es-timate (KDE). It then samples from the associated probability distribution. We follow the default configuration: max k (maximum kNN neighbors) is set to 5000, kde k (neighborhood size for KDE) is set to 1000, KDE bandwidth σ = 0.75, OT regularization α = 0.5, OT regularization scale C = 5.0.

Performances vs full-data training. Figure 5 compares E VO SELECT against baseline meth-ods relative to training on the full dataset under two selection ratios by enumerating performances across all datasets. Across both settings, E VO SELECT consistently achieves the highest performance, surpassing all baselines. Notably, even at a lower selection ratio, EVO SELECT consistently exceeds full-data performance, indicating its ability to identify high-quality subsets. In contrast, attribution-only and diversity-only methods exhibit in-consistent behavior, often falling below the full-data baseline, while heuristic combinations (Attr-Div ) provide limited improvement. TSDS (Liu et al., 2024) shows competitive perfor-mance but remains consistently below E VO SELECT , suggesting that its way of combining alignment and diversity is not sufficiently effective.
