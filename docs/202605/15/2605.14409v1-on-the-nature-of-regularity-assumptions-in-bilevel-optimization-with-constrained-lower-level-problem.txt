# On the Nature of Regularity Assumptions in Bilevel Optimization with Constrained Lower-level Problem 

## Xiaotian Jiang, Chang He, Mingyi Hong, Shuzhong Zhang ∗

## May 15, 2026 

Abstract 

In this paper, we study the regularity assumptions commonly adopted in bilevel optimization with constrained lower-level problems, including the linear independence constraint qualification, the strict complementary slackness condition, and the second-order sufficient condition. These conditions are typically required to hold for the lower-level problem at every upper-level variable 

x. We first show that the requirement that these conditions hold at every upper-level variable x

is strong, in the sense that it is non-prevalent: there exist problems for which no sufficiently small perturbation of the lower-level objective and constraints can make the conditions hold at every 

x. To establish the result, we prove rigidity theorems showing that certain structural quantities of the lower-level problem must remain invariant across all x whenever these conditions hold everywhere. We then construct explicit counterexamples in which these invariants differ between two values of x. In contrast, we show that the weaker requirement, that these conditions hold at almost every x, is a weak assumption, in the sense that it is prevalent: with probability one over a random perturbation of the lower-level objective and constraints, each condition holds at almost every x. We further analyze the gap between the two requirements. Although the “every 

x” and “almost every x” versions differ only on a measure-zero set, we show that this difference introduces fundamental difficulties in both theory and computation for bilevel optimization. 

# 1 Introduction 

BiLevel Optimization (BLO) has emerged as a fundamental modeling paradigm for hierarchical decision-making problems across machine learning, operations research, and game theory. It has been applied widely in modern learning systems, including hyperparameter optimization (Maclaurin et al., 2015; Franceschi et al., 2018), meta-learning (Finn et al., 2017; Franceschi et al., 2018), reinforcement learning (Zeng et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2023), and more recently machine unlearning (Reisizadeh et al., 2025) and large-scale model alignment (Li et al., 2024, 2025). In such applications, BLO provides a principled framework for representing two-stage interactions in which a decision at the upper level depends implicitly on the solution to a lower-level optimization problem. In the bilevel setting, these conditions are imposed on the lower-level problem viewed as a parametric optimization problem in y with upper-level variable x. Specifically, when we say “LICQ holds for the lower-level problem at upper-level variable x,” we mean that LICQ holds at every feasible point y ∈ Y (x). Similarly, “SCSC holds for the lower-level problem at upper-level variable 

x” means that SCSC holds at every KKT point of the lower-level problem at x, and “SOSC holds for the lower-level problem at upper-level variable x” means that the uniform version (Remark 1.1) holds at every local minimizer of the lower-level problem at x.2These three conditions are standard in the optimization literature and are commonly assumed in works on bilevel optimization with constrained lower-level problems. In the bilevel setting, the specific combination of regularity conditions required depends on the structure of the lower-level problem. The following three combinations are commonly adopted in the literature: 

• Strongly convex lower-level with LICQ (Khanduri et al., 2023; Xu and Zhu, 2023; Kornowski et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024a; Shen et al., 2025); 

• Strongly convex lower-level with LICQ and SCSC (Kwon et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2025a); 

• Non-strongly-convex lower-level with LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC (Giovannelli et al., 2021; Nolasco et al., 2025). We formalize these requirements as the following statement. 

Statement 1 (“Every x” regularity) . For every x ∈ X , LICQ (Definition 1.1), SCSC (Defini-tion 1.2), and uniform SOSC (Remark 1.1) hold for the lower-level problem. 

In this work, we aim to justify the validity of these conditions within the context of BLO, testing the three standard combinations listed above. A natural question that arises at this point is: 

Question 1: How strong is Statement 1? 

To address this question, we need a notion for quantifying the strength of an assumption. In this work, we adopt the perspective of prevalence to evaluate this strength. 

Definition 1.4 (Prevalence (informal)) . A property is said to be prevalent within a given function space if there exists a finite-dimensional family of perturbations such that, for any element in this space, a random perturbation from this family makes the perturbed element satisfy the property with probability one. 

The formal definition is given in Definition A.1. In this work, the function space is taken to be the space of smooth functions. In other words, if a property is prevalent, then after any sufficiently small perturbation, almost every perturbed problem satisfies the property. Such a property should therefore be viewed as a weak assumption. To contrast with this notion, we now introduce non-prevalent property. 

Definition 1.5 (Non-prevalence (informal)) . A property is said to be non-prevalent within a given function space if there exists an element in this space such that, for any finite-dimensional family of perturbations, the set of perturbations under which the perturbed element does not satisfy this property has positive measure. 

Formally, a property is non-prevalent if it is not prevalent in the sense of Definition A.1. In practice, we focus on a stronger form: there exist an element v and a constant ϵ > 0 such that every perturbation with norm less than ϵ yields a perturbed element v + p that does not satisfy the property (see Section 2). Such a property should therefore be regarded as a strong assumption. Based on the above definition, Question 1 can be restated as follows. 

Question 1.1: Is Statement 1 prevalent? 

Regrettably, as a primary contribution of this work, we provide a negative answer to Question 1.1. The non-prevalence of “every-x‘’ type regularity assumptions has also been observed in other bilevel settings (Jiang et al., 2025b). Nonetheless, “almost-every-x” type conditions have already been adopted in the literature as a workable basis for algorithm design (Khanduri et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2025b), which motivates us to formalize the following version of our regularity requirement: 3Statement 2 (“Almost every x” regularity) . For almost every x ∈ X , LICQ (Definition 1.1), SCSC (Definition 1.2), and SOSC (Definition 1.3) hold for the lower-level problem. 

This motivates a two-fold exploration of Question 1.1, again focusing on the three standard combinations listed above: 

Question 2: Is Statement 2 prevalent? Why is Statement 1 non-prevalent? 

Our analysis reveals a clear separation: Statement 2 is prevalent (Section 3), while Statement 1 is not (Section 2). We establish the prevalence of the former by utilizing Sard’s Theorem and transversality theory. To understand why the “at every x” requirement fails to be prevalent, we identify certain persistent structural patterns or invariants that cannot be eliminated by any C2

perturbation of the lower-level objective and constraints whose norm is sufficiently small. These inherent obstructions offer a critical perspective for understanding the fundamental non-prevalence of such strong assumptions. Having established the prevalence of Statement 2 and identified the structural obstructions that prevent Statement 1 from being prevalent, it is natural to ask how different Statement 2 really is from Statement 1. Although these two statements differ only on a measure-zero set of upper-level variables, it is well known that in optimization, properties that differ only on a measure-zero set can still belong to fundamentally different problem classes. For example, a smooth function and a nonsmooth function may differ only on a measure-zero set of nondifferentiable points, yet they require entirely different algorithmic treatments. A similar phenomenon may arise here: the difference between Statement 1 and Statement 2, despite occurring only on a measure-zero set of upper-level variables, can lead to fundamentally different outcomes in a BLO problem with a constrained lower-level problem. This motivates the following question: 

Question 3: What is the gap between Statement 1 and Statement 2? 

We will show that, despite differing only on a measure-zero set of upper-level variables, the two requirements lead to fundamentally different consequences: failure on this measure-zero set can cause the Lagrange multiplier to lose uniqueness and continuity, the hyperfunction to lose differentiability, or a branch of local minimizers to terminate abruptly. In all cases, the sensitivity formulas used for hypergradient computation become ill-conditioned in an entire neighborhood of each failure point (Section 4). 

## 1.1 Related work 

Regularity Assumptions in Bilevel Optimization with Constrained Lower-Level Prob-lems: A number of recent works on bilevel optimization with constrained lower-level problems rely on regularity assumptions, including LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC. Giovannelli et al. (2021) introduced a hyperfunction-based method to solve (1.1) with nonconvex lower-level objective g and nonconvex functional lower-level constraints h. To ensure the differentiability of hyperfunction, they assume 

y∗(x) is unique for any x, and LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC hold at the lower-level solution y∗(x)for every upper-level variable x. Xu and Zhu (2023) study bilevel problems (1.1) with strongly convex lower-level objective g and convex functional lower-level constraints h. They analyze the hypergradient at differentiable points and study the Clarke subdifferential of the hyperfunction at non-differentiable points. In their analysis, they assume that LICQ holds at the lower-level solution 

y∗(x) for every upper-level variable x. Kwon et al. (2023) study problem (1.1) with nonconvex lower-level objective g and convex lower-level constraint functions h. They introduce a penalized hyper-objective and analyze its relationship with the original bilevel problem (1.1) . To ensure 4sufficient regularity of the lower-level solution set of (1.1) , they assume that for every upper-level variable x, there exists at least one lower-level solution that satisfies LICQ and SCSC. Khanduri et al. (2023) consider (1.1) with strongly convex lower-level objective g and uncoupled linear constraints h

of the form {y : Ay ≤ b}. Under some technical assumptions, they show that adding a single random linear perturbation q⊤y to the lower-level objective g ensures that the resulting hyperfunction is differentiable at all iterates generated by a gradient-based algorithm with probability one. In their analysis, they also assume that LICQ holds at the lower-level solution y∗(x) for every upper-level variable x. Kornowski et al. (2024) consider (1.1) with strongly convex lower-level objective g

and two kinds of lower-level constraints h: coupled linear equality constraints {y : Ax + By = b}

and coupled linear inequality constraints {y : Ax + By ≤ b}. They require that LICQ holds for every feasible point y and for every upper-level variable x of the lower-level problem. This LICQ assumption ensures that the hypergradient is computable, and the Lagrange multipliers remain Lipschitz continuous with respect to the upper-level variable x under the inequality constrained case. Jiang et al. (2024a) consider a Lagrangian-based penalty reformulation for bilevel problems (1.1) with strongly convex lower-level objective g and convex functional lower-level constraints h. To guarantee that the Lagrange multipliers associated with the lower-level problem remain well-defined and continuous with respect to the upper-level variable x, they assume that LICQ holds for every upper-level variable x and for all feasible lower-level solutions y. Nolasco et al. (2025) study problem (1.1) with a convex lower-level objective g and convex lower-level constraint functions h. In order to ensure that the lower-level solution y∗(x) and its associated multipliers λ∗(x) are differentiable with respect to the upper-level variable x, they assume that LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC hold at y∗(x) for every x. Shen et al. (2025) consider problem (1.1) with a strongly convex lower-level objective g and linear lower-level constraint functions of the form {y : Ay + Bx ≤ b}. To ensure the existence and uniqueness of the lower-level Lagrange multipliers, they assume that LICQ holds at every feasible point y for every upper-level variable x.

Genericity of Regularity Assumptions in Single Level Constrained Optimization: 

The genericity of regularity conditions in nonlinear optimization has been studied under different notions of genericity. One notion of genericity is measure-theoretic and is formulated through finite-dimensional perturbations. In the single-level setting, Spingarn and Rockafellar (1979) considered linear perturbations of the objective and constant perturbations of the constraints, and showed that, for almost every perturbation parameter in the Lebesgue sense, LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC hold. This notion of genericity is the same as the prevalence notion used in this paper. A different notion of genericity is formulated in smooth function spaces: a property is called generic if the set of smooth functions satisfying it is open and dense in the corresponding strong 

C∞ topology. Jongen et al. (2000) developed a transversality-based theory establishing that, for an open and dense set of smooth problem data, LICQ holds at every feasible point and every critical point is nondegenerate. In particular, every local minimizer of such a problem satisfies LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC. For parametric programs min y g(x, y ) s.t. h(x, y ) ≤ 0 with parameter x ∈ Rn, building on the transversality-based viewpoint of Jongen et al. (2000), Still (2019) shows that, for generic smooth problem data, the singular set where any of LICQ, SCSC, or SOSC fails is confined to strata of codimension at least m + 1 in ( x, y )-space, where m is the dimension of y.However, for parameter-dependent problems, a measure-theoretic genericity theory based on concrete finite-dimensional perturbations remains missing. Moreover, Jongen et al. (2000) and Still (2019) show that, for generic smooth problem data, degeneracies, if present, occur only on small subsets of the parameter-state space. They do not show that degeneracies are unavoidable for a robust class of problems. In other words, they do not construct robust examples for which no sufficiently small perturbation can make the every-x regularity property hold. 5Beyond the classical NLP setting, related genericity and stability results have been established for several structured problem classes. These include linear conic and semidefinite programming (Al-izadeh et al., 1997; D¨ ur et al., 2017); semi-algebraic, definable, and tame optimization, with emphasis on constraint qualifications, active manifolds, and continuity of set-valued maps (Daniilidis and Pang, 2011; Drusvyatskiy et al., 2016; Lee and Ph a. m, 2017; Bolte et al., 2018); and bilevel programs, where genericity of partial calmness has been established via singularity-theoretic classifications of generalized critical points (Ke et al., 2022). 

## 1.2 Contributions 

In this work, we study the strength and limitations of the regularity assumptions. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: 

• We prove that Statement 1 is non-prevalent in the sense of Definition 1.5, and hence is a strong assumption. The argument proceeds in two stages. First, we establish rigidity theorems showing that if a regularity condition holds at every x, then certain structural quantities must remain invariant across all x ∈ X : LICQ implies the stratification of the feasible set Y(x) is preserved (Theorem 2.1); LICQ and SCSC together imply that no continuous KKT trajectory can migrate across different strata of Y(x) (Theorem 2.5); and LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC together imply the number of local minimizers on each stratum is constant (Theorem 2.8). Second, we construct explicit counterexamples in which these structural invariants differ between two different x, and prove perturbation stability results (Theorems 2.2, 2.6, 2.9) guaranteeing that these differences persist under any sufficiently small perturbation. The rigidity theorems then certify that the corresponding regularity condition must fail at some x

for any perturbed problem (Counterexamples 2.3, 2.4, 2.7, 2.10). Notably, Theorem 2.8 is different in nature from classical Robinson-type stability results Robinson (1980). Robinson-type results are local: they provide a local continuation of a given solution or KKT point in a neighborhood of itself. Theorem 2.8 is global: it asserts that the stratum-wise count of local minimizers is constant across the entire upper-level variable space X , which in particular rules out the emergence of new local minimizers at any point of X . Establishing this global statement requires, beyond local continuation, a closedness property of the graph of local minimizers. 

• We show that Statement 2 is prevalent in the sense of Definition 1.4, and hence is a weak assumption. Specifically, after applying an arbitrarily small constant perturbation to the constraint functions and a linear perturbation to the objective, each of these three conditions holds at almost every x with probability one (Theorems 3.1, 3.2, 3.3). This fills a gap in the measure-theoretic genericity theory for parameter-dependent problems based on concrete finite-dimensional perturbations. The single-level case follows immediately by taking the upper-level variable space to be a singleton, recovering the genericity results presented in Spingarn and Rockafellar (1979). 

• We analyze the gap between Statement 1 and Statement 2 by examining the structural consequences of regularity failure on a measure-zero set. We show that LICQ failure causes the Lagrange multiplier to lose uniqueness and continuity (Section 4.1); SCSC failure under strongly convex lower-level causes the hyperfunction to lose differentiability, turning the bilevel problem from smooth into nonsmooth (Section 4.2); and SCSC or uniform SOSC failure under non-strongly-convex lower-level can cause a branch of local minimizers to terminate abruptly, making the solution mapping discontinuous (Section 4.3). In all three cases, the sensitivity 6formulas (4.1)(4.2) used for hypergradient computation become ill-conditioned not only at the failure points but throughout an entire neighborhood surrounding them; 

• We show that LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC together imply uniform local quadratic growth over the upper-level variable space (Theorem 4.2). This uniform growth condition is the structural foundation underlying the global smooth continuation of local minimizer branches (Theorem 4.1). We further clarify the distinct roles played by each condition. Uniform SOSC controls curvature within the critical cone. SCSC provides a uniform lower bound on the multipliers at active constraints, ensuring quadratic growth in directions outside the critical cone. 

# 2 Non-Prevalence of LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC: “Every-x” Case 

In this section, we address Question 1.1 from the introduction: whether Statement 1, which requires LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC to hold at every x under the three settings listed in Section 1, is prevalent. We show that the answer is negative. For each of LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC, we construct explicit counterexamples demonstrating that no sufficiently small C2 perturbation of the lower-level objective and constraints can make the corresponding condition hold at all upper-level variables x simultaneously. Our approach has two stages. In the first stage, we prove rigidity theorems: if a regularity condition holds at every x, then certain structural features of the lower-level problem must remain invariant across all x ∈ X . The three rigidity results below correspond to the three settings commonly adopted in the bilevel literature, respectively: (i) strongly convex lower-level with LICQ, (ii) strongly convex lower-level with LICQ and SCSC, and (iii) non-strongly-convex lower-level with LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC (see Remark 1.1): 

• LICQ for every x implies that the feasible set Y(x), viewed as a manifold with generalized boundary (Figure 1), has a natural stratification (indexed by active constraint patterns) that is preserved across all x ∈ X up to a stratification-preserving diffeomorphism (Theorem 2.1); this result is independent of the convexity of the lower-level objective. 

• LICQ and SCSC for every x further imply that the active constraint index set along any continuous KKT trajectory is constant (Theorem 2.5); this result does not require strong convexity, although strong convexity automatically provides such a trajectory via the unique minimizer. Here, a KKT trajectory refers to a continuous map x 7 → y∗(x) such that y∗(x) is a KKT point of the lower-level problem at each x.

• LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC for every x further imply that the number of local minimizers on each stratum of Y(x) stays constant across all x ∈ X (Theorem 2.8). In the second stage, we use these rigidity theorems to construct concrete counterexamples. We exhibit specific problems in which the relevant structural quantity (the stratification type, the active set along a KKT trajectory, or the stratum-wise count of local minimizers) differs between two upper-level variables x1 and x2. To ensure that this mismatch is robust, we establish perturbation stability results (Theorems 2.2, 2.6, 2.9) showing that each structural quantity is preserved at any fixed x under sufficiently small C2 perturbations of the lower-level data. Combined with the rigidity theorems, these stability results imply that the corresponding regularity condition must fail at some 

x for every problem in a C2-neighborhood of our counterexamples. The rigidity theorems also provide necessary conditions for the “every-x” requirement that serve as practical screening criteria. To verify whether the “every-x” requirement is achievable for a 7given problem, one can first examine the original (unperturbed) problem: if the relevant structural quantity, such as the stratification type of Y(x) or the number of local minimizers on each stratum, differs between two different points x1 and x2, then the corresponding regularity condition cannot hold at every x. Moreover, the stability results guarantee that this conclusion is robust: since these quantities do not change under small perturbations at each fixed x, the same obstruction persists for all nearby problems.

Under LICQ, the feasible set is an MGB whose natural stratification consists of three types of strata: the interior (points where no constraint is active), the boundary arcs (one-dimensional pieces where exactly one constraint is active), and the vertices (zero-dimensional points where exactly two constraints are active). In this example, there are one interior component, four boundary arcs, and four vertices. active constraint patterns together with connectivity: each stratum is a connected component of the set SJ (x) := {y : J (x, y ) = J } for some index set J , where J (x, y ) = {i : hi(x, y ) = 0 }. Two feasible points lie in the same stratum if and only if they have the same active index set and belong to the same connected component of SJ . For example, in Figure 1, the strata are the interior (J = ∅), the four boundary arcs (each with a distinct singleton J ), and the four vertices (each with a distinct two-element J ). Two MGBs are said to share the same stratification if there exists a diffeomorphism between them that maps each stratum of one onto a corresponding stratum of the other. We are now ready to state the rigidity theorem for LICQ. It says that if LICQ holds for the lower-level problem at every x, then the stratification of Y(x) cannot change as x varies. As a technical requirement, we assume X to be a connected MGB. Intuitively, “connected” means X

consists of a single piece rather than several disjoint components; the formal definition is recalled in Appendix A. This connectedness requirement is mild and is satisfied by most upper-level variable spaces arising in practice, such as boxes, balls, convex sets, or smooth manifolds with a single component. In particular, X is a connected MGB whenever it is defined by smooth inequality constraints satisfying LICQ over a connected set. 

Theorem 2.1 (Rigidity of stratification) . Suppose Assumption 2.1 holds, the upper-level feasible set 

X is a connected manifold with generalized boundary (MGB), and the lower-level feasible set Y(x)

is uniformly bounded. Furthermore, assume that, at every x ∈ X , LICQ holds for the lower-level problem. Then, for any x ∈ X , the lower-level feasible set Y(x) shares the same natural stratification as a manifold with generalized boundary (MGB). Specifically, for any x1, x 2 ∈ X , there exists a stratification-preserving diffeomorphism between Y(x1) and Y(x2).

The proof builds on Jongen et al. (2000, Theorem 10.4.5), which establishes the corresponding diffeomorphism result for feasible sets depending on a single parameter. Extending this to our multi-parameter setting requires the connected MGB structure of X : for any x1, x 2 ∈ X , we use local path-connectedness to connect them by a piecewise smooth path γ, apply the one-parameter result 10 on each smooth segment of γ, and compose the resulting diffeomorphisms to obtain a diffeomorphism between Y(x1) and Y(x2). The stratification preservation then follows from (Jongen et al., 2000, Corollary 3.1.29). The full proof is given in Appendix C.1. Theorem 2.1 establishes a necessary condition for the “every x” LICQ requirement: the stratifica-tion of Y(x) must remain invariant across all x ∈ X . To show that this requirement is non-prevalent, our strategy is to construct a specific problem where the stratification of Y(x) strictly differs between two upper-level variables x1 and x2. However, to ensure that the mismatch between x1

and x2 persists even after perturbing the lower-level constraints, we first need to establish that the stratification at any fixed upper-level variable x is stable under small perturbations. The following theorem provides exactly this stability guarantee.

## 2.3 Non-prevalence of SCSC 

Throughout this section and Section 2.4, to simplify the exposition, we treat each active-set manifold 

SJ (x) := {y : J (x, y ) = J } as a single stratum, rather than further refining it into connected components. This convention suffices for our counterexample constructions; the same arguments go through under the standard stratification by connected components. The rigidity theorem for LICQ (Theorem 2.1) concerns the topology of the feasible set: if LICQ holds at every x, the stratification of Y(x) is constant. We now show that adding SCSC imposes a further rigidity on the behavior of KKT points within this stratification. Specifically, if both LICQ and SCSC hold at every x, then no continuous trajectory of KKT points can move from one stratum to another as x varies. In other words, the active constraint pattern at any KKT point is locked in place. 

Theorem 2.5 (Rigidity of active set) . Suppose Assumption 2.1 holds, the upper-level feasible set X

is a connected manifold with generalized boundary (MGB), and the lower-level feasible set Y(x) is uniformly bounded for all x ∈ X . Furthermore, assume that, at every x ∈ X , LICQ and SCSC hold for the lower-level problem. Then, for any continuous trajectory of KKT points y∗(x) parameterized by x, the active constraint index set remains constant. Consequently, no KKT point can migrate across different strata of Y(x) as x varies. 

Theorem 2.5 shows that under the “every x” SCSC assumption, the active constraint pattern of a KKT point is invariant. The proof idea is as follows: along any continuous KKT trajectory, SCSC ensures that the active set is locally constant. By the connectedness of X , it is globally constant. The full proof is given in Appendix C.3. As in the LICQ case, we now turn to perturbation stability. Unlike the stratification in the LICQ case, the active constraint pattern at a KKT point is not stable under general perturbations, because perturbing the objective may destroy the KKT point itself. Stability can be restored if additional nondegeneracy conditions are imposed (such as the nonsingularity of the KKT matrix in Theorem 2.9). In the setting of strongly convex lower-level with LICQ and SCSC, the lower-level 14 problem has a unique global minimizer y∗(x) that persists under small perturbations, and the active constraint pattern at y∗(x) is stable, as the following theorem makes precise.

18 Remark 2.3 (Uncoupled constraints) . Since LICQ depends only on the constraints, when the constraints are uncoupled, i.e., when h(x, y ) = h(y) does not depend on x, the “every-x” version of LICQ is prevalent by Section 3.1. In contrast, the “every-x” version of SCSC and uniform SOSC remains non-prevalent even when the constraints are uncoupled: indeed, Counterexamples 2.7 and 2.10 both use constraints that do not depend on x, yet no sufficiently small C2 perturbation can make SCSC or uniform SOSC hold at every x.

In summary, Statement 1 is non-prevalent: for each condition, there exist robust counterexamples that no small perturbation can fix. Having established the non-prevalence of Statement 1, we now turn to its weaker counterpart Statement 2 and show in Section 3 that it is prevalent. 

# 3 Prevalence of LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC: “Almost-Every-x” Case 

In Section 2, we established that Statement 1 is non-prevalent: the “every x” requirement cannot be enforced by any sufficiently small C2 perturbation. We now turn to its weaker counterpart, Statement 2, and show that it is prevalent. To establish the prevalence of Statement 2, we fix a small positive constant ν > 0 and draw a = ( a1, . . . , a k) uniformly from [0 , ν ]k and b = ( b1, . . . , b m)uniformly from [0 , ν ]m. We then apply the following perturbation to the objective and constraint functions of the lower-level problem: 

eg(x, y ) = g(x, y ) + ⟨y, b ⟩, (3.1) 

ehi(x, y ) = hi(x, y ) + ai.

We impose the following assumption for the prevalence results in this section. 

Assumption 3.1. The following conditions hold. 1. The upper-level set X ⊂ Rn is Lebesgue measurable. Moreover, the functions g and hi are smooth (i.e., C∞) on an open neighborhood of X × Rm;2. The lower-level feasible set Y(x) := {y : hi(x, y ) ≤ 0, i = 1 , ..., k } satisfies a uniform Slater condition. Specifically, there exists a constant ρ > 0 such that, for any x ∈ X , there exists a point y in the interior of Y(x) satisfying hi(x, y ) ≤ − ρ for any i = 1 , ..., k .

The first assumption ensures that the measure-theoretic statements in this section are well defined. The smoothness on an open neighborhood of X is needed because Sard-type arguments require an open domain to define critical points and critical values; the resulting measure-zero conclusions on this neighborhood then restrict to measure-zero subsets of X . We assume C∞

smoothness for simplicity, although finite-order differentiability would suffice in principle. The second assumption guarantees that the lower-level feasible set remains nonempty under the constant perturbations in (3.1) : choosing ν < ρ ensures eY(x)̸ = ∅ for every x ∈ X . Both assumptions are mild. We now state the prevalence results for each of the three regularity conditions in turn. Unlike Section 2, where the non-prevalence of SCSC is analyzed under the assumption that LICQ holds, and the non-prevalence of SOSC is analyzed under the assumption that both LICQ and SCSC hold, here we establish the prevalence of LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC separately. This suffices because finite intersections of prevalent sets remain prevalent, so the three results together imply that, with probability one, a random perturbation makes LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC hold simultaneously at almost every x ∈ X .19 Remark 3.1. The perturbation scheme (3.1) restricts the parameters (b, a ) to the small box 

[0 , ν ]m × [0 , ν ]k. This formulation is slightly more restrictive than Definition A.1, which requires full Lebesgue measure on the entire finite-dimensional probe space 

P = {(b⊤y, a 1, . . . , a k) : ( b, a ) ∈ Rm × Rk}.

The proofs of Theorems 3.1–3.3 rely on Sard’s theorem and the parametric transversality theorem, and the exceptional parameter set has measure zero on every bounded box in P , regardless of its size. Taking a countable union over boxes yields measure zero on the whole probe space P , and hence gives prevalence in the sense of Definition A.1. We state the results in the bounded form only because it reflects the setting of interest: in applications, one only wants arbitrarily small perturbations of the lower-level objective and constraints, and the bound ν also keeps the perturbed feasible set eY(x)

nonempty under Assumption 3.1(2).

# 4 Gap Between “Almost-Every-x” and “Every-x”

In Section 2, we showed that Statement 1 is non-prevalent. In Section 3, we showed that Statement 2 is prevalent. Although these two statements differ only on a measure-zero set of upper-level variables, the gap between them is far from negligible. In this section, we examine what is lost when the regularity conditions fail on such a measure-zero set. The structural consequences depend on which condition fails and on the problem setting: 

• LICQ failure (Section 4.1): the Lagrange multiplier loses uniqueness and continuity. 

• SCSC failure under strongly convex lower-level (Section 4.2): the unique minimizer 

y∗(x) loses differentiability, turning the bilevel problem from a smooth into a nonsmooth optimization problem. 24 • SCSC or uniform SOSC failure under non-strongly-convex lower-level (Section 4.3): a branch of local minimizers can terminate abruptly, making the solution mapping x 7 → y∗(x)discontinuous. In each case, we also discuss the numerical consequences: the formulas (4.1) and (4.2) used for hypergradient computation become ill-conditioned not only at the failure points themselves, but throughout an entire neighborhood surrounding them. 

## 4.1 The gap caused by LICQ failure 

When LICQ holds for the lower-level problem at every x ∈ X , the Lagrange multiplier associated with any KKT point is well-behaved in the following sense. 

Proposition 4.1 (Uniqueness and continuity of Lagrange multipliers) . Suppose Assumption 2.1 holds, at every x ∈ X , LICQ holds for the lower-level problem. Then, for any KKT point (x, y ∗(x)) ,the associated multiplier λ(x) is unique. Moreover, for any continuous trajectory of KKT points 

(x, y ∗(x)) , the multiplier λ(x) is also continuous. 

The uniqueness of the multiplier is immediate from LICQ: the active constraint gradients are linearly independent, so the KKT system has a unique solution in λ. For the continuity, we refer the reader to Jiang et al. (2024b, Lemma B.2). However, the prevalence results of Section 3 only guarantee that LICQ holds at almost every x,leaving open the possibility of failure on a measure-zero set. By definition, the failure of LICQ at an upper-level variable x means there exists some lower-level feasible point y where the condition is violated. As discussed in Remark 2.2, this feasible point can in fact coincide with the lower-level minimizer y∗(x): there exist problems in which no sufficiently small perturbation can separate the minimizer from an LICQ failure point. Therefore, in the following discussion regarding the measure-zero set of x where LICQ fails, we specifically consider the case where this failure occurs at a local minimizer y∗(x). In both cases det ∇vG(x, v (x)) → 0 as x → 1, so ∥(∇vG(x, v (x))) −1∥ → ∞ .Therefore, although LICQ fails only on a measure-zero set of upper-level variables, its conse-quences extend to a neighborhood of positive measure around each failure point: both sensitivity formulas (4.1) and (4.2) are undefined at x0 itself and numerically unreliable nearby, the former due to active set misidentification and the latter due to ill-conditioning of ∇vG(x, v (x)). Consequently, any bilevel algorithm that relies on (4.1) (Khanduri et al., 2023; Xu and Zhu, 2023; Kornowski et al., 2024) or on (4.2) (Giovannelli et al., 2021) to compute the hypergradient suffers not just at the measure-zero failure set, but throughout an entire neighborhood surrounding it.

## 4.3 The gap caused by SCSC or uniform SOSC failure under non-strongly-convex lower-level 

In the non-strongly-convex setting, the lower-level problem may have multiple local minimizers. Thus, a bilevel objective defined through a selected local minimizer branch y∗(x) is well behaved only if this branch can be continued as x varies. We first state a positive result showing that LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC guarantee the global existence of such a smooth branch. This justifies the global smooth branch assumed in existing works such as Giovannelli et al. (2021); Nolasco et al. (2025). This conclusion is not an immediate consequence of the Implicit Function Theorem or Robinson-type stability results (Robinson, 1980), which only provide local continuation near a given point. 

Theorem 4.1 (Global smooth continuation of local minimizer branches) . Suppose Assumption 2.1 holds, and X is a connected, simply connected smooth manifold with generalized boundary. Suppose also that Y(x) is uniformly bounded for all x ∈ X . Furthermore, assume that, at every x ∈ X ,LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC hold for the lower-level problem. Then, for any x0 ∈ X and any local minimizer y0 of the lower-level problem at x0, there exists a unique global C∞ local-minimizer branch y∗ : X → Rm such that y∗(x0) = y0.

Here “Simply connected” means every loop in X can be continuously shrunk to a point. Formal definitions are given in Appendix A. Any convex subset of Rn satisfies this property. The proof proceeds in two steps. First, we establish a global continuous continuation of the local minimizer 

y0. Although the Implicit Function Theorem gives local continuations, the key difficulty is to assemble them into a globally well-defined map y∗ : X → Rm with y∗(x0) = y0, where y∗(x) is a local minimizer of the lower-level problem at every x ∈ X . We achieve this by showing that the projection from the graph of local minimizers to X is a covering map, and then invoking the homotopy lifting property: since X is simply connected, any two paths connecting the same endpoints lift to the same endpoint, ensuring the global continuation is well-defined. Second, we upgrade continuity to smoothness using the Implicit Function Theorem at each point. The full proof is given in Appendix C.11. Theorem 4.1 justifies the global smooth branch assumption implicit in Giovannelli et al. (2021); Nolasco et al. (2025): LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC at every x are sufficient. However, the prevalence results of Section 3 only guarantee that these conditions hold at almost every x. When either SCSC or uniform SOSC fails, the global continuation may break down: a branch of local minimizers can abruptly terminate at a failure point. ## 4.4 Discussion 

We conclude this section with two remarks that provide further perspective on the gap between the “almost-every-x” and “every-x” regularity requirements. 

The role of uniform quadratic growth. Theorem 4.1 shows that LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC together guarantee the global smooth continuation of each local minimizer branch. On the other hand, Examples 4.3 and 4.4 demonstrate that removing either SCSC or uniform SOSC can cause a local minimizer branch to terminate abruptly, destroying even continuity. At the local level, the mechanism behind continuation is clear: the Implicit Function Theorem, applied to the reduced KKT system, provides a locally unique smooth extension of each local minimizer whenever the KKT Jacobian M+(x) is nonsingular (which requires LICQ and SOSC) and the active set is constant (which requires SCSC). But why can these local extensions be assembled into a global one? And why does global continuation break down when any of the three conditions is removed? The answer lies in a consequence that LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC jointly imply: uniform local quadratic growth. 

Theorem 4.2 (Uniform local quadratic growth) . Suppose Assumption 2.1 holds, the upper-level variable space X is a compact set, and the lower-level feasible set Y(x) is uniformly bounded for all 

x ∈ X . Furthermore, assume that the objective g and constraint functions hi are twice continuously differentiable, and at every x ∈ X , LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC hold for the lower-level problem. Then, the local minimizers satisfy a uniform local quadratic growth condition over X : there exist uniform constants c > 0 and δ > 0 such that for any x ∈ X and any local minimizer y∗(x) at x, it holds that 

g(x, y ) ≥ g(x, y ∗(x)) + c∥y − y∗(x)∥2, ∀ y ∈ Y (x) ∩ Bδ(y∗(x)) .

Uniform quadratic growth means that every local minimizer sits at the bottom of a quadratic bowl whose depth and width are bounded below uniformly over x. This prevents local minimizers from degenerating as x varies: no matter how far the upper-level variable moves, every local minimizer retains a definite gap in objective value from all nearby feasible points. It is this uniform gap that allows the local extensions provided by the Implicit Function Theorem to be assembled into a global continuation. It is worth noting a distinction between the single-level and the parametric (bilevel) settings. In single-level constrained optimization, SOSC at a KKT point directly implies local quadratic growth at that point; this is a standard result in nonlinear programming (Shapiro, 1992). One might therefore expect that in the parametric setting, imposing LICQ and uniform SOSC at every x would suffice to guarantee uniform local quadratic growth. However, this is not the case: SCSC is also indispensable. The reason is that SOSC controls curvature only within the critical cone, while the 32 local quadratic growth in directions outside the critical cone is maintained by the strict separation between active and inactive constraints. More precisely, uniform quadratic growth requires a uniform constant c > 0 and radius δ > 0 independent of x. Each condition controls a different component: 

• Uniform SOSC provides a uniform lower bound on the curvature of the Lagrangian Hessian within the critical cone. This lower bound contributes to both the quadratic growth constant 

c and the uniform radius δ. When it fails, this curvature approaches zero, and the constants in the quadratic growth estimate degenerate. This permits a bifurcation (Example 4.4). 

• SCSC, together with the compactness of X , provides a uniform lower bound on the multipliers at active constraints. This uniform gap ensures growth in directions outside the critical cone and also contributes to both the quadratic growth constant c and the uniform radius δ. When SCSC fails at some x0, the multiplier λi(x0) = 0 for some active constraint, and the constants 

c and δ in the quadratic growth estimate may degenerate as x → x0. At x0 itself, the critical cone expands to include directions with negative curvature, destroying quadratic growth entirely and causing the local minimizer to degenerate into a saddle point (Example 4.3). In summary, LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC are not merely independent technical assumptions: they work in concert to establish uniform quadratic growth, which is the structural foundation underlying the continuity of local minimizer branches. This stands in contrast to the single-level setting, where SOSC alone suffices for local quadratic growth, and highlights an intrinsic difficulty of bilevel optimization with constrained lower-level problems. 

Pointwise SOSC versus uniform SOSC. In Section 3, we showed that pointwise SOSC at almost every x is prevalent under finite-dimensional perturbations. In Section 2, we showed that uniform SOSC at every x is non-prevalent. A natural intermediate question is whether pointwise SOSC at every x is prevalent. We leave this as an open question, but argue that its resolution is not essential for the bilevel optimization setting. The reason is that, for bilevel optimization, pointwise SOSC at every x is insufficient to guarantee the well-behavedness of the solution mapping or the hyperfunction. As demonstrated by Example 4.4, even when LICQ and SCSC hold everywhere and pointwise SOSC holds at every local minimizer for every x > 0, the lack of a uniform lower bound on the second-order modulus permits a saddle-node bifurcation at x = 0, causing a local minimizer branch to disappear. Consequently, the solution mapping x 7 → y∗(x) is discontinuous and the hyperfunction x 7 → f (x, y ∗(x)) is ill-defined at the bifurcation point. This observation highlights that pointwise SOSC at every x is still insufficient for the structural properties established in Sections 4.3 and 4.4. In particular, it does not ensure a uniform local quadratic growth estimate, and consequently it cannot prevent a local minimizer branch from terminating via a saddle-node bifurcation (Example 4.4). Once such a termination occurs, the solution mapping x 7 → y∗(x) and the hyperfunction x 7 → f (x, y ∗(x)) along this branch lose continuity, even though SOSC holds pointwise away from the bifurcation point. Note that SOSC is by definition only imposed at local minimizers, and the bifurcation point itself has degenerated into a saddle point, so SOSC is not required to hold there. Therefore, while the prevalence of pointwise SOSC at every x is of independent mathematical interest, it does not address the specific role of uniform SOSC in the bilevel setting, which is to ensure the global smooth continuation of local minimizer branches and the continuity of the associated hyperfunction. 33 5 Conclusion 

In this work, we studied the strength and limitations of the regularity assumptions (LICQ, SCSC, and SOSC) commonly adopted in bilevel optimization with constrained lower-level problems. We showed that the “every x” versions are strong assumptions in the sense of non-prevalence. We established rigidity theorems identifying structural invariants that must be preserved when these conditions hold everywhere, including the stratification of the feasible set, the active constraint pattern along KKT trajectories, and the stratum-wise count of local minimizers. We then constructed robust counterexamples certifying the failure of each condition. In contrast, the “almost every x” versions of these conditions are weak assumptions in the sense of prevalence: they hold with probability one after arbitrarily small perturbations of the lower-level objective and constraints. We further analyzed the gap between the two requirements: even though the “every x” and “almost every 

x” versions differ only on a measure-zero set, this difference can cause the Lagrange multiplier to lose uniqueness, the hyperfunction to lose differentiability, or a branch of local minimizers to terminate abruptly. In all cases, the sensitivity formulas used for hypergradient computation become ill-conditioned in an entire neighborhood of each failure point. Finally, we showed that LICQ, SCSC, and uniform SOSC jointly imply uniform local quadratic growth, which serves as the structural foundation for the global smooth continuation of local minimizer branches.

## Sard’s Theorem and Transversality 

Theorem A.1 (Sard’s Theorem) . Let f : Rp → Rq be Cl, where l ≥ max {p − q + 1 , 1}. Let X ⊂ Rp

denote the critical set of f , which is the set of points x ∈ Rp at which the Jacobian matrix of f has rank < q . Then the critical value set, that is, the image f (X), has Lebesgue measure 0 in Rq.

Definition A.2 (Transversality) . Let f : X → Y be a smooth map between smooth manifolds, and let Z be a submanifold of Y . We say that f is transverse to Z, denoted f ⋔ Z, if for every 

x ∈ f −1(Z),

im( df x) + Tf (x)Z = Tf (x)Y, 

where Tf (x)Z and Tf (x)Y are the tangent spaces of Z and Y at f (x), and im (df x) is the image of 

df x : TxX → Tf (x)Y .

Transversality formalizes the notion that f intersects Z in the most generic way possible. In particular, if dim X < codim Z, then f ⋔ Z implies f −1(Z) = ∅.

Theorem A.2 (Parametric Transversality Theorem) . Let F : X × S → Y be a smooth map of manifolds, and let Z be a submanifold of Y . If F ⋔ Z, then for almost every s ∈ S, the slice map 

fs(·) := F (·, s ) satisfies fs ⋔ Z.

37 Connectedness, path-connectedness, and simple connectivity 

We recall the topological notions used in the requirements of Theorems 2.1, 2.5, 2.8, and 4.1. Throughout, X denotes a topological space. 

Definition A.3 (Connected space) . X is connected if it cannot be written as a disjoint union 

X = U ⊔ V of two nonempty open subsets. Equivalently, the only subsets of X that are both open and closed are ∅ and X itself. 

Definition A.4 (Path-connected space) . X is path-connected if for any two points x0, x 1 ∈ X,there exists a continuous map γ : [0 , 1] → X with γ(0) = x0 and γ(1) = x1. Such a map is called a path from x0 to x1.

Definition A.5 (Locally path-connected space) . X is locally path-connected if every point x ∈ X has a neighborhood basis of path-connected open sets; that is, for any x ∈ X and any open neighborhood 

U of x, there exists a path-connected open set V with x ∈ V ⊆ U .

Path-connectedness implies connectedness, but the converse is false in general. For example, the closure of the topologist’s sine curve is connected but not path-connected. However, when X

is locally path-connected, the two notions coincide: a connected, locally path-connected space is automatically path-connected. This equivalence is used in the proof of Theorem 2.1: since any MGB is locally path-connected, the assumption that X is a connected MGB yields path-connectedness for free. 

Definition A.6 (Homotopy of paths relative to endpoints) . Two paths γ0, γ 1 : [0 , 1] → X sharing the same endpoints γ0(0) = γ1(0) = x0 and γ0(1) = γ1(1) = x1 are homotopic relative to endpoints if there exists a continuous map H : [0 , 1] × [0 , 1] → X satisfying H(s, 0) = γ0(s), H(s, 1) = γ1(s),and H(0 , t ) = x0, H(1 , t ) = x1 for all s, t ∈ [0 , 1] .

Definition A.7 (Simply connected space) . A path-connected space X is simply connected if every loop in X can be continuously contracted to a point: for any continuous map γ : [0 , 1] → X with 

γ(0) = γ(1) = x0, the map γ is homotopic relative to endpoints to the constant map s 7 → x0.

Equivalently, a path-connected space is simply connected if and only if any two paths with the same endpoints are homotopic relative to endpoints. This equivalent formulation is the form used in the proof of Lemma C.3: when continuing a branch of local minimizers along two different paths joining the same pair of upper-level variables, simple connectivity guarantees that the two resulting continuations agree, so the global branch is single-valued. 

## Manifolds with generalized boundary 

Definition A.8 (Manifold with generalized boundary (Jongen et al., 2000, Definition 3.1.9)) . Asubset X of Rn is called a manifold with generalized boundary (MGB) of class Cr if, for each x ∈ X,there exists a local Cr-coordinate system of Rn, say ψ : U → V , with ψ(x) = 0 , such that 

ψ(U ∩ X) = {(v1, . . . , v n) ∈ V | v1 = · · · = vq = 0 , v q+1 ≥ 0, . . . , v q+p ≥ 0}.

Here q = q(x) and p = p(x) may depend on x. The coordinates vq+1 , . . . , v q+p are called semifree, and vq+p+1 , . . . , v n are called free. 

Definition A.9 (Dimension and strata (Jongen et al., 2000, Definition 3.1.16)) . Let X be an MGB in Rn of dimension s, that is, q(x) = n − s for all x ∈ X. For 0 ≤ ℓ ≤ s, define 

Σℓ = {x ∈ X | p(x) = s − ℓ}.

Each path-component of Σℓ is called an ℓ-dimensional stratum of X.

## Covering maps 

The following notions from algebraic topology are used in the proof of Theorem 4.1 to establish the global uniqueness of the continuation map. 

Definition A.11 (Covering map) . Let p : E → B be a continuous surjection between topological spaces. The map p is called a covering map if every point b ∈ B has an open neighborhood U such that p−1(U ) is a disjoint union of open sets in E, each of which is mapped homeomorphically onto 

U by p.

We will use the following standard fact from point-set topology. 

Lemma A.1 (Tube lemma) . Let X and Y be topological spaces with Y compact. If N is an open subset of X ×Y containing the slice {x0}× Y for some x0 ∈ X, then there exists an open neighborhood 

W of x0 in X such that W × Y ⊂ N . As a direct consequence, the projection πX : X × Y → X is a closed map. 

A continuous map p : E → B is called proper if the preimage p−1(C) of every compact set 

C ⊂ B is compact. The following result is well known in point-set topology. 

Proposition A.1 (Proper local homeomorphism implies covering map (Ho, 1975, Lemma 2)) . Let 

p : E → B be a surjective proper local homeomorphism between locally compact Hausdorff spaces. Then p is a covering map. 

The key consequence we use is the following lifting property. 

Theorem A.3 (Homotopy lifting property (Hatcher, 2002, Proposition 1.30)) . Let p : E → B be a covering map. Given a homotopy H : [0 , 1] × [0 , 1] → B and a lift eH(·, 0) : [0 , 1] → E of the initial path (i.e., p ◦ eH(·, 0) = H(·, 0) ), there exists a unique continuous lift eH : [0 , 1] × [0 , 1] → E such that p ◦ eH = H.

# B Localized Form of the Counterexample Arguments 

In Section 2, Counterexamples 2.3, 2.4, 2.7, and 2.10 are presented without repeatedly displaying the compact sets Kin and Kout . In this appendix, we provide the localized version of the same argument. 1. Choice of compact sets. For each counterexample, we can choose compact sets Kin ⊂ int (Kout )such that the lower-level feasible sets are uniformly contained in int( Kin ), namely, 

Y(x) ⊂ int( Kin ), ∀x ∈ X .

We then compare the relevant invariant inside Kin . The goal is to show that no sufficiently small perturbation can make the Kin -localized analogue of Statement 1 hold: the corresponding regularity condition cannot hold for the lower-level problem at every x when restricted to Kin .Here the restriction to Kin is only a localization of the analysis, not an additional constraint in the lower-level problem. 39 2. Endpoint stability. The compact-local stability result is applied only at the two endpoint parameters x1 and x2 used in the counterexample. It gives two perturbation thresholds, and we choose the smaller one. Then, under any perturbation below this size, the relevant invariant inside Kin is preserved at both endpoints. Therefore the endpoint mismatch of this invariant remains after perturbation. 3. Localization by cutoff. To apply the rigidity theorem, we use a smooth cutoff function which equals one on a neighborhood of Kin and vanishes outside Kout . The cutoff problem agrees with the original perturbed problem on Kin . Moreover, by choosing the perturbation sufficiently small, the cutoff perturbed feasible set remains inside int (Kin ) for every x. Thus the regularity condition inside Kin for the original perturbed problem is the same as the global regularity condition for the cutoff perturbed problem. 4. Contradiction with rigidity. If the Kin -localized analogue of Statement 1 held after perturbation, then the cutoff perturbed problem would satisfy the corresponding every-x regularity condition globally. The rigidity theorem would then force the endpoint invariants at x1 and x2 to be identical. This contradicts the endpoint mismatch preserved by the stability result. Hence the regularity condition must fail for some x inside Kin , and this failure is also a failure for the original perturbed problem.
