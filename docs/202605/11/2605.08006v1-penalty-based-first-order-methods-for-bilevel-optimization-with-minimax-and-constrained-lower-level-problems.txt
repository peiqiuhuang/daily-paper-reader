## Abstract 

We study a class of bilevel optimization problems in which both the upper- and lower-level problems have minimax structures. This setting captures a broad range of emerging applications. Despite the extensive literature on bilevel optimization and minimax optimization separately, existing methods mainly focus on bilevel optimization with lower-level minimization problems, often under strong convexity assumptions, and are not directly applicable to the minimax lower-level setting considered here. To address this gap, we develop penalty-based first-order methods for bilevel minimax optimization without requiring strong convexity of the lower-level problem. In the deterministic setting, we establish that the proposed method finds an ŌĶ-KKT point with ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí4) oracle complexity. We further show that bilevel problems with convex constrained lower-level minimization can be reformulated as special cases of our framework via Lagrangian duality, leading to an ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí4)

complexity bound that improves upon the existing ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí7) result. Finally, we extend our approach to the stochastic setting, where only stochastic gradient oracles are available, and prove that the proposed stochastic method finds a nearly ŌĶ-KKT point with ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí9) oracle complexity.

Bilevel optimization has found numerous applications in machine learning, including but not limited to hyperparameter tuning [ 4, 16 ], meta-learning [ 16 , 5 , 59 ], reinforcement learning [ 23 , 71 , 40 ], and neural architecture search [ 44 ]. On the other hand, minimax optimization problems also arise in a wide range of applications, such as robust learning [ 13 ], distributionally robust optimization [ 3], generative adversarial networks [ 19 ], and adversarial training [ 54 ]. Incorporating such minimax problems as the lower-level problem in bilevel optimization leads to a broad class of novel and practically relevant applications of (1) . We will present the examples of (1) from hyperparameter tuning for distributionally robust deep learning in Section 7 with numerical results. Additional applications of (1) are given in Section A. Despite the extensive literature on both bilevel optimization and minimax optimization, existing bilevel formulations typically assume that the lower-level problem is a single-objective minimization problem (often strongly convex), with or without constraints. The setting where the lower-level problem itself is a minimax problem remains largely unexplored. To fill this gap, this work proposes both deterministic and stochastic first-order algorithms for (1) based on a value-function penalty reformulation. Such reformulation has been studied for bilevel optimization with a lower-level minimization problem [ 62 , 72 , 73 , 30 , 38 , 39 , 8, 53 , 51 ] but has not been studied for (1) . We establish the (stochastic) gradient oracle complexity of the proposed methods for finding an ŌĶ-KKT point of (1) (see Definition 3.2) in the deterministic case and for finding a nearly ŌĶ-KKT point of (1) (see Definition 6.2) in the stochastic case. Furthermore, we consider a bilevel optimization problem in which the lower-level problem is a convex minimization problem with convex nonlinear inequality constraints. ## 2 Related works 

Most first-order methods for bilevel optimization focus on the smooth setting in which the lower-level problem is a strongly convex constrained or unconstrained minimization problem. Under this assumption (and constraint qualification in the constrained case), the lower-level solution is a single-valued and differentiable mapping of the upper-level solution, and the gradient of the outer objective can be computed through the chain rule, the implicit function theorem and Danskin‚Äôs theorem. This leads to the class of hypergradient or implicit-gradient methods [ 58 , 18 , 59 , 20 , 29 , 70 , 21 , 9, 65 , 28 ,11 , 10 , 41 , 33 , 67 , 47 , 69 , 24 , 23 , 39 , 8, 30 ]. Their assumptions exclude applications in which the lower-level problem is merely convex and has multiple solutions. Several recent works have replaced the lower-level strong convexity assumption to other assumptions, such as the Polyak-ŇĀojasiewicz condition, or consider an optimization framework without using a hypergradient, for example, the penalty-based, value-function-based, and optimality-condition-based approaches [ 45 , 48 , 46 , 2, 49 , 7, 64 , 43 , 38 , 68 , 26 , 27 , 25 ]. Shen and Chen [62] , Shen et al. [63] consider a generic penalty method when the lower-level optimal set allows a squared-distance bound function. These methods can be applied to bilevel optimization with merely convex or non-convex lower-level objectives, but not directly to (1) whose lower-level problem has a minimax structure. More recently, Lu and Mei [51] proposed a first-order penalty framework that solves bilevel optimiza-tion through a minimax reformulation. Their method finds an ŌĶ-KKT point of (4) with complexity 

ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí4) and ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí7) when the lower-level problem is unconstrained and constrained, respectively. By a similar penalty approach, Lu and Mei [53] developed a sequential minimax optimization method that reduces the complexity to ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí6) when the lower-level problem is constrained but has a strongly convex objective function. Our work is motivated by Lu and Mei [51 , 53] , but studies a substantially more general bilevel minimax structure in (1) , which covers their problem as a special case. When the lower-level problem is unconstrained, we obtain the same ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí4) complexity for finding an ŌĶ-KKT point, but improve their complexity from ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí7) and ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí6) to ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí4) when there are constraints. Ouattara and Aswani [57] , Jiang et al. [30] , Nie et al. [56] study bilevel problems in which the lower-level problem involves nonlinear constraints, and reformulate the bilevel problem as a single-level problem via Lagrangian duality in a way similar to our approach. Ouattara and Aswani [57] do not provide a complexity analysis, however. Jiang et al. [30] and Nie et al. [56] consider a penalty formulation of the single-level problem, but require strong convexity of the lower-level problem to ensure uniqueness of the lower-level solution; this assumption is not needed in our work. Yao et al. [73] propose a related reformulation based on a proximal Lagrangian value function and, similarly to our approach, impose a box constraint on the dual variable. However, their algorithm requires projection onto the constraint set, which can be computationally inefficient. In [ 72 ], the authors propose a single-level reformulation by expressing the lower-level constrained problem as a minimax problem and incorporating it as a penalty term. This approach is similar to ours, but they introduce additional proximal terms into the minimax formulation to ensure smoothness of the reformulated problem. Moreover, their convergence analysis is established in terms of a stationarity measure for the penalty problem, without relating it to a stationarity measure of the original bilevel problem. In addition, their method does not guarantee ŌĶ-feasibility, while ours does. In [ 72 ][Section 5], the authors mention that their approach can be extended to (1) but without providing an anaysis. Finally, Khanduri et al. [34] , Kornowski et al. [36] , Tsaknakis et al. [65] study bilevel problems in which the lower-level constraints are linear, whereas we allow general nonlinear convex constraints. Li et al. [42] , Allende and Still [1] consider reformulations of (4) based on Wolfe duality and the KKT conditions of the lower-level problem, respectively, which can accommodate general lower-level constraints. Ye et al. [74] and Gao et al. [17] adopt value-function-based reformulations and solve (4) using techniques from difference-of-convex optimization.. However, these works do not provide complexity guarantees for finding an ŌĶ-KKT point. Recent works by Khalafi and Boob [32] , Dvurechensky et al. [14] study stochastic bilevel variational inequality problems. Although 3minimax problems can be formulated as variational inequality problems, their models use the same decision variables at the upper and lower levels, which is substantially different from our setting.

## 8 Limitations 

This work has the following limitations: (1) Assumption 5.1 requires compactness of the feasible domains. (2) The ŌĶ-KKT point in Definition 3.2 is defined in a lifted space with additional variables 

z1 and z3. It would be more desirable to consider a stationarity notation that only involves the original variables. (3) In the stochastic case, the ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí9) complexity is high. We believe using the variance-reduction technique can reduce this complexity, which we leave it as a future work. 99 Conclusion 

We studied bilevel optimization problems in which both the upper- and lower-level problems exhibit minimax structures. We proposed a penalty-based first-order method which finds an ŌĶ-KKT point with 

ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí4) complexity in the deterministic case and finds a nearly ŌĶ-KKT point with ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí9) complexity in the stochastic case. When applied to deterministic bilevel problems with convex constrained lower-level minimization, the proposed framework improves the existing complexity bound from 

ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí7) in [51] and ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí6) in [53] to ňúO(ŌĶ‚ąí4).10 References

## I Additional details on numerical experiments 

Synthetic experiments are run on Intel Xeon Gold 6438Y+, and deep DRO experiments are run on one H100 GPU. 

I.1 Constrained Linear Optimization I.1.1 Instance generation 

We first randomly generate c and d with all the entries independently chosen from N (0 , 1) . We then randomly generate eA and eB with all the entries independently chosen from N (0 , 0.01) . In addition, we randomly generate ňÜy ‚ąą [‚ąí1, 1] m with all the entries independently chosen from N (0 , 0.1) and then projected to [‚ąí1, 1] m and choose ňúd and ňúb such that ňÜy is an optimal solution of the lower level optimization of Problem (26) with x = 0 and y = 0 . Occasionally, generated instance cannot converge for SMO, so we only take into account of instances where all four methods converge for fair comparison. 

I.1.2 Hyperparameters 

Hyperparameters of our baselines are based exactly on the description of Lu and Mei [53 , Section 3 ] .It is worth mentioning that for the FOP baseline, they did not implement the pseudocode of Lu and Mei [51 , Algorithm 2 ] but instead used a practical variant with a schedule for ( őĶk, ŌĀ k). We similarly implemented the variant for the FOP baseline. For all methods, Algorithm 2 is run for a maximum of 200 iterations. For our method, we used a Lagrange bound of B = 200 .

I.2 DRO with hyperparameter tuning I.2.1 Datasets 

We use the exact same split for both datasets as provided by Sagawa et al. [61] . The primary task of the target label prediction is severely confounded by a spurious attribute (See Table 3). In the CelebA 32 dataset, standard ERM models tend to exploit the spurious correlation, effectively using gender as a proxy for hair color to minimize average loss. This shortcut learning leads to disastrous performance on minority group, i.e., blond male [ 61 ]. To show the severity of this issue along the same strand, CUB images [ 66 ] are overlaid on top of Places images [ 77 ] with imbalanced proportions to create the Waterbirds dataset as done by Sagawa et al. [61] , where the labels are the type of the bird (waterbirds vs. landbirds), confounded by the background (water vs. land). Because the data is defined by the intersection of the target label and the spurious attribute, it naturally partitions into G = 4 predefined groups. With deep learning paradigms, we use the proposed stochastic bilevel optimization from Section 6.
