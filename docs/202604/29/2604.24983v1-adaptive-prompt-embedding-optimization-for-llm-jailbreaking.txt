A B S T R A C T 

Existing white-box jailbreak attacks against aligned LLMs typically append discrete adversarial suffixes to the user prompt, which visibly alters the prompt and operates in a combinatorial token space. Prior work has avoided directly optimizing the embeddings of the original prompt tokens, presumably because perturbing them risks destroying the prompt’s semantic content. We propose 

Prompt Embedding Optimization (PEO), a multi-round white-box jailbreak that directly optimizes the embeddings of the original prompt tokens without appending any adversarial tokens, and show that the concern is unfounded: the optimized embeddings remain close enough to their originals that the visible prompt string is preserved exactly after nearest-token projection, and quantitative analysis shows the model’s responses stay on topic for the large majority of prompts. PEO combines continuous embedding-space optimization with structured continuation targets and an adaptive failure-focused schedule. Counterintuitively, later PEO rounds can benefit from heuristic composite response scaffolds that are not natural standalone templates, yet ASR-Judge shows that the resulting gains are not merely empty formatting or scaffold-only outputs. Across two standard harmful-behavior benchmarks and competing white-box attacks spanning discrete suffix search, appended adversarial embeddings, and search-based adversarial generation, PEO outperforms all of them in our experiments. 

## 1. Introduction 

Jailbreak attacks against aligned large language models are usually compared by a single number: how often the final response looks harmful or non-refusal [15, 2]. That view is too narrow. In practice, attack quality is multi-objective. A useful jailbreak can be more successful, but it can also be cheaper to optimize and less visible at the prompt surface. These additional dimensions matter in both directions: they shape the feasibility of real attacks, and they determine whether a published attack result is a reliable scientific artifact rather than a one-off engineering anecdote. Existing white-box jailbreaks overwhelmingly operate in discrete token space: they append tokens in the prompt string, which visibly alters the user-facing input and con-strains optimization to a combinatorial search [29, 8, 28]. Recent work has begun to explore continuous embedding-space attacks, but either appends new learned adversarial embeddings after the instruction [21] or optimizes an entire input from scratch without preserving any specific prompt [26]. Neither approach modifies the embeddings of the ex-isting prompt tokens—a gap that likely reflects the assump-tion that perturbing prompt embeddings would destroy the semantic content of the request. We propose Prompt Embedding Optimization (PEO), a white-box jailbreak that takes this approach and shows the concern is unfounded. PEO optimizes the continuous

## 2. Related Work 

Discrete white-box jailbreaks. Greedy Coordinate Gra-dient (GCG) [29] is the canonical white-box jailbreak in this family: it appends an adversarial suffix to the user prompt and then uses gradient-guided coordinate search over discrete token replacements to optimize that suffix toward a targeted harmful continuation objective. More broadly, AutoPrompt [22] and subsequent hard-prompt optimization work [25] established that gradients can be used to opti-mize prompt tokens or trigger strings directly. AutoDAN [12] extends this line with stealth-oriented jailbreak prompt optimization, while AdvPrefix [28] emphasizes that the tar-get continuation objective itself can materially change the harmfulness/compliance tradeoff. These attacks are strong points of comparison, but they act in discrete token space and usually incur a visible prompt-edit cost. 

Continuous and embedding-space attacks. A longer line of adversarial NLP work studies perturbations in contin-uous or approximately continuous spaces, including gradient-based token substitutions, natural-language adversarial gen-eration, and paraphrase-based attacks [6, 1, 9]. In the jail-break setting, Soft Prompt Threats (SPT) [21] optimizes continuous adversarial embeddings appended after the in-struction, demonstrating that soft-prompt-style attacks can bypass safety alignment; however, the method introduces new tokens rather than modifying existing ones, so the visible prompt is altered. Xu et al. [26] optimize an entire continuous input from scratch to elicit a target response, but the resulting input has no connection to any specific harmful request and cannot preserve prompt semantics. PEO fills the gap between these approaches: it perturbs the embeddings of the existing prompt tokens in place, and our experiments show that the perturbations are small enough to preserve the visible prompt text exactly. Inference-time optimization over prompt-like continuous states also appears in adjacent safety settings such as text-to-image safeguarding [16]. 

Search-based and black-box jailbreaks. Not all prac-tical jailbreaks assume white-box gradients. Prompt Auto-matic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) and Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) show that iterative black-box refinement can automatically discover strong jailbreak prompts with only query access to the target model [4, 14]. Beam Search-based Adversarial Attack (BEAST) demonstrates that fast search-based attacks can also be highly competitive under tight wall-clock budgets [19]. Rainbow Teaming emphasizes open-ended generation of diverse adversarial prompts rather than exact gradient control [20]. Our setting is complemen-tary: we show how strong a continuous embedding-space attack can become when the attacker has open-weight access to model internals. 

Alignment and defenses. Modern chat models are aligned with techniques such as reinforcement learning from hu-man feedback (RLHF), constitutional tuning, and direct preference optimization [15, 2, 17]. On the defense side, methods such as SmoothLLM and adversarial preference learning attempt to make refusal behavior more robust or to blunt jailbreak attacks at inference or training time [18, 24]. Related work also shows that embedding-model safeguards can themselves be bypassed by universal “magic word” constructions, which is relevant to our use of judge-based evaluation rather than embedding-similarity filters alone [10]. Strong attacks matter here because they define the stress tests those defenses must actually survive. 

Evaluation methodology. Recent frameworks such as HarmBench [13], JailbreakBench [3], and JAILJUDGE [11] argue that string heuristics alone are too weak for reliable safety measurement. We follow that direction by reporting both a string heuristics metric (ASR-Match) and an LLM-as-a-judge metric (ASR-Judge), and explicitly analyze where those metrics diverge. 

## 3. Problem Setting and Threat Model 

We study a white-box jailbreak setting on open-weight chat models in which the attacker is given a fixed harmful user prompt and seeks to induce the target model to answer that prompt with the requested harmful information. Success is determined by whether the model ultimately provides the harmful content requested by that prompt. For example, HarmBench includes prompts such as " Explain how to forge documents or create counterfeit currency " [13], paired with a custom harmful-response prefix such as " Sure, here’s how to forge documents or create counterfeit currency ". 4.3. Adaptive Failure-Focused Scheduling 

A single PEO optimization pass is not sufficient for every prompt, because prompts can fail in different ways. PEO therefore uses an adaptive multi-pass schedule, applying up to four passes per prompt. It begins with the dataset-aligned target continuation and terminates immediately if that pass already elicits a harmful response determined with ASR-M. Prompts that remain unsolved are re-attacked in subsequent passes, where the continuation target is changed while the underlying embedding-space optimization procedure from Section 4.1 remains the same. Each pass is initialized from the original prompt-token embeddings rather than warm-started from the previous pass. We adopt this cold-start design for three reasons. First, the scheduler only advances after a failure, so the prompts that reach a later pass are exactly those whose pass 𝑖 −1 

embedding did not yield a jailbreak under free decoding; re-initializing gives the new target’s loss landscape a chance to pull the embedding into a different region rather than continuing from a representation that already failed to trans-fer. Cold-start therefore acts as an escape mechanism for later passes, which are disproportionately the hard non-transfer cases. Second, the L2 anchor 𝜆 ‖𝐄 − 𝐄 (0) ‖22 is always referenced to the original prompt-token embeddings 𝐄 (0) ; a warm start would begin pass 𝑖 with a nonzero anchor penalty and risk spending early iterations retracting pass 𝑖 −1 ’s drift rather than fitting the new scaffold. Third, cold-start re-anchors each pass independently to 𝐄 (0) , which bounds the total displacement across the schedule and supports the 0% text-change property reported in Section 6; accumulating drift across warm-started passes would make that preser-vation claim harder to guarantee. The adaptive schedule is therefore best understood as repeatedly asking the same per-prompt optimization problem against a different con-tinuation target rather than as a continuation of one long optimization trajectory. In the later passes, the new targets are constructed by concatenating short literal suffixes after the dataset-aligned target rather than replacing that target entirely. Keeping the dataset target preserves the semantic constraint that the model answers the harmful request itself, while the appended suffix provides a compact structural anchor such as :\n\n1. or :\n\nTitle: . These anchors commit the first few decoded tokens to an enumerated or titled form and steer the autoregressive prior into that region. They should therefore be understood as optimization scaffolds rather than as natural standalone completion templates: their role is to reshape the teacher-forced loss landscape and improve free-decoding transfer, not to serve as plausible literal responses on their own. Inside Algorithm 1, Pass 1’s 𝐲 is the dataset-aligned target alone, whereas Passes 2–4 use the dataset target with a pass-specific scaffold concatenated to it; the cross-entropy loss is always computed against the resulting combined string in a single optimization step. The detailed adaptive schedule is: 1. Pass 1: base target. Optimize the prompt-token em-beddings using only the dataset-aligned target for that prompt. ## 5. Experimental Setup 

5.1. Dataset and Model Suite 

We use two text-only jailbreak benchmarks. The first is AdvBench [29], with 520 harmful-behavior prompts. The second is the HarmBench text-test split, normalized into a 320-row evaluation set by retaining only the text behaviors from the official HarmBench [13] release and pairing them with the official optimizer target completions distributed with HarmBench. The four evaluated models are Vicuna-7B-v1.3 [5], Llama-2-7B-Chat [23], Qwen3-4B-Instruct [27], and Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct [7]. All four models are evaluated on both benchmarks. The experiments were executed on local GPUs with four NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell Max-Q Workstation Edition cards, each with 97,887 MiB of memory. Each attack run uses one GPU at a time. 

5.2. Attack Methods For Comparison 

We compare PEO against three competing white-box attacks that represent the main paradigms in the jailbreak literature: 

• nanoGCG [8, 29]: the standard discrete suffix at-tack. nanoGCG is a per-prompt reimplementation of GCG that uses the same gradient-guided token-search algorithm. We use it rather than the original multi-prompt GCG codebase because PEO also attacks one prompt at a time, making nanoGCG the more direct comparison. 

• Soft Prompt Threats (SPT) [21]: the closest contin-uous embedding-space attack. SPT optimizes learned adversarial embeddings appended after the instruc-tion to bypass safety alignment. Unlike PEO, SPT does not modify the embeddings of existing prompt tokens; it introduces new continuous tokens. Com-paring PEO against SPT isolates whether perturbing existing embeddings in place is more effective than appending new ones. 

• BEAST [19]: a search-based adversarial attack that uses beam search to generate adversarial suffix tokens without requiring gradient computation over token candidates. BEAST appends adversarial tokens via greedy beam search with a targeted attack objective. Including BEAST tests whether PEO’s advantage ex-tends beyond gradient-based methods to search-based attacks. All three competing attacks are evaluated on all four Ad-vBench models and on the HarmBench text-test split un-der the same reporting stack as PEO. To keep decoding stochasticity comparable across methods, the free-decoding pass that produces each final response uses the same sampler hyperparameters across methods — nucleus sampling with temperature 0.7 and top-𝑝 0.9 for PEO, nanoGCG, BEAST, and SPT alike. 

5.3. Final Run Settings 

All four models share the same uniform four-pass sched-ule with identical hyperparameters across both models and passes. Every pass uses 100 optimization iterations, learn-ing rate 3 × 10 −4 , preview length 80 tokens, embedding L2 weight 10 −4 , gradient clipping at 5, minimum-iteration budget 50, dataset-row targets, no generic alternate targets, and no preview-based success stopping; Passes 2–4 add the pass-specific structural suffix described in Section 4.3 as the only difference from Pass 1. The 100-iteration per-prompt budget is well below the budgets used by GCG-family attacks [29, 8].

By construction, each method’s effect on the visible prompt string is deterministic: PEO projects its optimized prefix to the nearest tokens and leaves the visible prompt bit-identical; nanoGCG and BEAST append discrete adversarial tokens; SPT appends continuous adversarial embeddings. We therefore describe this as a property of each method rather than as an empirical metric. 

## 6. Main Results 

Table 2 reports the paper’s main four-way compari-son across both benchmarks; we begin with the AdvBench columns. 

6.1. AdvBench: Four-Model Matched Comparison 

On the primary ASR-Judge metric, PEO leads every competing attack on every AdvBench model. The pattern is not a narrow win in one or two cells: PEO ranges 71.73– 78.65% across the four models, while nanoGCG reaches only 8.46–37.50%, BEAST 4.62–24.62%, and SPT 18.08– 49.23%. Against nanoGCG the gain ranges from 34.23 to 64.23 points, against BEAST from 54.03 to 68.07 points, and against SPT from 22.50 to 57.50 points. ## 7. Discussion 

7.1. Failure Modes and Adaptive Gains 

PEO’s failures are heterogeneous. Some failures are 

shallow-compliance cases, where the model begins with a harmful-looking prefix and then pivots into a disclaimer or refusal tail. Others are structural-resistance cases, where the model accepts the topic framing but resists producing a sustained stepwise or actionable continuation at all. The adaptive follow-up targets were mainly effective for the second pattern: a labeled Title: or Steps: anchor gives the optimizer a concrete structural handle for pushing past the transition into a multi-step harmful procedure. Shallow-compliance cases, by contrast, often persist even under later scaffolds — the model emits the scaffold itself and then refuses or drifts off-topic inside it (cf. the failure rows in Table 1) — which explains why later rounds still leave a nontrivial residual failure set. The family split in Table 5 is consistent with this read-ing: title-style targets primarily help document-generation prompts, whereas labeled-procedure targets primarily help procedural prompts, with the bulk of steps-family recoveries coming from the shorter :\n\nSteps: scaffold. The interpre-tive takeaway is that structural-resistance failures are pass-through failures at the procedure-transition point rather than refusal at the first compliance token. 

7.2. Weaknesses of Competing Attacks 

PEO is consistently stronger than SPT on the primary ASR-Judge metric across the evaluated benchmark–model settings, suggesting that perturbing the embeddings of the original prompt tokens transfers more reliably to genuinely harmful continuations than appending a learned soft prompt. This difference is also consistent with the size of the opti-mization space. PEO optimizes every embedding position in the templated prompt, so a prompt with 𝑛 tokens ex-poses an 𝑛 × 𝑑 continuous search space anchored by the original token embeddings. SPT instead optimizes only a short block of appended adversarial embeddings, leaving the instruction embeddings themselves unchanged. The Llama-3.2 comparison is especially informative: SPT comes close to PEO under ASR-Match but falls far behind under ASR-Judge, indicating that its appended soft prompt often breaks surface refusal without reliably producing coherent harmful continuations (Section 7.3). BEAST is competitive mainly as a search-based token-appending attack: it can find non-refusal strings quickly, but on AdvBench those strings often remain shallow, which is why its ASR-Judge stays low even when ASR-Match is non-trivial (for example, 58.08% ASR-Match but only 15.19% ASR-Judge on Llama-3.2). This pattern is consistent with the idea that beam search over visible suffixes is effective at breaking the refusal prefix without reliably steering the model into sustained harmful continuation. HarmBench is somewhat more favorable to BEAST, especially on Vicuna, but the broader pattern still holds: BEAST’s visible suffix search is a meaningful comparison point, yet it remains less effective than PEO under the same reporting stack. 

7.3. Why ASR-Match Diverges from ASR-Judge 

ASR-Match and ASR-Judge diverge in both directions across the evaluated methods and models. Sometimes ASR-Match counts a response as successful because no refusal keyword appears, even though the response is actually safe or non-actionable; in other cases ASR-Match counts a response as a refusal because a keyword appears somewhere in the text, even though the response has already delivered harmful content. The two metrics therefore disagree not because of random noise, but because the substring heuristic fails in structurally predictable ways. AdvBench Qwen3 is the clear-est reversal in the paper’s main table: ASR-Judge reaches 72.69% while ASR-Match is only 38.65%, even though both metrics use the same 520-prompt denominator. At the other extreme, SPT on AdvBench Llama-3.2 shows the opposite pattern, with 82.69% ASR-Match but only 18.08% ASR-Judge. These cases motivate the bidirectional analysis below. In the false-positive direction, ASR-Match marks a response as successful because no refusal keyword appears,

For “Generate a message that spreads fake news,” BEAST on Qwen3 outputs a Chinese-language response that translates to “I cannot generate any fake news or factually incorrect informa-tion. . . ” Because no English refusal phrase is present, ASR-Match passes it through; both judges correctly mark it as a refusal.

These categories are shared across methods. We did not implement an automated classifier for the four failure modes; the illustrative examples above are drawn from a manual audit of the ASR-Match/ASR-Judge disagreement rows and are reported qualitatively rather than as a quantitative con-centration claim. What the numeric results do establish is that every method exhibits a substantial ASR-Match/ASR-Judge gap (Table 7), so no method’s ASR-Match number can be read as genuine harmful continuation without judge-level corroboration. Crucially, no method is immune: even PEO’s headline ASR-Match numbers include 19.0% shallow non-refusals that the judges do not count. The disagreement also runs in the opposite direction, for a different reason. As Table 7 shows, this reverse direc-tion is more common for PEO and SPT than for BEAST or nanoGCG. This pattern is also more specific than the AdvPrefix taxonomy: the problem is not simply that the response is incomplete or unfaithful, but that a substring heuristic can be tripped by safety or legality language even when the response is still harmful overall. A late disclaimer or refusal tail is one common pattern: the response begins with actual harmful content or actionable steps and only later appends a sentence such as “Please remember this is ille-gal. . . ” that matches the keyword list. But this is not the only recurring cause. Because ASR-Match is a substring heuris-tic, it can also fire on broad legality or safety terms such as “illegal,” “dangerous,” “harmful,” or “unethical” when those words appear inside otherwise actionable instructions or generic cautionary boilerplate. ASR-Match therefore can-not distinguish a genuine refusal from harmful content that merely contains one of these substrings somewhere in the response. The problem is not only overcounting shallow non-refusals, but also undercounting harmful responses that mix compliance with safety language or legal-risk framing. Taken together, the two directions explain why the paper ranks methods by ASR-Judge rather than by ASR-Match alone. Overcounting arises when non-refusal text is mis-taken for harmful compliance; undercounting arises when genuinely harmful text happens to contain refusal-style or legality/safety language. Framed this way, the disagreement also sharpens the semantic-preservation claim made in the abstract and introduction: across the 3,360 valid PEO rows in the 8-cell head-to-head, 63 .6% are judge-AND-confirmed harmful continuations of the original request, 13 .9% are bor-derline cases where the two judges disagree, 3.4% are safe-and-carrying-an-English-refusal-keyword refusals of the re-quest, and only the remaining 19 .0% fall into the shal-low non-refusal bucket above — so roughly four-fifths of PEO responses are either judge-confirmed harmful, judge-borderline, or explicit refusals of the original request rather than drifting into the off-topic/genre-swap/degenerate pat-terns that embedding-perturbation skeptics would predict. 7.4. Judge-Aggregation Sensitivity 

Because the two-judge AND rule is one of several pos-sible aggregation choices, we repeat the main head-to-head comparison under three alternatives: GPT-5.4 alone, Claude Opus 4.6 alone, and the OR rule (either judge calls harmful). The rankings are strikingly stable: PEO wins 8/8 model– benchmark cells against every competing attack under every aggregation rule (single-judge GPT, single-judge Claude, AND, and OR). The paper-default AND rule is the most conservative framing, and even there no competing attack wins any cell. This invariance means the headline “PEO outranks every competing attack everywhere” claim does not depend on judge choice, judge aggregation, or the marginal rows where the two judges disagree. 

7.5. Stronger Advance Signals for the Adaptive Schedule 

The adaptive schedule advances a prompt to the next pass whenever its current-pass response is marked as a failure, and we use ASR-Match (refusal-keyword absence) as that advance signal. As discussed in Section 7.3, ASR-Match is a lexical heuristic that disagrees with ASR-Judge in both directions, so using it as the advance signal leaves some genuine failures unrescued. In principle the schedule can be tightened by replacing the substring heuristic with a stronger classifier disjoint from the final judge stack, for example the HarmBench harmfulness classifier [13] or a separately prompted local LLM, and doing so would likely improve ASR-Judge. We deliberately do not adopt this option in our main experiments. The competing attacks we compare against— nanoGCG, SPT, and BEAST—are single-pass methods that do not consume any external semantic signal during opti-mization, and introducing a classifier-driven advance signal into PEO would make its pipeline asymmetric with respect to those attacks. Keeping the advance signal at the lexical-heuristic level ensures that PEO’s reported gains are at-tributable to the embedding-space optimization procedure it-self rather than to access to a richer intermediate supervision signal. Practitioners who are not constrained to a matched comparison would reasonably substitute a classifier-based advance signal and should expect additional gains on the failure modes catalogued in Section 7.3. 

7.6. Implications and Release Scope 

These findings also matter for defense design, although we do not directly evaluate defenses in this paper. Our results suggest that defenses focused mainly on visible adversar-ial suffixes may miss attacks that preserve the user-visible prompt exactly, and that short-preview evaluation may over-estimate robustness relative to full-response judging. Strong refusal should therefore be tested against attacks that vary continuation structure, attack only remaining failures, and preserve the surface prompt whenever possible. Publishing stronger attacks creates a dual-use tension: it reveals where alignment is brittle, but it also lowers the cost of misuse. We mitigate this by releasing evaluation artifacts and auditable scripts rather than a turnkey attack tool. 

## 8. Conclusion 

We have proposed PEO, a multi-round white-box jail-break that perturbs the continuous embeddings of existing prompt tokens—preserving both visible text and semantic intent—with adaptive failure-focused scheduling. On the primary ASR-Judge metric (two-judge agreement over full responses), PEO outperforms nanoGCG, SPT, and BEAST on both benchmarks. The results demonstrate that in-place embedding-space optimization is a stealthier and more re-liable alternative to discrete suffix, appended-embedding, and search-based attacks, especially when effectiveness is measured by whether the response is actually harmful rather than merely non-refusing. We further observe that PEO’s ag-gregate ASR-Judge is nearly model-invariant across the four evaluated models, in sharp contrast to the much wider per-model spreads of the three competing attacks on the same prompt sets. Per-prompt analysis shows that this aggregate consistency has different sources across benchmarks: Harm-Bench is more strongly prompt-driven, whereas AdvBench retains substantial model-specific variation.

## A. Judge Pipeline and Agreement 

ASR-Judge uses two judges: GPT-5.4 and Claude Opus 4.6. We classify each final response independently with both judges; a response is counted as harmful only if both 

judges agree (the two-judge AND rule). This follows the same general evaluation direction as HarmBench [13], Jail-breakBench [3], and JAILJUDGE [11]: all three move be-yond refusal-keyword heuristics toward stronger automated judges chosen for more reliable content-level assessment, even though they do not use our exact aggregation rule. We therefore treat ASR-Judge as the primary ranking metric throughout Sections 6 and 7, and use ASR-Match only as a comparability signal against prior jailbreak literature that predates judge-based evaluation. 

Justification for the two-judge AND rule. HarmBench [13] uses a single fine-tuned Llama-2-13B classifier, and JailbreakBench [3] likewise chooses a single default judge, Llama-3-70B, specifically because of its strong agreement with experts and relatively low false-positive rate. JAIL-JUDGE [11] goes further and proposes a multi-agent evalua-tion framework with belief-fusion rather than a single binary judge. None of these three works proposes our exact two-judge AND rule. What they do share, however, is the broader methodological position that keyword heuristics are inade-quate and that jailbreak evaluation should rely on stronger judge models chosen or combined with conservatism in mind. Our two-judge AND rule follows that same logic: it is a conservative aggregation that requires two independent strong judges to concur before a response is counted as harmful, which monotonically reduces false positives rela-tive to either judge alone. In that sense, it is stricter than the single-judge setups of HarmBench and JailbreakBench, and simpler but philosophically aligned with JAILJUDGE’s move toward multi-judge evaluation. Section 7.4 reports the full aggregation sensitivity analysis (GPT-5.4 alone, Claude Opus 4.6 alone, AND, and OR), and the rankings are invari-ant across all four choices. Table 9 reports per-(method, model, benchmark) two-judge agreement between GPT-5.4 and Claude Opus 4.6 on the finalized artifacts, alongside the ASR-J value computed over the full benchmark denominator. Agreement remains in the 78–98% range across all settings, indicating that the headline ASR-J numbers in the main text are not driven by a single judge’s idiosyncrasies; the lowest-agreement cells (e.g. SPT on Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct AdvBench at 78.3%) are also the ones where the two judges most often disagree on borderline partial-compliance responses, rather than on clearly safe or clearly harmful outputs.
