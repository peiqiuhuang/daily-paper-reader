# Randomized Max-Vertex-Cover Interdiction with Matroid Constraints 

Changjun Wang 1 Chenhao Wang 2,3

1 AMSS, Chinese Academy of Sciences 2 Beijing Normal University-Zhuhai 3 Beijing Normal-Hong Kong Baptist University 

Abstract 

We study a new bilevel optimization problem, termed the Randomized Max-Vertex-Cover Interdiction (RMVCI) problem under matroid constraints, which can be modeled as a zero-sum Stackelberg game on a network between a leader and a follower. The leader randomly se-lects a subset of vertices to protect, subject to a matroid constraint, while the follower—after inferring the leader’s protection probability distribution—chooses a subset of vertices (also matroid-constrained) to attack, aiming to maximize the expected total weight of edges in-cident to the set of vertices that are both attacked and unprotected. The leader’s objective is to determine an optimal randomized interdiction strategy that minimizes the follower’s expected payoff. Since the follower’s response problem is NP-hard, the resulting bilevel program is com-putationally challenging. We develop a conceptual approximation framework for tackling general bilevel interdiction problems. For the RMVCI problem under matroid constraints, we first formulate the follower’s problem as an integer linear program and show that its linear relaxation admits a tight integrality gap of 43 . Within the approximation framework, we replace the follower’s problem by its LP relaxation, and then study the resulting bilevel program. By shifting from distributions over sets to distributions over vertices and applying our approximation framework, we manage to design a polynomial-time 2-approximation al-gorithm for this relaxed bilevel problem. Combining these ingredients within our framework yields a polynomial-time 83 -approximation algorithm for RMVCI under matroid constraints. 

1 Introduction 

Network interdiction problems model strategic interactions between two players—a leader (in-terdictor) and a follower—in a zero-sum Stackelberg game played over a network. The leader first interdicts a subset of edges or vertices subject to a resource or capacity constraint. After observing the interdictions, the follower responds optimally to maximize (or minimize) their own objective. The value of the game is determined by the follower’s optimal outcome, while the leader aims to minimize (or maximize) this value, depending on the problem setting. A rich body of research has explored interdiction variants of classical network optimization problems, including shortest-path interdiction (removing edges to maximize the shortest path 1

> arXiv:2605.10526v1 [math.OC] 11 May 2026

length) [12, 14], maximum-flow interdiction [18, 16], minimum spanning tree interdiction [11], maximum matching interdiction [19], facility coverage interdiction [6], and interdiction of pack-ing linear programs [9]. In all these studies, the follower’s second-level problem is solvable in polynomial time. A comprehensive overview of network interdiction models and algorithms is provided by Smith and Song [17]. Motivated by real-world security scenarios such as protecting critical facilities or communi-cation hubs in infrastructure and social networks, in this paper, we introduce a new network interdiction problem, termed the Max-Vertex-Coverage Interdiction problem . Given an undi-rected edge-weighted graph G = ( V, E ), the leader determines a subset of vertices from V to interdict (or to protect). The follower then observes the leader’s interdiction strategy and selects a subset of the remaining (uninterdicted) vertices to attack, so as to maximize the total weight of edges incident to the selected vertices. The leader’s objective is therefore to determine an interdiction strategy that minimizes the follower’s payoff. In this work, we model both players’ feasible strategies as matroid-constrained subsets. The follower’s problem can then be viewed as a variant of the matroid-constrained Max-Vertex-Cover problem [15], with the key distinction that interdictions restrict certain edges to be covered by only one of their endpoints. 

Randomized Max-Vertex-Coverage Interdiction. In many practical scenarios, interdic-tion actions are carried out repeatedly over time. While the follower can observe realized inter-dictions, it is advantageous for the leader to adopt a randomized strategy to increase uncertainty and hinder the follower’s decision-making. Specifically, the leader commits to a probability dis-tribution over all feasible vertex subsets and, in each round, randomly selects one subset to interdict (protect) according to this distribution. After observing these realizations, the follower can infer the underlying distribution and subsequently launches a one-shot attack by selecting a subset of vertices so as to maximize the expected total weight of successfully covered (and thus compromised) edges. Accordingly, we study the problem of designing optimal randomized interdiction strategies for the leader to minimize the follower’s expected maximum coverage, which we refer to as the 

Randomized Max-Vertex-Cover Interdiction problem (RMVCI) under matroid constraints. Unlike most existing interdiction studies that consider polynomial-time solvable follower problems, RMVCI involves an NP-hard follower subproblem—the matroid-constrained Max-Vertex-Cover problem. Although prior works have examined NP-hard inner problems such as knapsack interdiction [4, 5, 7], the vertex-cover setting under matroid constraints is considerably more intricate due to the combinatorial dependencies imposed by both the network structure and matroid feasibility. Besides, most prior work focuses on deterministic interdiction strategies, whereas randomization is often essential in practice, particularly in applications such as critical infrastructure protection and counter-terrorism planning. Recent works have begun to highlight the value of randomization, such as the randomized max-flow interdiction model of Bertsimas et al. [2] and the randomized shortest-path interdiction studied by Holzmann and Smith [14], where the leader randomly removes arcs. However, in RMVCI, the leader’s randomized strategy introduces vertex-wise interdiction probabilities, turning the follower’s task into an expected maximum coverage problem with nonlinear dependencies on the probabilities. This added layer of randomness makes not only the follower’s problem but also the leader’s decision-making 2substantially more challenging. Since the follower’s optimization problem is already NP-hard even in the absence of inter-diction, there is no hope for a polynomial-time exact solution to RMVCI. Therefore, we focus on developing approximation algorithms. While most prior work has primarily concentrated on exact approaches, to the best of our knowledge, no general methodology currently exists for de-signing approximation algorithms for network interdiction problems involving NP-hard follower subproblems.

Proposition 1 provides a general framework for developing approximation algorithms for bilevel interdiction and other min–max (or max–min) problems in which the follower’s subprob-lem is NP-hard. It suggests that when the inner problem max x∈Q 2 f (y, x ) is computationally intractable, we can construct a tractable surrogate function ˜f (y) that approximates it within bounded factors. For instance, if the inner problem can be formulated as an integer linear pro-gram (ILP), one can define ˜f (y) as the optimal value of its linear programming (LP) relaxation, which can be solved efficiently. In this case, we have c1 = 1, and c2 equals the integrality gap of 5the relaxation. Alternatively, ˜f (y) may be defined as the objective value obtained by applying a polynomial-time approximation algorithm A (e.g., a greedy or local search heuristic) to the inner problem. Here, c2 = 1, and c1 corresponds to the approximation ratio of A. In both cases, Proposition 1 ensures that solving the simplified problem min y∈Q 1 ˜f (y) yields an approx-imate solution to the original bilevel problem (3.1), thus transforming an otherwise intractable formulation into a more manageable one. Although the above framework appears conceptually simple, identifying a suitable and prac-tically effective surrogate function ˜f (y) is often nontrivial. The design of ˜f (y) must strike a careful balance between its approximation fidelity to max x∈Q 2 f (y, x ) and the computational tractability of solving min y∈Q 1 ˜f (y). For our RMVCI, we begin by showing that the follower’s subproblem (2.2) under the matroid independence constraint can be formulated as an ILP. We then analyze its LP relaxation and prove that it admits a tight integrality gap of 43 . This allows us to derive a single-level minimiza-tion problem for the leader. Next, we consider the leader’s problem of minimizing the optimal value of the follower’s LP relaxation under its own matroid constraint. While standard exact-solving techniques do not work, we solve it with an approximation factor of 2. Consequently, we obtain an overall 43 × 2 = 83 –approximation algorithm for the interdiction problem (2.1).

6 Discussion 

For general matroid constraints, the exponentially many inequalities needed to represent the polytope in (5.3) necessitate the use of the ellipsoid method, which limits the approach’s prac-ticality to theoretical analysis. In the special case of uniform matroids, however, both players are subject to simple cardinality constraints, and their feasible regions are described by single inequalities: P 

> i∈V

qi ≤ kl and P 

> i∈V

xi ≤ kf . In this setting, the standard dualize-and-combine technique can be applied to solve the problem (5.3) more efficiently. 13 Specifically, first take the dual of the inner problem max x∈P f ˜Lq(x) in (5.3), treating q as fixed, so that both players face minimization problems. Then release q as a decision variable, obtaining a single-level minimization formulation. By the strong duality of linear programming, this single-level problem has the same optimal value as (5.3) but can be solved more efficiently (for example, using the simplex method), since it involves only a polynomial number of variables and constraints. This approach yields a 83 -approximation to the original interdiction problem (2.1) without relying on the impractical ellipsoid method. For future work, the unified approximation framework introduced in Section 3 can be ex-tended to a broader class of network interdiction problems with NP-hard inner problems, where the decision variables may include not only vertices but also edges or even paths. For instance, in the case where the inner problem is Max Edge Domination, both the leader and the follower select subsets of edges under given constraints, with the objective of minimizing or maximizing, respectively, the total weight of edges dominated by the follower’s selection. Another natural example arises in path interdiction: suppose there are multiple (possibly overlapping) paths from a source s to a sink t in a network. After the leader deploys protection on a subset of vertices or edges, the follower selects a set of edges to disrupt so as to intercept critical s-t

paths and invalidate as many of them as possible. These examples illustrate the flexibility of the proposed framework, and it would be interesting to further explore the design of approximation algorithms for other network interdiction problems within this framework.
