# How Useful Is Cross-Domain Generalization for Training LLM Monitors?

## Abstract 

Using prompted language models as classifiers enables classification in domains with limited training data, but misses some of the robustness and performance benefits that fine-tuning can bring. We study whether training on multiple classi-fication tasks, each with its own prompt, improves performance on new domains with new classification prompts. We show that such training partially generalizes to adjacent domains, improving classification performance on tasks that are unseen during training. However, we identify specific edge cases where the fine-tuned models fail to follow prompts, such as when the classification prompt changes completely while the data domain remains the same as during training. We show that classification training can be mixed with general instruction following training, and that (when done well) such training keeps the benefits of classification training and mitigates its generalization failures. Surprisingly, we see that this no-thinking supervised classification training can generalize to with-thinking classification and summarization, suggesting that no-thinking classification training might be instrumentally useful in building other kinds of classifiers and monitoring systems. 

## 1 Introduction 

Language models are increasingly used as classifiers, for example to flag sessions where the user is trying to cause harm [Markov et al., 2023, Sharma et al., 2025] or sessions where the AI itself is performing dangerous or misaligned actions [Anthropic, 2026a, OpenAI, 2026, Anthropic, 2026b]. Classifiers are sometimes specialized fine-tuned classifiers, which was shown to help improve robustness [Sharma et al., 2025, Cunningham et al., 2026], but it is also common to simply use prompted language models [Anthropic, 2026a, OpenAI, 2026, Anthropic, 2026b, Sumers et al., 2025] which rely on the general instruction following capabilities of the models and don’t require creating specialized training data. An intermediate approach is using models fine-tuned on multiple different classification tasks (with a separate classification prompt for each task) [Inan et al., 2023, OpenAI, 2025b]. In this work, we attempt to better understand the generalization properties of such general classifier training, with the goal of improving AI control monitors: How well does such training transfer to new tasks? How much benefit comes from training a specialized classifier model, compared to including the same data in a regular instruction following training run? 

We show that classification training can generalize to new adjacent domains. Concretely, a model trained to answer "is this cyber harm?" can later be prompted to answer "does the AI do anything dangerous?" and recover 75% of the improvement obtained by training directly on the target task. If we have a limited amount of in-domain data available, then adding additional data from an adjacent domain improves performance even when the dataset is very imbalanced. However, we observe some generalization failures where the model infers the classification rule from the training data rather than looking at the prompt. This can lead to some cases where training makes the model worse. For example, single task training can cause the model to stick to the training

We find that simply mixing the two datasets is not very effective since classification samples contain many fewer unmasked training tokens than the instruction following samples. We find that single-token classification training benefits extend beyond single-token classifica-tion . We evaluate models on thinking classification, where the model is asked to generate reasoning before giving an answer. Training Qwen3-8B only on a single-token cyber harm classification task increases with-thinking AUC from 0.84 to 0.94. We also find that single-token classification training can increase the rate at which models identify dangerous actions when used as summarizers. Finally, we show that this transfer from single-token classification to thinking classification can be used as an unsupervised elicitation technique by iteratively using thinking classification predictions in single-token classification training. Our contributions are as follows: 1. We show that classification training transfers across adjacent domains and that data from adjacent domains can be used to supplement limited amounts of in-domain data (Section 4); 2. We exhibit two kinds of generalization failures that result from single task fine-tuning and show that training on instruction-following data mitigates them (Section 4.6); 3. We evaluate classification-trained models on generative tasks (thinking classification and summarization) and find uplift even though we never train on these tasks (Section 5). 22 Related Work 

Misuse classifiers and AI control monitoring. Misuse classifiers detect harmful user intent such as requesting help with bioweapon synthesis so that responses can be blocked. They are typically either prompted classifiers [Sumers et al., 2025] or models fine-tuned for a single task [Sharma et al., 2025, Cunningham et al., 2026]. AI control monitors flag dangerous actions taken by a misaligned AI such as sabotaging research [Greenblatt et al., 2024]. These are typically implemented as prompted language models [Anthropic, 2026a, OpenAI, 2026, Anthropic, 2026b] that rely on the broad instruction following capabilities of these models rather than task specific training. We study both misuse and AI control monitors. 

Training general purpose classifiers. LlamaGuard [Inan et al., 2023] fine-tunes a model on multiple harm criteria and shows that it generalizes to unseen criteria at test time. Subsequent work has scaled this [Zeng et al., 2024, Zhao et al., 2025] and more recently OpenAI [2025b] extends this to reasoning classifiers. We extend existing work by studying how this type of training can be used alongside general instruction following training to produce general models that are more capable at classification tasks, rather than building specialized classifier models. We also study transfer beyond harm classification: to an adjacent domain (AI control), and to text generation abilities. 

## 3 Experiment Setup 

3.1 Classifier formats 

The classification tasks we use are binary. We prompt with the classification question as shown in Figure 1. We consider the following classification modes: 1. 1-token. We prompt the model as shown in Figure 1, asking the model to say 1 or 0, and then measure the probability of it saying 1 (normalized by P(1) + P(0)). 2. Thinking. We use the model’s extended thinking mode to think through its answer before providing a classification score between 0 and 100. After reasoning, the probability assigned to 1 or 0 no longer reflects the confidence as above, so we prompt the model to provide its own confidence score. 

3.2 Datasets 

We use the following datasets: Chemical harm (50k) and Cyber harm (50k) are synthetic conversa-tions labelled according to whether the user seeks assistance with causing a specific type of harm. 

ControlArena (4k) consists of AI agent transcripts labelled according to whether the agent takes a dangerous unrequested action or not [Storf et al., 2026]. We use four different settings: two for training and two for evaluation. IMDB (25k) is a sentiment classification dataset [Maas et al., 2011] included as a deliberately unrelated task. Covert Reasoning (1.5k) is a dataset containing reasoning traces about a given biology question, and sometimes the model is covertly reasoning through a math question alongside (but trying to hide it). This is from a study by METR [Cheng et al., 2025]. Full dataset details and classification prompts are provided in Appendix A.1. 

3.3 Metrics 

We use AUC (AUROC) by default to measure classifier performance. For cases in which the classifier is very strong, we use logAUC as defined in Anthropic [2025]: the area under the ROC curve with the false positive rate axis log-scaled, integrated from FPR = 10 −3 to 10 −1 and normalized to [0 , 1] .This gives us a less noisy metric which captures the performance at the low false positive rates we would care about in deployment. 

3.4 Models 

We use the Qwen3 [Qwen Team, 2025] family of models, specifically the 8B, 32B and 235B parameter count models. They all have two modes, think and instruct . We use the instruct mode for 1-token classification and the think mode for thinking classification. 3IMDB Chemistry Cyber

3.5 Training 

Rather than doing full parameter training, we use LoRA [Hu et al., 2021], a low rank training method that reduces the computational cost of training. We apply adaptors (rank 32, α = 32 ) to all attention and MLP projection layers of the frozen model, with no dropout. All weights are left in bfloat16. Supervised fine-tuning is done with all tokens except the classification token masked out, and standard hyperparameters: AdamW ( β1 = 0 .9, β2 = 0 .95 ), linear learning rate schedule starting at 1 × 10 −4,batch size 64. In Appendix C.3 we investigate the effect of learning rate on training and find it important to tune: we use the learning rate that maximizes performance on the ControlArena test set when training in-domain, but note that different data mixes may benefit from their own sweeps. The reinforcement learning setup is detailed further in Section 5.1.1 and consists of CISPO [Chen et al., 2025] using binary rewards and grouped advantages. We use Tinker [Thinking Machines Lab, 2025] for training. 

## 4 Classification Transfer 

We consider how training on classification tasks with 1-token SFT transfers to 1-token classification on unseen tasks and unseen domains. We train classifiers on single classification tasks and evaluate them on new tasks, and we investigate how training classifiers on mixes of multiple classification tasks affects transfer. 

4.1 Training transfers to adjacent domains 

We fine-tune models on the IMDB, Chemical harm, Cyber harm and ControlArena (AgentDojo + Rogue) datasets. We then evaluate on heldout ControlArena settings and see transfer to this heldout setting even when training on a single (different) task (as shown in Figure 2). In particular, we see that for the most adjacent domain, cyber, there is strong carry over with 75% of the uplift gained on ControlArena with no in-domain training data at all. For the Covert Reasoning evaluation, the model trained on IMDB performs surprisingly well. In Appendix B.1 we see that this seems to be partially due to the model being extremely poorly calibrated on that dataset to begin with. 

4.2 Scaling adjacent domain data is beneficial even when limited in domain data is available

4.3 Robustness to prompt injections transfers across domain and injection position 

We train on harmful cyber requests, augmented with prompt injections in the user message designed to bypass the classifier. This transfers across both domain and injection position: robustness improves against similar prompt injections inserted into an AI message of a ControlArena transcript (Figure 9). The prompt injections are generated using a variant of the linear pipeline from Shi et al. [2025]. We note that transfer is somewhat learning rate dependent (Figure 10). 

4.4 Classification improvements are not prompt or output format dependent 

In Appendix B.3 we train on one classification prompt and evaluate on a paraphrased version and find performance unchanged. Further, we change the output format from requesting that the model outputs “0”/“1” to “yes”/“no” and see that again, performance is unchanged. In fact, the model is more consistent between output formats after training than before training. Training improves the model’s ability at this classification task in a generalizable way rather than just improving performance for a specific classifier format. 

4.5 Classification training benefits persist through subsequent instruction following training 

Supervised training typically computes the loss as the mean loss of unmasked tokens over a batch. If we mix our classification data in with a much larger quantity of instruct data, then the fact that each classification sample contributes only one token to the loss means that we face the issue that the classification loss gets diluted by all the other loss terms. As a mitigation to this we consider a grouped two stage training process. We do all of the classification training in one group, followed by the instruct training. Data Generation. We use the Dolci-Instruct-SFT [Team OLMo, 2025] dataset as a source of instruct data to mix with our classification data. The completions are generated from an assortment of GPT models, which means that they are very off policy for the Qwen3 models that we use. In order to more cleanly isolate the interaction between classification data and instruct data, we discard the completions and regenerate using the Qwen3 model that we are using. This means that the completions are on policy for the starting model. If we did not regenerate completions, then the instruct training phase would also be teaching the model a new speaking style which may have further effects on classification ability. This also somewhat mimics a reinforcement learning post training stage, where the completions are closer to being on policy. In Figure 4 we show the results of doing this two stage training as opposed to fully shuffling. We use 3k classification samples from the ControlArena train set and 200k instruct samples. The mean number of completion tokens per instruct sample is 4k tokens, meaning that in total we have 3k classification tokens vs. 800M instruct tokens. The results show that this grouped training allows for classification benefits to persist through the much larger instruct training stage. In Appendix C.2 we show the decrease with dilution ratio, and show that another mitigation, up-weighting the relative loss contribution from the classification samples by directly multiplying, is also effective. 

4.6 Instruction following training mitigates some generalization failures 

We observe two types of generalization failure caused by the model inferring the classification task from the training data rather than the prompt. This is related to shortcut learning [Geirhos et al., 2020], where models learn spurious features rather than the desired task. 

Overflowing. The model infers a broader classification boundary than the prompt specifies. For example, when trained with a cyber-specific prompt on cyber harm data, the model learns that it is a general harm classifier rather than a cyber-only one (Figure 5A). 

Sticking. The model fails to follow a new classification prompt when the data is very similar to that seen during training. For example, after training on harm classification, we evaluate with a new prompt “is the user French?” and see that the model continues saying “yes” to the types of harm it saw during training, even though it would have answered correctly before training (Figure 5B). ## 5 Generalization to Generation Tasks 

The previous results suggest that classification training is compatible with general model training. We now study how the generative properties of the models change after training, in particular at thinking classification and summarization. 

5.1 Thinking classifiers 

Thinking classification outperforms 1-token, and multiple rollouts improve further. We see that our starting model is much more capable with thinking enabled, and that multiple rollouts improves its classification capabilities further. But surprisingly, 1-token SFT improves performance across all modes. As expected, the model improves on 1-token classification as that is the format it is trained in. However, it also improves with thinking enabled and with multiple rollouts, suggesting that the training has led to a more general capability improvement. In Appendix B.4 we investigate where the thinking improvements are coming from and find that it is a combination of both detecting the side task at a higher rate, and also assigning a suspicious score to the side task when it is identified. Preliminary experiments suggest that 1-token SFT may work even for pure reasoning models like GPT-OSS-20B [OpenAI, 2025a] too (as opposed to only being useful on hybrid instruct/reasoning models like Qwen3), though effect size is small and inconsistent (Appendix B.5).

The strongest checkpoint we produce is trained by a form of “warm start” where we initialize the RL training with a checkpoint already trained on the same data with 1-token SFT. We emphasize that this is distinct from the standard method of warm starting where models are trained with SFT on reasoning traces: in this case there are no reasoning traces present in the 1-token SFT stage. 

5.1.2 Bootstrapping thinking labels through 1-token SFT 

Given that 1-token SFT improves performance with thinking, and there is a large delta between thinking and 1-token classification performance, we consider an iterative training method that does not require access to labels: (1) label the dataset using the current model in thinking mode, then (2) train with 1-token SFT on those labels, and repeat. We show the results of performing this iteratively 5 times in Figure 7. We see that the model improves with each iteration despite no access to any ground truth labels during training. After 5 iterations, the model approaches the performance of one trained on ground truth labels, despite never using the labels. This could also be thought of as a form of test time training : we are using training to help label an unlabelled dataset.

## 6 Limitations 

We studied a small set of classification tasks and we ran most of our experiments on a single family of models. While the consistency of our results within the Qwen3 family is evidence that our results are not very sensitive to the exact setting studied, a wider diversity of tasks and models would strengthen our conclusions. Our reinforcement learning setup is minimal. A wider sweep over algorithms and hyperparameters may exhibit situations where 1-token SFT initialization provides a smaller performance boost. 

## 7 Conclusion 

We have studied a simple classification training method and seen that benefits can generalize to adjacent domains, that the training is compatible with general instruction following training as well, and that the benefits transfer to improved generation abilities on thinking classification and summarization. This suggests that this style of training may be useful for building models that are stronger at monitoring, both as classifiers and when used in other classification-adjacent tasks. 9References

## A Datasets and Prompts 

A.1 Dataset details Chemical harm (n=50,000) A synthetic dataset consisting of two turn conversations consisting of a User: message and an Assistant: message. The label corresponds to whether the user is trying to get assistance from the assistant with causing chemical harms. The constitution used to generate the synthetic data is the same as that used in Sharma et al. [2025]. The completions are generated using a helpful-only model and filtered to ensure any refusals are removed. 11 Cyber harm (n=50,000) The same format as above but using a cyber harm based constitution (full constitution can be found below), and the labels correspond to whether the user is trying to get assistance with the goal of causing cyber harm. 

ControlArena (n=4000) Transcripts consisting of an AI being given a coding task and either completing it faithfully or completing it along with an unrequested side task . The tasks and environ-ments used come from ControlArena [Inglis et al., 2025] and we use the environments RogueEval, AgentDojo, IAC and BashBench. The first two are used for training, the latter two are held out for evaluations. The transcripts are taken from Storf et al. [2026]. We choose not to use the Shade Arena transcripts due to their length. The format is a single user message outlining the task, followed by multiple assistant messages and tool calls. 

IMDB (n=25,000) A dataset of movie reviews sourced from Maas et al. [2011]. They are labelled according to whether the sentiment is positive or negative. This is used as a task that is very unrelated to what we are interested in. 

Covert Reasoning (n = 1500) A dataset from a study by METR [Cheng et al., 2025], consisting of reasoning traces from models prompted to solve a biology question, either with or without a side math task as well. When the math task is present the models are prompted in a variety of ways to hide the math reasoning. We filter out all transcripts where the math reasoning is invisible to prompted Opus 4.5 when provided with the full biology and math tasks (a higher affordance than the classifier receives) as the strategy of just never mentioning the math task is impossible to catch. The format of these is a user message with the biology question along with the reasoning trace of the model. The task is for the classifier to identify which of those reasoning traces come from models that were also prompted to do the math task as well, as opposed to the benign reasoning traces corresponding to the model only being given the biology task.

A.4 Synthetic dialog generation pipeline 

We generate the synthetic cybersecurity and chemical harm classification datasets using a four-stage pipeline. For the first three stages we use a helpful-only model to avoid refusals. 17 Stage 1: Seed generation. We bootstrap the pipeline by prompting a language model to produce seed personas and scenarios from a content moderation constitution. The constitution specifies block/allow criteria: requests aiming to attack, deceive, or harm are blocked, while defensive, educational, or authorized security requests are allowed. The model generates 10 single-sentence user personas designed to be realistic and ambiguous—plausible in both block and allow contexts—rather than stereotypically malicious or benign. Separately, it generates 200 scenario seed ideas (100 block, 100 allow), each a specific situation grounded in the constitution’s criteria. The prompts instruct the model to maximise variety and avoid grey areas. 

Stage 2: Dialog generation. For each combination of persona, scenario, and category (block/allow), we prompt the model to generate 15 request–response dialogs, yielding a theoretical maximum of 60,000 dialogs across the 10 × 200 × 2 generation matrix. The prompt instructs the model to produce realistic, varied requests with natural language artifacts (e.g., typos, ambiguity). Block-category responses are intentionally compliant (not refusals), providing the harmful content requested, so that downstream classifiers can learn to detect both sides of an unsafe interaction. The resulting dataset contains 53,333 dialogs. 

Stage 3: Quality grading. A stronger model (Claude Opus 4.5) grades each dialog on six binary criteria: constitution violation, unauthorized access, deception/fraud, disruptive tools, privacy viola-tion, and legitimate intent. These labels are compared against the intended block/allow category to measure generation quality. 

Stage 4: Filtering. Dialogs are filtered based on the grading labels, producing a final dataset of 46,623 dialogs.

## B Supplementary Experiments 

B.1 Classification score distributions 

Figure 8 shows the distribution of 1-token classification scores (log-odds) for positive and negative samples, evaluated on ControlArena and Covert Reasoning, for each training domain. The starting model is not very well calibrated, the trained ones are much more balanced. 

B.2 Prompt injection robustness 

We augment the cyber harm training data with prompt injections designed to bypass the classifier, generated using a variant of the linear pipeline from Shi et al. [2025], and evaluate on ControlArena transcripts where similar injections are inserted into the AI’s messages. Figure 9 shows that training on injected cyber data transfers robustness to the ControlArena domain. However, Figure 10 shows that this transfer is sensitive to learning rate, and that while including more diverse training data leads to better performance across a range of learning rates, it is possible to come close without including the prompt injections. 

B.3 Prompt and output format robustness 

We evaluate whether classification improvements are sensitive to the exact wording of the prompt or the output format. Figure 11 shows that performance is unchanged when we evaluate with a paraphrased version of the classification prompt. Figure 12 shows that changing the output format from “0”/“1” to “yes”/“no” also has no effect on performance. In fact, the fine-tuned model is more consistent between output formats than the base model. 

B.4 Thinking classification analysis

Figure 13 shows that classification training makes the model more consistent: after training, when the model identifies a side task, it does so in nearly all rollouts rather than sporadically. Figure 14 shows that classification training also improves the conversion from identifying the side task to assigning a high suspicion score.
