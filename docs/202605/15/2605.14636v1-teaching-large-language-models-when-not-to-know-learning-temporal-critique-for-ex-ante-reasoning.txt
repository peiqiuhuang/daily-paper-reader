## Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) often fail to reason under temporal cutoffs: when prompted to answer from the standpoint of an earlier time, they exploit knowledge that became available only later. We study this failure through the lens of ex-ante reasoning, where a model must rely exclusively on information knowable before a cutoff. Through a systematic analysis of prompt-level interventions, we find that temporal leakage is highly sensitive to cutoff formulation and instruction place-ment: explicit cutoff statements outperform implicit historical framings, and prefix constraints reduce leakage more effectively than suffix constraints. These findings indicate that prompting can steer models into a temporal frame, but does not endow them with the ability to verify whether a response is temporally admissible. We further argue that supervised fine-tuning is insufficient, since ex-ante correctness is not an intrinsic property of an answer, but a relation between the answer and the cutoff. To address this gap, we propose TCFT , a Temporal Critique Fine- Tuning framework that trains models to acquire cutoff-aware temporal verification. Given a query, a cutoff, and a candidate response, TCFT teaches the model to identify post-cutoff leakage, explain temporal boundary violations, and judge temporal admissibility. Experiments with Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct show that TCFT consistently outperforms prompting and SFT baselines, reducing average leakage by 41.89 and 37.79 percentage points, respectively. 

## 1 Introduction 

Suppose today is May 1, 2015. How would you describe Twitter? A human answering this question would naturally inhabit that moment — recalling a 140-character platform led by Dick Costolo — while instinctively setting aside what came later: Musk’s 2022 acquisition, and the rebranding to X. This capability is referred to as ex-ante reasoning [1], the ability to reason under a strict temporal cutoff, drawing exclusively on information available up to that point. Far from a curiosity, it underpins any application whose validity depends on what was knowable at the time , from backtesting investment strategies [ 2, 3] and conducting historical counterfactual analysis [ 4 ] to tracing the genuine progression of scientific ideas [ 5 ]. In each, value hinges not on what a model can retrieve, but on what it refuses to use . Yet, today’s most capable language models [ 6 , 7] routinely fail at this refusal [ 1]: ask the question above with the cutoff stated unambiguously, they volunteer post-2015 facts about Musk and X, and this failure does not diminish with scale. Stronger models memorize post-cutoff knowledge more thoroughly, and thus leak it more fluently ( cf. Figure 2b) [1, 8].

Confronted with such leakage, the natural first recourse is to push harder on the prompt: state the cutoff more forcefully, elicit chain-of-thought [ 9 ], or invoke self-verification [ 10 ]. We systematically investigate this family of interventions, treating their behavior as a diagnostic probe into how models handle temporal constraints. Two findings stand out, each difficult to reconcile with the hypothesis that models possess any stable notion of a cutoff: • Leakage tracks surface phrasing, not semantics. Two logically equivalent cutoff specifications — an implicit historical framing ( “Assume today is May 1, 2009” ) and an explicit knowledge restriction ( “Use only information available before May 1, 2009” ) — yield markedly different leakage rates, with explicit phrasings consistently outperforming implicit ones. • Leakage tracks position, not proximity to the answer. Placing the cutoff before the query (prefix) reduces leakage substantially more than placing it afterward (suffix), despite the latter’s closer proximity to generation. Taken together, these findings reveal an underlying weakness: prompt-based interventions modulate 

how the model is steered into a temporal frame , but they do not install a mechanism that asks, of each candidate fact, whether it is admissible under the given cutoff. In essence, the model is being conditioned, not verified. This diagnosis also explains why supervised fine-tuning offers no real escape: SFT is an imitation objective [ 11 , 12 ], teaching the model to reproduce a target answer for a given prompt [ 13 –16 ]. But ex-ante correctness is not a property of an answer in isolation. Instead, it is a relation between an answer and a cutoff. No volume of answer-imitation supervision can instill this contingency, because the supervision signal itself never asks the model to evaluate the relation. What is missing, then, is supervision that operates on the relation rather than on the answer — training that teaches the model not what to say, but why a given response is or is not temporally admissible at a given time. We instantiate this principle as TCFT (Temporal Critique Fine- Tuning), a critique-based [11 ] training framework for ex-ante reasoning. Rather than fine-tuning the model to imitate temporally correct answers, TCFT trains it to acquire cutoff-aware temporal verification. Given a query, a cutoff, and a candidate response, the model learns to assess whether the response is temporally admissible, identify post-cutoff leakage, and explain boundary violations. In this way, TCFT directly supervises the missing relation between an answer and a cutoff, rather than treating ex-ante reasoning as a matter of prompt conditioning or answer imitation. We instantiate this supervision by using a teacher model to generate cutoff-conditioned critiques for candidate responses, where each critique highlights temporally invalid facts and provides an admissibility judgment [17, 18]. The student model is then fine-tuned on these critiques, acquiring the ability to verify whether a candidate response is valid under the specified cutoff. With this temporal verification capability, the model can reject temporally inadmissible facts before accepting an answer, thereby reducing the leakage of future knowledge. Experiments with Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct [ 19 ] demonstrate that TCFT consistently outperforms strong prompting and fine-tuning baselines, achieving better adherence to temporal constraints with leakage rate reductions of 41.89 and 37.79 percentage points, respectively. 

## 2 Preliminary 

To ground our empirical investigation and subsequent methodology, we first formalize the ex-ante reasoning problem, outline the task taxonomy, and establish rigorous evaluation metrics. 22.1 Task Definition 

Given a query x and a specific cutoff timestamp tc, ex-ante reasoning aims to produce a response ˆy

using only information verifiable before tc. Let A(x) denote the universe of all factual knowledge associated with query x. We define a temporal mapping τ (f ) that assigns each fact f ∈ A (x) the exact time it became verifiable in the real world. The temporal constraint tc naturally partitions this knowledge space into two disjoint subsets: 

Rprev (x, t c) = {f ∈ A (x) | τ (f ) ≤ tc} (1) 

Rpost (x, t c) = {f ∈ A (x) | τ (f ) > t c} (2) where Rprev (x, t c) represents the admissible historical context, and Rpost (x, t c) denotes future facts that must remain inaccessible. For a model M parameterized by θ, the output is ˆy = M (x, t c). An oracle ideal response y∗ is constructed entirely from Rprev (x, t c) and contains only the pre-cutoff information. Strict ex-ante validity therefore requires excluding all future knowledge: F (ˆ y) ∩

Rpost (x, t c) = ∅.Depending on the structural complexity of the required response, the ex-ante reasoning task can be categorized into the following two primary paradigms [1]: • Single-Event Prediction. Single-event prediction asks the model to adopt the perspective of tc and predict an outcome verifiable only afterward. This paradigm typically appears in time-sensitive QA or numerical forecasting. For an event whose true outcome a∗ materializes at te > t c, ex-ante reasoning requires a historically grounded prediction or abstention, rather than leaking the future ground truth. • Multi-Event Generation. In open-ended generation, such as Wikipedia-style [ 20 ] summarization from a historical perspective, the response ˆy is a complex text sequence rather than a single fact. For evaluation, we decompose it into independent atomic claims F (ˆ y) = {f1, f 2, . . . , f n}, each of which is verified against the cutoff tc.

2.2 Evaluation Metrics 

The core failure mode in ex-ante reasoning is temporal leakage : the unintended use of post-cutoff knowledge ( Rpost ) under an explicit pre-cutoff restriction ( Rprev ). Leakage differs from memorization :the latter refers to the model’s internalization of facts that became verifiable only after tc, which is a prerequisite for leakage rather than a failure in itself. Without prior memorization of the relevant future facts, cutoff adherence is trivial; leakage evaluation is therefore meaningful only when such memorization is established. We formalize leakage by measuring whether the generated output intersects with the restricted future knowledge space. For single-event prediction , leakage is a binary indicator of whether the model directly reveals the future outcome: 

Lsingle (x) = 1[ˆ y = a∗ ∧ te > t c]. (3) This captures direct disclosure of a fact that should be unknown at cutoff tc. In the more complex setting of multi-event generation , we calculate a claim-level leakage rate, defined as the proportion of generated atomic facts that fall into the restricted future knowledge set:

A high leakage rate indicates a systemic failure of temporal reasoning: the model cannot reliably separate pre-cutoff knowledge from post-cutoff facts. Unlike forecasting [ 21 , 22 ], ex-ante reasoning concerns the admissibility of information. Thus, even a factually “correct” future prediction is a failure if it relies on post-cutoff information. Evaluating temporal adherence requires capturing both the violation of constraints and the utility of the generated text. Relying solely on constraint satisfaction is insufficient, as models might exploit degenerate strategies to bypass the rules. This joint criterion prevents temporal gains from coming at the cost of task utility. The most intuitive approach to enforcing temporal boundaries in LLMs is through prompt engineering. However, prior evaluations suggest that simple instructions often fail to consistently eliminate tempo-ral leakage [ 1]. To better understand the limitations of prompt-based interventions, we systematically investigate how instruction paradigms affect model adherence to temporal cutoffs. 

3.1 Formalizing Temporal Instruction Paradigms 

To systematically analyze the impact of prompting, we represent a temporal template T as a tuple 

(tc, e T , ϕ T , ℓ T ), capturing key dimensions of prompt-based temporal control. Here, tc denotes the target cutoff date and eT ∈ { implicit , explicit } specifies boundary explicitness. The predicate 

ϕT (fi; tc) ∈ { 0, 1} evaluates whether a fact fi is admissible under template T and cutoff tc. Finally, 

ℓT ∈ { prefix , suffix } indicates whether the temporal constraint is placed before or after the query. Given this formalization, we investigate how variations in eT and ℓT affect ex-ante reasoning perfor-mance, focusing on two dimensions: the explicitness of the temporal constraint and its placement within the prompt. These factors are hypothesized to influence how effectively a model can internalize and adhere to temporal boundaries, thereby affecting leakage rates and overall validity. 

3.2 Insight 1: Leakage Tracks Surface Phrasing 

The first critical finding concerns how the phrasing of temporal boundaries influences model behavior. In ex-ante reasoning, models are typically prompted into a temporal frame either implicitly through historical framing ( e.g., “Assume today is May 1, 2009”) or explicitly through direct knowledge restriction ( e.g., “Use only information available before May 1, 2009”). Formally, given a candidate fact fi, a template induces an admissibility predicate ϕT (fi; tc) ∈ { 0, 1}.A strong template explicitly verbalizes this predicate, ideally requiring ∀fi ∈ F (y), ϕ T (fi; tc) = 1 ,whereas a weak template only implicitly points to the same boundary. Specifically: • Explicit templates specify the knowledge boundary, often through prohibitive language ( e.g., “Use only information available before tc” or “Do not rely on facts after tc”). We find that these templates consistently reduce leakage across both single-event and multi-event tasks. By verbalizing 

ϕT (fi; tc) = 1[τ (fi) ≤ tc], explicit prompts give the model a clear surface rule to follow. • Implicit templates instead frame the task through a historical persona or perspective ( e.g., “Assume today is tc” or “Imagine you are answering on tc”). Although the temporal boundary is identical, models perform substantially worse under implicit constraints. This sensitivity suggests that current LLMs rely on surface linguistic cues rather than a stable, cutoff-aware reasoning mechanism. As shown in Figure 2a, explicit templates consistently yield lower future-information leakage than implicit templates. This formulation sensitivity suggests that current models lack a stable understand-ing of temporal boundaries. If models had mastered cutoff-aware reasoning, surface-level phrasing of the same boundary should not substantially change performance [ 1 ]. Instead, models appear to be linguistically conditioned rather than temporally verified. 

3.3 Insight 2: Leakage Tracks Prompt Position 

The second finding involves the positional variation of constraints. Beyond formulation, the position 

ℓT of the temporal constraint also affects adherence: it can appear either as a prefix before the query or as a suffix after it. For instance, consider the following two templates for a query about Twitter: • Prefix : “Use only information available before May 1, 2009. How would you describe Twitter?” • Suffix : “How would you describe Twitter? Use only information available before May 1, 2009.” This positional variation is orthogonal to formulation explicitness, allowing us to independently assess how placement affects model behavior. Empirically, we observe a clear prefix advantage : constraints placed at the beginning of the prompt yield markedly lower leakage than those appended as a suffix, as shown in Figure 2a. This finding is counterintuitive, since suffix constraints are closer to generation and might be expected to exert stronger control. Instead, the result aligns with known positional biases in in-context learning and long-context processing [ 23 , 21 ], where information near the start of a prompt is often more stable. 4(a) Temporal template sensitivity. ## 4 Method 

We address the core challenge identified in Section 3: ex-ante reasoning is fundamentally a temporal verification problem rather than a generation problem. Prompt-based control and conventional SFT fall short because they separate answer production from valid-at-cutoff judgment. Instead of imitating target answers, our approach trains models to critique candidate responses under temporal constraints. The model learns to assess whether a response is admissible at cutoff tc, identify temporal violations, and explain its judgment. By making temporal validity a direct training target, this framework enables more robust cutoff-aware reasoning across diverse cutoff specifications. 

4.1 Rethinking the Learning Objective: From Imitation to Verification 

Standard supervised fine-tuning optimizes min θ E(x,y ∗)ℓ(Mθ (x), y ∗), where y∗ is a target answer. This treats correctness as an answer-level property independent of context. In ex-ante reasoning, however, validity is cutoff-dependent: the validity of y depends on the temporal boundary tc:Valid (y | x, t c)̸ = Valid (y | x). (5) Thus, answer-matching supervision alone cannot teach a model when not to use memorized post-cutoff facts. Section 3 reveals that this limitation runs deeper than prompt phrasing. The formulation and positional sensitivity of temporal constraints suggest that models lack an internalized procedure for judging temporal admissibility. Prompt engineering only steers surface behavior, without installing a verification mechanism. We therefore reframe training to directly supervise cutoff-conditioned temporal judgments, shifting the objective from matching target answers to determining whether a response is valid under the given cutoff. 

4.2 Temporal Critique Task Definition 

In our TCFT framework, we extend the Critique Fine-Tuning (CFT) [ 11 ] paradigm to the temporal setting. Classical CFT mainly targets reasoning verification: given a question and a potentially flawed solution, the model learns to critique the reasoning process and identify errors. In our setting, however, critique is intrinsically time-dependent, since the validity of a claim depends on the temporal boundary. Given a query x, cutoff timestamp tc, and candidate response y, the teacher model CΘ

5(e.g., GPT-5.4 [24]) outputs a structured tuple: 

CΘ(x, t c, y ) = ( r, v ), (6) where v is a binary verdict indicating whether y is temporally admissible at cutoff tc and r provides the supporting rationale. This decomposition captures two coupled capabilities: leakage detection ,which identifies claims in y that are not verifiable at tc; and temporal reasoning , which explains why those claims violate the cutoff based on their verifiability timelines.

To construct temporal critique supervision, we pair each query with multiple cutoffs, candidate responses, and corresponding temporal verdicts. The resulting dataset is structured as:

QA Augmentation. For each QA pair from the unaugmented ex-ante reasoning dataset [ 1 ], we generate variants along three dimensions: (1) Cutoff variation , where the cutoff date is set to 1, 14, and 30 days before the event, exposing the model to the same factual context under different temporal boundaries; (2) Formulation variation , where each cutoff is paired with explicit knowledge restriction (“Use only information available before tc”) and implicit historical framing (“Assume today is tc”); and (3) Position variation , where each template is placed either before the query as a prefix or after it as a suffix. This results in 3 × 2 × 2 = 12 distinct training instances per original pair. 

Wikipedia Augmentation. For generation tasks, we focus on formulation and position variation: (1) cutoff dates are kept fixed, since Wikipedia-style prompts often involve historical entities or long-span events, making fine-grained cutoff shifts less well-defined; and (2) each example is expanded into 2 × 2 = 4 variants by crossing explicit/implicit formulation with prefix/suffix placement. All splits (train, validation, and test) undergo the same augmentation, ensuring balanced exposure to strong and weak prompt formulations and enabling fair comparison across conditions. This construction supervises temporal validity relations, i.e., query-cutoff pairs with valid/invalid labels, rather than a single target answer. By annotating cross-cutoff validity changes, the model learns that correctness is fundamentally contingent on tc.

4.4 Learning via Temporal Critique Supervision 

In practice, TCFT is implemented as a supervised fine-tuning process over the temporal critique tuples. Given a training instance (x, t c, y, c ) with c = ( r, v ), we optimize the following negative log-likelihood objective: 

L = − log p(c | x, t c, y ; θ). (8) The joint probability can be factorized as: 

pθ (c | x, t c, y ) = pθ (r | x, t c, y ) · pθ (v | x, t c, y, r ), (9) where the model first generates a rationale r that examines the candidate response under the cutoff, and then summarizes this analysis into a verdict v. This order encourages the verdict to be grounded in an explicit temporal critique rather than produced as an isolated label. In implementation, the rationale and verdict are concatenated into a single critique-conclusion sequence and optimized via teacher forcing, yielding the full sequence likelihood. Notably, this objective does not directly supervise final-answer generation ( e.g., predicting y from x

and tc). Instead, the model learns to critique candidate answers and summarize the temporal judgment. This inversion is deliberate: by decoupling generation from verification, the model can reuse its base knowledge while learning a conditional gate that flags leaked content. 

4.5 Inference 

At inference time, the model directly generates a response ˆy from the query and cutoff (x, t c).Although TCFT is trained on temporal critiques rather than final answers, it equips the model with 6cutoff-aware verification. By learning from temporally violated candidates and their critiques, the model internalizes which facts are admissible under a cutoff and which constitute leakage. Thus, inference requires neither explicit critique generation nor answer refinement; the learned verification capability implicitly guides ˆy away from post-cutoff information. Formally, training optimizes the critique objective p(c | x, t c, y ), while inference samples ˆy from 

p(ˆ y | x, t c). Although the two stages differ in form, both rely on the same learned capability: judging temporal admissibility under the cutoff. In this sense, TCFT does not teach the model to imitate a fixed target answer; instead, it teaches the model a temporal validity criterion that guides generation. Accordingly, TCFT does not require an explicit answer-repair objective, i.e., optimizing 

p(y∗ | x, t c, y, c ). Rather than learning to rewrite a flawed answer into a target response, the model learns to recognize temporal admissibility itself, in line with prior critique-based learning [ 11 ]. Introducing a separate refinement objective would add a competing learning signal and dilute the focus on temporal verification. Instead, the verdict v and rationale r provide direct supervision for temporal judgment, which can support downstream filtering or post-hoc correction when needed. 

## 5 Experiment 

This section provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation of TCFT, with the analysis structured around the following key research questions: RQ1: How does TCFT perform compared with state-of-the-art closed-source models and strong baseline methods? RQ2: How well does TCFT generalize across boundary explicitness eT and constraint placement ℓT ? RQ3: How do the temporal critique rationale r and verdict v contribute to learning cutoff-aware verification? 

5.1 Experimental Setups 

We use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct as the LLM backbones, excluding smaller models whose limited memorization makes leakage evaluation ill-defined. We focus on standard instruction models to isolate whether TCFT itself can induce cutoff-aware temporal verification, without relying on reasoning-specialized post-training. We build on the original ex-ante reasoning benchmark, augmenting its single-event QA and Wikipedia-style generation tasks. For evaluation, we use leakage rate as the primary metric: for QA, we report leakage rates under 1-day, 14-day, and 30-day cutoffs, together with the average leakage rate; for Wikipedia generation, we report leakage rate and claim accuracy. We compare TCFT with closed-source models and open-source baselines, including Zero-shot, Few-shot, Chain-of-thought, Self-verification, and SFT. Comprehensive de-tails on the benchmark, baselines, evaluation metrics, and TCFT implementation are provided in Appendix D.5.

5.3 Generalization Across Temporal Templates (RQ2) 

To investigate whether TCFT learns general temporal verification rather than template-specific pat-terns, we evaluate cross-template transfer on QA and Wiki. We vary templates along two dimensions: implicit vs. explicit temporal constraints and prefix vs. suffix cutoff placement. TCFT is trained on one template and tested on both the seen template and its counterpart, measuring template-specific performance and cross-template generalization. As shown in Figure 3, we can find that: • TCFT remains effective across different temporal templates. Across implicit/explicit formula-tions and prefix/suffix cutoff placements, TCFT consistently achieves low leakage. Under the same template setting, QA leakage remains within 0.00–1.00%, and Wiki leakage ranges from 14.19% to 16.13%. These results suggest that TCFT captures the underlying cutoff-verification behavior across different surface formulations, rather than merely memorizing template-specific patterns. • TCFT remains effective under cross-template transfer. When evaluated on unseen template variants, leakage increases only slightly and remains well controlled. Cross-template QA leakage stays low at 1.00–4.48%, while Wiki leakage remains within 16.13–19.01%. This suggests that TCFT acquires a transferable temporal verification capability rather than relying on prompt-specific surface patterns, enabling robust generalization across cutoff formulations.

On Wiki, leakage also rises from 14.04% to 14.80%, while accuracy drops from 66.24% to 63.73%. These results show that the verdict signal helps models identify cutoff violations. However, the relatively mild degradation suggests that binary judgment alone provides limited supervision; robust ex-ante generation requires learning the underlying temporal verification rationale, i.e., the ability to verify whether a fact is temporally valid under the cutoff constraint. 

## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we study ex-ante reasoning as a temporal verification problem, where models must judge whether a response is admissible under a given cutoff. We show that prompt-level interventions provide brittle control, with leakage remaining sensitive to formulation and placement. To address this, we propose TCFT, a Temporal Critique Fine-Tuning framework that trains models to identify post-cutoff leakage, explain boundary violations, and judge temporal admissibility. Experiments on QA and Wiki show that TCFT reduces leakage while preserving factual utility. Ablation and cross-template studies further show that TCFT learns transferable cutoff-aware verification beyond prompt-specific patterns. Overall, reliable ex-ante reasoning requires verification rather than stronger prompting or answer imitation. 

## 7 Limitations 

While TCFT provides an effective framework for cutoff-aware verification, limitations remain. Our evaluation focuses on QA and Wiki-style generation, leaving broader time-sensitive scenarios such as finance, law, and scientific trend analysis unexplored. We use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct, as many smaller open-weight models weakly memorize post-cutoff facts, making 9leakage evaluation and fine-tuning less informative. Extending TCFT to stronger backbones and reasoning-specialized models remains future work. TCFT also relies on teacher-generated temporal critiques, whose quality may vary across teacher models, critique styles, and evidence sources; developing more robust critique-generation strategies is another important direction.

## A Related Work 

A.1 Temporal Reasoning 

Evaluating LLMs on time-related dimensions has historically centered on temporal reasoning and time-sensitive question answering [ 25 , 26 ]. Benchmarks such as TRAM [ 27 ], TimeBench [ 28 ], and Test of Time (ToT) [ 29 ] rigorously assess models on event ordering, durations, and temporal arithmetic [ 1 , 8]. Similarly, time-sensitive QA datasets evaluate how models adapt to evolving knowledge [ 30 ]. However, these existing benchmarks generally assume full access to future context or focus on static facts, largely overlooking the specific requirement of cutoff-aware temporal restriction [31, 32]. To bridge this gap, the ExAnte benchmark [ 1 ] was introduced to systematically evaluate LLMs’ ability to reason while strictly adhering to temporal cutoffs. Experimental results across multiple tasks reveal that existing LLMs frequently violate these temporal constraints, with no single prompting strategy—including Zero-shot, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) [ 9], and Self-Verification [ 10 ]—consistently eliminating temporal leakage [ 1]. Beyond general failure, empirical observations indicate specific vulnerabilities: (1) Cutoff Explicitness Effect : Models exhibit a significant sensitivity to whether a cutoff is phrased through implicit or explicit templates, suggesting a reliance on surface-level linguistic cues rather than robust time-conditioned validity reasoning. (2) Constraint Placement Sensitivity : A distinct “prefix advantage” is observed, where temporal constraints at the beginning of a prompt yield more effective control than those at the end—a phenomenon that contradicts the intuition that proximity to the output should enhance constraint adherence. These insights suggest that failures in ex-ante reasoning are fundamentally temporal verification failures, indicating a lack of genuine temporal verification capability in current models. Since surface-level prompting proves insufficient to resolve these internal reasoning gaps, research has begun to explore more intrusive forms of knowledge control [33–35]. 13 A.2 LLM Unlearning 

As prompting strategies fail to provide a robust solution for temporal adherence, machine unlearn-ing has emerged as a potential paradigm to suppress or remove specific knowledge within model weights [ 36 , 37 ]. Subsequent research has proposed various frameworks, such as gradient-ascent fine-tuning [ 38 ], preference-based unlearning [ 39 ], and in-context unlearning [ 33 ], to selectively erase the influence of specific data points [40]. However, ex-ante reasoning presents a challenge fundamentally distinct from traditional unlearning. While unlearning focuses on the permanent removal of data for privacy or safety, ex-ante inference requires a dynamic, context-dependent suppression of knowledge based on an arbitrary temporal cutoff [ 1]. A critical obstacle is the “memorization-leakage” paradox: evidence suggests that stronger recall and memorization of post-cutoff events are often positively correlated with higher leakage rates, as models struggle to decouple internalized memories from temporal constraints [ 1, 41 ]. Consequently, ex-ante reasoning demands temporary knowledge suppression and active admissibility judgment—capabilities that traditional unlearning methods, which target permanent erasure, are not designed to achieve [1]. 

A.3 Critique Learning 

To overcome the limitations of standard prompting and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) [ 13 , 16 , 15 , 14 ], recent research has turned toward self-correction and critique learning [ 42 – 44 ]. While self-correction aims to refine outputs, studies show that LLMs typically struggle to identify complex flaws without specialized training [ 45 ]. Critique Fine-Tuning (CFT) was proposed to shift the training paradigm from simple imitation to active verification, training models to critique noisy responses and identify errors [11, 46]. In the context of ex-ante reasoning, standard SFT is inadequate because its objective is static imitation; yet, in ex-ante scenarios, the validity of a response is strictly conditioned on the specified temporal cutoff. This necessitates an extension of the critique paradigm to the temporal domain. By training models to evaluate the temporal admissibility of responses and identify specific leakage violations, it is possible to move beyond superficial prompt adherence. Such an approach aims to instill an intrinsic cutoff-aware verification capability, enabling models to judge whether a response remains valid under shifting temporal constraints [17, 47–50]. 

## B Validity Evaluation Metric 

Another core dimension of evaluating ex-ante reasoning is guarding against degenerate low-leakage strategies [ 1]. For instance, a naive model could trivially achieve zero leakage by refusing to answer (e.g., “I cannot provide the related information” ) or by generating vacuous, low-quality text, which while technically adhering to the cutoff, offers no functional utility. To distinguish genuine reasoning from mere abstention, a quality guardrail Q(ˆ y, y ∗) is introduced, where Q is a task-specific similarity or factual precision function evaluated against the ideal pre-cutoff response [ 1]. The instantiation of Q is task-dependent to reflect diverse output structures [ 1]. For numerical single-event tasks, the quality function can be instantiated with task-specific regression metrics such as negative mean absolute error [ 1 ]. For QA, we do not evaluate validity separately, since the task asks for the exact date of an unexpected future event, and providing the correct date is itself temporal leakage. Thus, there is no meaningful pre-cutoff target answer for quality evaluation; rewarding exact correctness would reward leakage, while penalizing uncertainty or abstention would be inappropriate. We therefore evaluate QA using leakage rate only. In the context of multi-event generation, Q is operationalized as factual precision, representing the proportion of generated atomic claims that are verifiable within the Rprev knowledge space [ 1, 51 ]. Formally, a response is valid only if it satisfies both the temporal boundary and a minimum utility threshold γ [1]: Valid (x) = 1[L(x) = 0] ∧ 1[Q(ˆ y, y ∗) ≥ γ]. (10) 14 C Discussion 

C.1 Relation to Supervised Fine-Tuning 

Formally, standard SFT [16, 15, 14, 13] optimizes the following objective: 

min  

> θ

E(x,t c,y ∗) − log p(y∗ | x, t c; θ),

where y∗ denotes the oracle response under cutoff tc. This objective treats (x, t c) as the input context and y∗ as the target output, thereby learning a direct mapping from prompt to answer. However, because y∗ is constructed to be temporally admissible by design, SFT only exposes the model to positive answers and provides no supervision on what future leakage looks like or why it violates the cutoff. As a result, it remains an answer-generation objective rather than a temporal verification objective. TCFT directly addresses this limitation. Instead of training only on admissible target answers, TCFT pairs diverse candidate responses, including both admissible and leaked ones, with explicit temporal judgments. The model therefore learns to evaluate a response against the cutoff, distinguishing temporally valid content from post-cutoff leakage, rather than merely memorizing a mapping from context to answer. 

C.2 Relation to Conventional Critique Fine-Tuning 

Template CFT [ 11 ] has shown promise in teaching models to identify errors in problem-solving chains and self-correct. Its key contribution is to shift learning from output imitation to error identification. TCFT extends this inversion to the temporal setting, where the object of critique is not only whether a response is factually correct, but whether it is admissible under a given cutoff. In standard CFT, the critique of a reasoning error is largely context-independent: whether a solution step is flawed depends on the problem and the step itself. In temporal critique, however, admissibility is cutoff-dependent. For example, the statement “Elon Musk acquired Twitter” is factually correct, but temporally inadmissible if the cutoff precedes 2022. Likewise, the same response y may be valid when tc = 2023 but invalid when tc = 2020 . Thus, the critique function must be conditioned on the cutoff: 

c(tc) = Cθ (x, t c, y )̸ = Cθ (x, y ). (11) This makes the learned critique capability inherently temporally parameterized. Without conditioning on tc, a CFT-style critique would assign the same judgment across different cutoffs, failing to capture the temporal validity relation central to ex-ante reasoning. 

## D Experimental Setups 

D.1 Datasets 

We build our experiments on the ExAnte benchmark [ 1 ], focusing on two representative ex-ante inference tasks: single-event QA and Wikipedia-style generation. These two tasks cover different levels of response complexity: QA requires the model to decide whether it can answer a future-event question under a given cutoff, while Wiki requires the model to generate multiple factual claims that must all remain valid before the cutoff. To improve generalization, we augment both datasets along several dimensions. Specifically, we vary whether constraints are expressed implicitly or explicitly, diversify constraint positions in the prompt, and introduce different cutoff-date settings. We further mix multiple prompt templates during data construction, so the model is exposed to semantically similar tasks with varied surface forms rather than overfitting to a single prompt pattern. The QA dataset contains 9,072 examples in total, split into 8:1:1 for train/validation/test. The Wiki dataset contains 5,268 examples in total, split into 8:1:1 for train/validation/test. 

QA. The QA task evaluates single-event temporal leakage. Each example contains an event query, the true event date, and a cutoff date preceding the event. Following our main experiments, we evaluate three cutoff gaps: 1 day, 14 days, and 30 days before the target event date. A response is counted as leaked if the model reveals the correct post-cutoff event information under the specified cutoff. Wiki. The Wiki task evaluates multi-event generation under temporal cutoffs. During evaluation, generated responses are decomposed into atomic claims, and each claim is checked for whether it is supported before the cutoff or leaks post-cutoff information. 

D.2 Evaluation Metrics 

We use leakage rate as the primary metric for both QA and Wiki. Lower leakage indicates stronger cutoff adherence. 

QA leakage. For QA, leakage is measured under 1-day, 14-day, and 30-day cutoff gaps. A response is considered leaked if it provides the correct post-cutoff event information despite being instructed to answer under the cutoff. We report leakage rates for each cutoff gap and their average. 

Wiki leakage and accuracy. For Wiki, we report leakage rate and claim accuracy. Leakage rate measures the proportion of generated claims that rely on post-cutoff information. Claim accuracy measures the proportion of generated claims that are factually supported by pre-cutoff information. This metric is included to prevent degenerate low-leakage strategies, such as refusing to answer or producing overly vague content. Detailed definitions of leakage rate and claim accuracy are provided in Table 2. 

D.3 Baselines 

We compare TCFT with proprietary LLMs, prompt-level interventions, and a parameter-level fine-tuning baseline. 

Proprietary LLMs. We evaluate GPT-5.4 [ 24 ], Claude-Opus-4.6 [ 52 ], and Gemini-3.1-Pro-Preview [ 53 ] under the same evaluation protocol. These models serve as strong proprietary references for assessing whether general model capability alone leads to reliable cutoff adherence. 

Zero-shot. The model is directly prompted with the task query and the cutoff constraint, without additional demonstrations or verification steps. 

Few-shot [ 54 ]. The model is provided with demonstration examples that illustrate how to answer under temporal cutoffs. The demonstrations are designed to encourage the model to avoid post-cutoff information while preserving useful pre-cutoff content. 

Chain-of-thought [ 9]. The model is prompted to reason step by step before answering. This baseline tests whether eliciting intermediate reasoning improves temporal cutoff adherence. 

Self-verification [ 55 ]. The model first generates an initial response and then performs an additional verification step to check whether the response contains post-cutoff information. If leakage is detected, the model is instructed to revise the response. 16 SFT. We train a supervised fine-tuning baseline on temporally admissible target answers generated by GPT-5.4 [ 24 ] for each query under the corresponding cutoff. This baseline uses the same backbone models and augmented prompt formats as TCFT, but optimizes final-answer imitation rather than temporal critique [ 15 , 14 , 13 ]. It is included to test whether answer-level supervision alone is sufficient for learning ex-ante reasoning.

D.5 Implementation Details 

We use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct [ 19 ] as the open-weight backbones. We exclude smaller models because their limited memorization of post-cutoff facts makes leakage evaluation less informative. All models are evaluated on the same QA and Wiki test sets. 

Temporal critique generation. For TCFT, we construct critique supervision using a teacher model (i.e., GPT-5.4 [ 24 ]). Given a query, cutoff, and candidate response, the teacher generates a temporal-validity rationale and a binary verdict. The rationale explains which parts of the response are valid or invalid under the cutoff, while the verdict summarizes whether the response contains post-cutoff leakage. 

Training. TCFT is trained using standard supervised fine-tuning over critique-conclusion sequences. The input consists of the query, cutoff, and candidate response, while the target output consists of the temporal rationale and verdict. We optimize the model with the standard next-token prediction objective. For fair comparison, SFT uses the same backbone and prompt augmentation but is trained to imitate final target answers rather than critiques. 

Inference. At inference time, TCFT directly generates the final response from the query and cutoff. It does not require explicit critique generation or answer repair during evaluation. The learned temporal verification capability is expected to guide generation away from post-cutoff information. 

Hyperparameters. Unless otherwise stated, we use the same training configuration for SFT and TCFT. The learning rate is set to 1e-5 , warmup ratio to 0.03 . E.1 Temporal Critique Prompt for QA System Prompt 

You are an expert critic for ex-ante temporal reasoning. You will receive an event, its true date, a cutoff date, the original prompt, and a model answer. Your job is to critique the answer under the cutoff constraint. 

Rules: 

• If the true event date is later than the cutoff date, then the event had not happened yet at the cutoff. Any answer that gives a specific future date or otherwise relies on post-cutoff knowledge is wrong. • If the true event date is on or before the cutoff date, then the date was already knowable at the cutoff. In this case, the answer is right only if it gives the correct date in the required format. If it is wrong, explain the factual mistake. In this case, it is allowed to mention the correct date in the critique. • Focus first on temporal validity, then on factual correctness, then on format. • Keep the critique concise, natural, and useful for training. 

18 • End with exactly one of: “Conclusion: right” or “Conclusion: wrong”.

E.2 Temporal Critique Prompt for Wiki System Prompt 

You are an expert critic for ex-ante temporal reasoning on Wikipedia-style factual claims. You will receive a topic, a cutoff date, the original prompt, and a model answer containing several atomic claims. Your job is to critique whether the claims are valid under the cutoff constraint. 

Rules: 

• Evaluate each claim according to what could reasonably be known on or before the cutoff date. • A claim is wrong if it relies on information from after the cutoff date. • For future events, distinguish scheduled or planned information from completed-event information: 

– It is acceptable to say that an event was scheduled, planned, announced, selected, or expected before the cutoff if that was knowable then. 

– It is not acceptable to state a future event as already having happened, completed, concluded, won, opened, released, died, etc., if that happened after the cutoff. • If any claim is temporally invalid, the overall answer is wrong. • If all claims are temporally valid and factually plausible under the cutoff, the overall answer is right. • Keep the critique concise, natural, and useful for training. • End with exactly one of: “Conclusion: right” or “Conclusion: wrong”.

Topic: {title} Cutoff date: {cutoff_date} Original prompt: {prompt} Model answer: {raw_response} Claims: {claims} Write a concise critique of the model answer under the cutoff date.
