# CLIPer: Tailoring Diverse User Preference via Classifier-Guided Inference-Time Personalization 

Jinyan Su, Jinpeng Zhou, Claire Cardie, Wen Sun 

Cornell University

Abstract 

Personalized LLMs can significantly enhance user experiences by tailoring responses to pref-erences such as helpfulness, conciseness, and humor. However, fine-tuning models to address all possible combinations of user preferences is computationally expensive and impractical. In this paper, we introduce CLIPer (Cl assifier-guided Inference-time Per sonalization), alightweight personalization approach that lever-ages a classifier model to steer LLM generation dynamically to different user preferences at in-ference time. Our method eliminates the need for extensive fine-tuning, inducing negligible additional computational overhead while en-abling more controllable and nuanced person-alization across single and multi-dimensional preferences. Comprehensive empirical analy-ses demonstrate the scalability and effective-ness of our approach in delivering personalized language generation. 

1 Introduction 

In recent years, LLMs have emerged as powerful tools, driving advancements across various appli-cations. Their potential for delivering personalized services to meet diverse user needs has spurred sig-nificant interest in developing personalized LLMs, with the ultimate goal of leveraging them as per-sonal assistants. While methods like Reinforce-ment Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) have aligned LLMs with general human prefer-ences, they fall short in addressing the vast diversity of individual user preferences. This generalization can negatively impact the user experience, particu-larly for minority groups, and limit the accessibility of LLMs. For instance, an LLM designed for chil-dren should be amicable and easy to understand, whereas one for IT professionals should employ technical and formal language. A practical way to approach this is by clustering user preferences along various dimensions, such as helpfulness, con-ciseness, or humor. Users can then define their ideal LLM personality by choosing among these di-mensions, allowing the model to adapt dynamically to their needs. The simplest approach for personalization is through direct prompting, i.e., we include the user preference into the prompt. However, preference prompting can struggle especially when preference cannot be explicitly articulated in natural language, but can only be inferred from user preference data. In such cases, the most straightforward approach is to fine-tune a model on data reflecting each user’s preferences. However, fine-tuning a model for each preference type already demands significant com-putational and memory resources. The challenge intensifies when user preferences span multiple di-mensions, as the number of possible combinations grows exponentially. However, training d separate models for each preference dimension can be impractical for for large LLMs. To make personalization more fea-sible, we need a lightweight personalization ap-proach without fine-tuning LLM for every prefer-ence dimension. In this paper, we propose CLIPer ,a Cl assifier-guided Inference-time Per sonalization approach that eliminates the need for training and maintaining multiple fine-tuned models during both training and inference. Our classifier is trained to predict a distribution over all preference dimen-sions given the past text. The distribution models how likely the given text can be classified as each preference dimension (e.g., what is the probability that the given text can be classified as funny). Our classifier predicts at the token level, and its output can be used to dynamically adjust the base model’s logits at token level during the inference time. Our method incurs negligible computational overhead compared to fine-tuning multiple large LLMs while effectively steers the LLM toward personalized be-haviors. To summarize, our contributions are: (1) We propose a Cl assifier-guided Inference-time 

Per sonalization approach ( CLIPer ) that trains a small classifier which dynamically adjusts the log-its of the LLM during inference time, enabling personalization without fine-tuning the LLM. This approach maintains a constant training and infer-ence cost with respect to preference dimensions, making it both scalable and practical for real-world applications. (2) Our method supports both single-dimension and multi-dimension preference settings. (3) We introduce a novel classifier model architec-ture that reduces computational overhead by jointly predicting the conditional distribution over the pref-erence dimensions conditioned on past tokens (pre-fix) at the same time. This architecture enables our approach to perform only one forward pass on both the large LLM and the small classifier during the inference time. (4) Through comprehensive empirical analyses, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in achieving personalized and controllable text gener-ation without the need for extensive fine-tuning or additional computational burden.

Personalized LLM. P-RLHF (Li et al., 2024) introduces a lightweight user model to capture in-dividual preferences and jointly learns both the user model and the personalized LLM from human feedback. Poddar et al. infer a user-specific la-tent representation, using it to condition both the reward model and policy—without requiring ad-ditional user-specific data. (2024) require training an expert model for each dimension, re-sulting in d additional training computations for 

d dimensions. Furthermore, Zhou et al. (2024) employs d expert models simultaneously during in-ference, leading to O(d) computational complexity for inference, as summarized in Table 1.

3.2 Personalized Text Generation 

Given a user preference c and prompt x (such as shown in Figure 1), the text generation from per-sonalized model can be formulated as a conditional text probability of generation y given x and c:

p(y|x, c ) = Π ni=1 p(yi|y<i ; x, c ), (1) where yi denotes the i-th token in the generated text and y<i represents the tokens generated up to step i − 1.While training a separate model for each possi-ble preference c would theoretically enable perfect personalization, it is computationally infeasible due to the exponential growth of required models as the number of preference dimensions d increases. In-stead, we focus on inference-time personalization, where a general base model p(y|x) is dynamically adjusted to approximate the personalized model 

p(y|x, c ).To achieve this, we leverage Bayesian infer-ence by introducing an auxiliary classifier model 

p(c|y, x ) to steer the base model: 

p(y|x, c ) ∝ p(y|x) · p(c|y, x )α, (2) where α is a hyperparameter controlling the influ-ence of the classifier model. When α = 0 , the 3formulation reduces to the base model without per-sonalization. In the case of autoregressive language models, where p(y|x, c ) = Π ni=1 p(yi|y<i ; x, c ), the condi-tional probability for each token is: 

p(yi|y<i ; x, c ) ∝ p(yi|y<i ; x) · p(c|y≤i, x )α. (3) Thus, the overall personalized text generation prob-ability becomes: 

p(y|x, c ) ∝ Πni=1 p(yi|y<i ; x)p(c|y≤i, x )α

Here, p(c|yi, y <i , x ) represents the likelihood of preference c given prompt x and the partial genera-tion y≤i, which are given by the classifier model. 

Multiple Preferences It is intuitive to general-ize the above formulation to multiple preferences, i.e., c = {c1, · · · , c d}, and |c| = k. Using the independence assumption, we have

where α = ( α1, · · · , α d) are hyperparameters con-trolling the influence of the each preference. In inference time, to sample the next token, we need to construct the distribution over the next to-ken (i.e., p(y|y<i ; x, c ) for all y ∈ V ). This would require us to evaluate the classifier model for all 

y ∈ V , i.e., we need to compute p(c|y<i , y, x ) for all y ∈ V . Naively implementing this would re-quire us to perform |V| forward passes on the clas-sifier model (Sitdikov et al., 2022). This can signif-icantly slow down the inference procedure. 

3.3 Efficient classifier Modeling 

To address the above efficiency challenge, we train a classifier model capable of producing classifi-cation probabilities for all tokens in the vocabu-lary at each step. Specifically, during the gen-eration, the classifier model takes the prompt x

and partial generation y<i as inputs, and outputs a matrix M ∈ R|V|× d where Mv,c j models the probability of having label cj given prompt x,the partial generation (y<i ), and the next token 

v ∈ V , i.e., p(cj |yi = v, y <i , x ). The k-th row of M corresponds to the preference probabilities 

p(c|y<i , v k, x ), where vk ∈ V is the k-th token in the vocabulary. The rows are normalized such that the probabilities sum to 1. This design allows the classifier model to efficiently compute preference probabilities for all possible tokens in a single step, avoiding the need for performing multiple forward passes of the model. 3.4 Classifier Model Guided Generation. 

After training the classifier model, we utilize it to achieve personalized generation as described in Eq. (2). At each generation step i, the base model and the classifier model both take the prompt x and the current partial generation y<i as input. Then, pro-duces the unconditional probabilities p(v|x, y <i )

for all tokens v ∈ V , where V is the vocabulary. And the classifier model generates probabilities of user preference c, i.e., p(c|x, y <i , v ), for all v ∈ V .The next token yi is sampled from the personal-ized probability distribution p(v|x, y <i , c ), which combines the outputs of the base model and the classifier model through Eq.(3) (Or Eq.(4) for mul-tiple preferences). 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Detail Dataset for Classifier Model Training Follow-ing prior work (Zhou et al., 2024; Jang et al., 2023), we consider six preference dimensions across three categories: audience, content density, and tone (see Table 2). Consistent with previous studies (Jang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024), we use GPT4-Alpaca (Peng et al., 2023) as training data and generate n = 16 samples per dimension using Tulu-7B as the base model, with prompts from (Jang et al., 2023) (summarized in Table 2). The top-1 ranked sample, based on the reward model from (Jang et al., 2023), is selected as training data for each dimension. We then train a classifier us-ing LLaMA-160M on this collected data. Due to the scarcity of high-quality validation and test datasets, we tune hyperparameters α using data randomly sampled from Alpaca-Eval. For evalua-tion, we follow prior works (Jang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) and use the Koala and UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2024) datasets.

The details description of these dimensions can be found in Table 2. 

Baselines We mainly compare our method to 3 baselines: Direct Prompting , which directly prompt the LLM to generate the text according to user pref-erences. Noting from prior works (Jang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024), Direct prompting is a rather strong baseline since tulu-7b is well instructed to generate personalized texts based on instructions alone. Evaluation Metrics Since our classifier model is trained on dataset created with reward models, we use the reward model as a judge for win-rate. Ad-ditionally, we employ GPT-4o-mini as an alterna-tive judge to simulate an out-of-domain preference scenario—where preferences are learned from one user group (i.e., the reward model) but evaluated on another (i.e., GPT-4o-mini). This further under-scores the importance of personalizing LLMs for different user groups. We used the same prompts from (Jang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) for GPT-4o-mini when using it as a judge. 

Hyperparameter The primary hyperparameter we tune is α, which controls the influence of the classifier model in modifying the base model’s log-its. A lower α results in less personalization, al-lowing the base model’s original responses to dom-inate, while a higher α enforces stronger personal-ization. In practice, α should be chosen to balance the trade-off between preserving the base model’s original responses and incorporating personalized adjustments. For CLIPer, α is a tuple, depending on total number of dimensions in the combination c.For single dimension experiments, α is a scalar, so we use two randomly sampled subset of alpaca eval dataset, each of 100 data samples, and conduct pa-rameter search over α = {0.05 , 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.8}.We end up having α = 0 .5 to be the best parameter for one subset and α = 0 .8 for another. We end up choosing α = 0 .8 as the hyper-parameter for our main experiments by taking the average of the win rate of these two dataset, and select the hyper-parameter based on this averaged win rate. Note that, since the win rate is pair-wise, to simplify the hyper-parameter selection, we only compute the win-rate against direct-prompting, and use this win rate to choose hyper-parameter. Also, we use the same hyper-parameter for all the experiments, thus, our hyper-parameter selection is rather coarse. Single preference Dimension For single pref-erence dimension experiments, we only have 1 hyper-parameter to tune. In order to show the ro-bustness of our method, we present the average performance over these 6 dimensions in Table 3 without tuning α for different dimensions. Even without carefully tuning α, we find that CLIPer still achieves a rather high average win rate com-pare to other baseline. Table 3 presents the average win rates (across six single preference dimensions) from both reward model and gpt-4o-mini evalua-tions, demonstrating the effectiveness of our pro-posed approach. Correlation Analysis An important assumption for our approach is that the preference dimensions we are combining should be independent since dur-ing inference the classifier output probability is multiplied across preference dimensions. To em-pirically verify this, in Figure 6, we analyze the correlation of different dimensions where we gen-erate text that is intentionally steered toward that dimension through direct prompting. For each text, we use the reward model to label each dimension, and compute the Pearson correlation coefficient over the reward model scores among all preference dimension pairs. We observe that for every prefer-ence and its opposite, the correlation is always very strongly negative. This is expected and not an issue since we never group opposite preferences in to c.Another observation is that when the text is gen-erated based on a particular preference, it usually has low correlation with other preferences (except itself and the opposite preference), suggesting pref-erence dimensions that we are combining are fairly independent from each other, which verifies our independence assumption.

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduced CLIPer, a classifier-guided inference-time personalization approach that enables dynamic adaptation of LLMs to di-verse user preferences without extensive fine-tuning. By leveraging a lightweight classifier model, CLIPer steers generation in real time, offer-ing a scalable and efficient alternative to traditional fine-tuning approaches. Our experimental results demonstrate the potential of CLIPer as a practical and adaptable solution for real-world LLM person-alization. 8Limitations 

As our work focuses on building a scalable and ef-ficient personalization framework—an intrinsically challenging task—it naturally introduces certain limitations. First, our classifier model is trained on synthetic data derived from reward model rank-ings, rather than human-annotated labels. While this design choice enables scalable training with-out costly human supervision, it may also intro-duce alignment biases from the reward model. However, our empirical evaluations across both in-domain (reward model) and out-of-domain (GPT-4o-mini) judges help mitigate this concern. Sec-ond, our method assumes conditional independence between preference dimensions to avoid exponen-tial scaling. While this assumption holds empir-ically for the dimensions we evaluate (as shown in our correlation analysis), future work could ex-plore modeling interdependencies explicitly to sup-port more nuanced personalization. Third, due to resource constraints, we evaluate CLIPer on a fixed set of six well-defined preference dimensions. This allows for controlled comparisons and bench-marking, but does not fully capture the range of real-world user preferences—especially those that are implicit or context-specific. Nonetheless, the framework is general and can easily accommodate new dimensions as data becomes available. Finally, we apply a shared set of hyperparameters across all combinations of preferences to ensure fairness and reduce tuning overhead. While this likely un-derstates the upper bound of our method’s perfor-mance, it demonstrates robustness and practicality for real-world use without extensive hyperparame-ter tuning.

Preference Dimension Output 

P1A The TCP/IP model is like a cake! The cake is like the OSI model, which is a way to explain how computers communicate with each other. The OSI model has seven layers: 1. Physical Layer 2. Data Link Layer 3. Network Layer 4. Transport Layer 5. Session Layer 6. Presentation Layer 7. Application Layer. The Physical Layer is like the cake’s crust. It’s the part that holds everything together and makes sure the cake is safe to eat. The Data Link Layer is like the frosting on the cake. It’s the part that makes the cake look pretty and delicious. The Network Layer is like the cake’s middle layer. It’s the part that helps the cake stay together and move around. The Transport Layer is like the cake’s topping. It’s the part that makes the cake taste good and enjoyable to eat. The Session Layer is like the candles on the cake. It’s the part that helps the cake celebrate special occasions. The Presentation Layer is like the cake’s decorations. It’s the part that makes the cake look even more special and fun to eat. The Application Layer is like the cake’s party. It’s the part that helps the cake have a good time and make everyone happy. Just like how the cake has different layers that work together to make it delicious, the TCP/IP model has different layers that work together to make computers communicate with each other. P1B The TCP/IP model is a conceptual framework that describes the structure and function of the Internet. It consists of four layers: the application layer, the transport layer, the internet layer, and the link layer. At the application layer, one protocol is the Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP). HTTP is a protocol that is used for transmitting information between a client and a server. It is used for tasks such as retrieving web pages, sending and receiving email, and streaming media. At the transport layer, one protocol is the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP). TCP is a protocol that is used for reliable data transmission. It ensures that data is sent in the proper order and that it is received without errors. At the internet layer, one protocol is the Internet Protocol (IP). IP is a protocol that is used for routing data packets between networks. It is responsible for determining the best path for data to take as it travels from its source to its destination.
